What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Do not be afraid of previous Q–guide reviews regarding this class. It is not as bad as it seems. It is indeed very time–consuming, but I found much of the work to be spent on research and/or documentation, and working with a team certainly helps a lot in terms of splitting up the workload.

If you want to go into the medical device industry, you should definitely take this class. This class is one of the closest things at Harvard that will give you exposure to what it's like to work as an engineer––– from constant feedback, collaboration, iterative prototyping, design validation/testing, working with clients, presentations to documentation. The experience and skills you gain in this class will look great to have on your resume or grad school applications. What you gain out of this class makes all the time and sacrifices spent worth it.

The professors have high expectations of you, and you will sometimes have to scrap ideas and projects you were working on and start over.

If you have a bad team, communicate with your teammates your expectations maturely and take some measures to improve the team dynamics immediately. You will be spending most of your semester with them.
Very rewarding! This class is gonna be your whole life for the semester (not an exaggeration). The nature of this course is to get through the entire design cycle of a device (a process that takes years) in 12–13 weeks so yes the rigor of the course is a lot. However, HOWEVER, if you are interested of device design to treat medical conditions. make the space in your semester to take it! You work directly with clinicians, you get prototyping and verification testing experience and at the end you have a team paper and presentation that can actually be entered into research competitions. This class does so much for you academically and will look good on your application when you apply to grad school, med school etc. My team had great support from our course staff which made the semester a lot more bearable but I have heard experiences from other teams who did not have course staff that were not as supportive and how that impacted their team so that is another thing to consider. Do not take this course unless you genuinely really want to and are ready to commit your semester to this project.
Good exposure to medical device design, but it does take a lot of work, especially at the end. Bring some physical and electronics prototyping experience and you’ll be good, the difficulty lies in narrowing your design at the beginning with your team and managing your time at the end.
If you are Mech E and debating between this class and ES96, take ES96. Listen to the course evaluations when they say this class is challenging. I wanted to take it for the hands–on aspect, and you definitely get a lot of experience in building and prototyping. However, this class does expect a lot from you, especially if you are an undergrad student, and you will find yourself spending countless hours and nights in the SEC or analyzing your work. The feedback can be disheartening at times, but if you get a good TF then it makes all the difference in the class (very very grateful for our TF). Am I happy with the learning experience I got from this class? Yes. Was it worth the countless late nights, little sleep, and confusion? Probably not.
This course is amazing; definitely take it if you have the opportunity and are interested in design and/or health technology! This course introduces you to real world problems and lets you communicate with stakeholders in the field. This course enables you to work in a small group of 4–5 students to actually design and iterate on a physical solution. It demands a lot of work, but this can be balanced by effective teamwork and good communication. Also, extensive prototyping at the beginning of the semester can avoid some of the end–of–semester crunches that you've likely heard about. There will inevitably be a lot of last minute refinement up to the final presentation and report; nevertheless, this extensive time commitment feels so worthwhile since it's devoted to a meaningful project. Some fabrication, electronics, and/or coding experience is vital for this class; this includes skills such as CAD, 3D printing, ImageJ, circuit design, Arduino, Python, etc. Visualization skills are not necessary but very helpful (platforms such as Adobe Illustrator, etc.). Overall, this was one of the best classes I've taken during my time here!
Great class. Student led.
