TL;DR: Take it if you’re considering mechE, but know that it’ll take up a lot of your life. Go to office hours, stay on top of labs, and hope you get a good team, and you’ll be okay.

Although I definitely don’t regret taking ES51 and I’m more sure of my decision to concentrate in mechE, I think that the course is not very well organized. ES51 is basically three classes rolled into one–––lectures, psets, and labs all feel very separate. It’s definitely time–consuming, and for better or worse, it goes more for breadth over depth. Your experience will also probably be heavily dependent on your (randomly assigned) lab group.
Personally, I thought the lectures were not the most effective. I felt like they tended to move really quickly without any explanation of why each step was being taken, so I would get lost within the first 15 minutes, find it too difficult to catch up, and then check out for the next hour. Office hours were a necessity for completing the psets; I honestly felt like I learned more in office hours than lectures (Anna and Katelyn in particular are really helpful, and Eric helps a lot with SolidWorks!). I would still recommend attending lectures, since without it I would have been even more lost than I already was. Lab hours are not super flexible leading up to the competition (usually the machine shop closes around 3pm), making it difficult to manage time. In particular, CAD tends to be the limiting factor (it’s hard to machine parts without it), so definitely try and stay on top of that as much as possible. Regardless, almost everyone ends up working on the robot extensively in the last couple of weeks. Anyways, as long as you complete your work, you shouldn’t have to worry about your grade.
I think it is important for the student to understand that this class is very doable and you can perform well in it but there will be periods of time in which the class picks up and gives you an extreme amount of work which will be stressful.
This class is quite a bit of work, so be ready. Seymur's is not a great lecturer but a very sweet person and is easy to go to with questions. In the end, you'll come out of this class with plenty of complaints, lots of opinions about how it could be better, a conviction that you should've started your robot earlier, and quite a bit of knowledge learned. I suspect it's like the meche concentration as a whole: a bit of a mess but mostly lovable.

Start working on the robot EARLY and expect to spend a lot of time on it towards the end. Try your best to enjoy the late nights with everyone else working on the robot.

This is a class that I would discourage most people from taking, unless you are taking it for a degree requirement. While it gives students a broad overview of mechanical engineering topics, I think it would be more beneficial to take other classes over this. The content itself is not too difficult, but the lectures are poorly explained and difficult to follow. Most students end up having to spend time outside of class teaching themselves the material, since it is not clearly taught in class. Usually, though, you can learn it in a fraction of the time that it takes for it to be explained in lecture. Additionally, there are participation activities in class, so your grade will be directly impacted by how many classes you attend. Aside from the lecture component of the class, the logistical side of the class is lacking. It is an extremely unorganized class. Most of the expectations for students are not outlined in the syllabus, and the Canvas page is extremely confusing. Instructors will also each say different things to different people (or the same person on different occasions) and have different expectations for everyone. There is not a centralized location to look for information––you will often have to check through many Google Drive folders, various pages on Canvas, and the class Slack in order to get information you were looking for. The lab portion is not connected at all to the content learned in lecture, and it feels as though it is an entirely separate class. Students also often spend a significant portion of their lab hours doing nothing since the course and lab staff is either unprepared to start lab or there is not enough staff and machines to be working at the same time. Overall, unless you are truly passionate about mechanical engineering, I would not recommend students to take this class.

ALL ENGINEERS NEED TO TAKE THIS CLASS, no matter what specific engineering discipline you are in! The concepts and SolidWorks skills you learn in ES51 are fundamental to every engineering discipline. The project, SolidWorks, and machining experience you gain from this class is very useful to have on your resume, and it helped me to secure an engineering internship! Even if you don't plan to use those directly after college, it's great to have exposure to MechE skills and concepts frin ES51, so that you at least have the CHOICE of deciding between a role that is more MechE–focused or an alternative career path. You also have the opportunity to get practice materials and take the SolidWorks certification exam at the end of the course, which is great!

Your team is very important to your success in this class. If you have any conflicts, communicate and handle it maturely early in advance!
Learn some solid works prior to save time on beginning psets.
ES 51 is an easy but incredibly boring class. Any familiarity with engineering in high school means that this course will be a breeze and students are often comfortably able to finish with an A or A–. Lectures are 1 hour and 15 minutes of learning about formulas and are incredibly monotonous and boring. Other than going for participation credits, the material can be easily gleaned from the in depth lecture notes and often times cramming the night before exams is a perfect strategy to do well because the content in this course is so shallow. For exams (2 in the class), your performance is basically tied to how in depth of a formula sheet you make.

Lab is a good introduction to the fundamentals of machining and manufacturing and I definitely learned how to do things I'd never done before. The final project is really not as big of a deal as it may seem. Usually if you come up with a creative idea, get an at least partially functioning robot by the competition date, and put effort into the supplementary materials you'll get the full points.
If you are reading this, you likely have to take this course as a requirement. I am extremely thankful that it is over as it has been the most draining class. This will probably put you off engineering as a concentration, but I think dont let it. It is not representative of the whole concentration. All you have to do is get through it.

To give some more practical information, there is one pset a week, three lectures and a 3hr lab. The lectures are entirely pointless and you dont learn a single thing from them. Seymur is evidently very knowledgeable but does not know how to convey this knowledge to a class. This then makes the psets time consuming and difficult. You will definitely make use of office hours, as long as the CA's dont cancel them which happened fairly often. The 3 hr lab depends completely on your lab group, which is only determined by which time you have your lab. Therefore make extra extra sure that those people are ones you get on with and will actually do some work, otherwise it is going to be a time consuming and emotionally frustrating semester in the class. You end up spending every day in the sec with these people frantically trying to make a functional robot. So try to pick good people and it would theoretically be fun.

The only good thing I have got out it are some very lovely friends in the class, the ability to use solidworks and hopefully a decent grade. While everything Seymur says goes over our heads, he also wants people to get good grades in the class, and so you will probably be fine in terms of grading!

Good luck and I hope you have a better time than I did.
