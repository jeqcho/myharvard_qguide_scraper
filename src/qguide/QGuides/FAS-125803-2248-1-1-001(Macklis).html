Neuro180 overlaps with neuro80 a bit so I wouldn't recommend taking both at the same time. Neuro180 encourages one to think about a variety of solutions to spinal cord injury and the drawbacks of those solutions. The end project was also super interesting and I got a lot of feedback.
Take this class! This course is a clear example of one that teaches you more than just the material, but ways to think about the material going forward. As regeneration and regrowth is such a new field, it feels essential to not only master previous literature but also ways to explore and expand going forward. The final project is a little bit daunting and expectations aren't very clear from the beginning, but the staff is receptive to feedback. Prof. Macklis can be a bit sassy and self–referential, but he means well and has a vast amount of expertise in research and medical fields. Everything is super interesting, just take good notes!
I went in wantign to like this course and it really did work for the first few weeks, but then it collapsed on itself. It has an identity problem where it doesn't know what it wants to do and how to be taught.
Great class! This class is incredibly rewarding! It’s designed to teach you how to think critically like a scientist, so expect to dive deep into complex ideas and really engage with the material. The problem sets and exams will push you to think creatively and apply what you’ve learned to real–world scenarios. The patient perspectives—through videos and guest speakers—will remind you why the work matters and keep you motivated. The teaching staff is kind, supportive, and creates a safe environment where you’ll feel respected and encouraged to ask questions. Overall, this course will not only improve your scientific skills but also change the way you approach problem–solving and research.
I would like to tell future students that this course requires immense creativity and engagement, but is beyond rewarding. This is a course that feels right on the cutting edge of amazing things happening out in the world, and is deeply integrated with where the field is at this current moment. This course also pushes you to understand research from many angles, from presentations to papers. Overall, this course is immensely helpful in immersing yourself in both the topical research landscape and in all the ways scientists have tried to understand repairing the CNS.
This course is a fascinating insight into how developmental and regenerative neuroscience has been pursued by scientists historically and some of the newest avenues that scientists are pursuing now. This course pushed me to learn and think in different ways as we were expected to discuss with classmates and collaborate in the final Blue Sky group project. That project was one of the most valuable parts of the class. Course staff (Mariale, Dustin) and Prof. Macklis were engaged and accessible, always available to answer questions.
Take this class! Professor Macklis and the TFs are extremely knowledgeable, approachable, willing to answer any questions regarding course content/assignments, and even their own research. The course was extremely useful to me as a neurobiology concentrator and future medical student, given that you spend the first half of the class learning how the central nervous system is developed. You develop your critical thinking skills to think like a scientist and apply the knowledge you learn in class to novel situations/problems and develop experiments to solve them. I will definitely take all I've learned in this class and apply it in my future studies/career!
Really interesting class. Super disorganized, but great if you are interested in learning small details to build up to a larger idea. Must be creative in this class.
In the most respectful way possible, this course was hands–down the worst upper–level course I have taken at Harvard. To begin, the syllabus seemingly implies structured lectures, but they were the complete opposite. Macklis unfortunately cannot go on for more than 5–10 minutes without going on an incoherent tangent that never pertains to the material at hand, despite stating he always knows how to get back on track. So much so, that we are often 3–4 lectures behind schedule on the syllabus. When points of confusion arise and you ask a question, he goes on another tangent or rudely cuts you off and never fully answers the question if at all. Everything taught in this class is covered by the developmental unit in NEURO80, so each lecture often feels like a waste of time. Coupled with Macklis' pompous and exceedingly arrogant attitude, this is easily one of the worst classes I have taken. Assignments, "midterms", and the final project are relatively straightforward but can feel repetitive and like busy work. Each lecture's readings are unreasonable and unnecessary (many of which are the professor's own work or collaborations), as Macklis rarely touches upon them or discusses their contents. The only "bright–side" of this course are: it being over and the sections where you review and learn about new experimental techniques or about revolutionary developmental biology with the TFs. Save yourself the time, frustration, and boredom and skip this course if it is still available.
Prof Macklis is a great lecturer! He's very engaging and you can take a lot of out of this course (if you're willing to put effort in). I really loved the content we went over each week. It is cool to imagine the future next steps of treating spinal cord injuries and other issues with the brain
