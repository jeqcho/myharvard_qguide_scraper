This was the greatest course I've taken, or will ever take, at Harvard. Jordan has set the new standard for teaching 210, par excellence. His deep understanding of general relativity, combined with his philosophical perspective, created a class where I did not merely learn how to compute a bunch of metrics, but to ponder the deep mystery of space and time.

Jordan declared his thesis statement at the beginning of the first lecture: Einstein's theory of general relativity gives the definitive explanation of the nature of space and time, a question which had provoked thought for millennia. He developed this thesis throughout the semester. It is my understanding that by the end of February we had covered what past renditions of 210 take the entire semester to do. Despite this, Jordan's lectures felt appropriately paced and incredibly insightful.I especially appreciate how the second half of the course was devoted to topics and applications in GR. Jordan's conjuring of the Ghosts of Universes Past, Present, and Yet–to–Come in the series on cosmology was particularly beautiful. The final lecture may have been the greatest of them all.

What I will remember most from this course are the parting words Jordan left for us at the end of the final class. He shared two quotes, one from Einstein and another from Wittgenstein, which were a fitting chiasmus to his opening remarks at the start of the semester. General relativity provides an outlet for wonder more than anything else. It is a theory of singular beauty, yet one which retains a quality bordering on the mystical a century after its inception. That space and time so desperately want to be equals yet are forever distinguished by a single minus sign really makes you wonder. The curvature of spacetime flies in the face of common intuition. Plato writes that what distinguishes knowledge from mere belief is the ability to peer into the realm of Forms and see the underlying, unchanging nature of things. This class teaches you knowledge of the real nature of space, time, matter, and indeed the universe itself.

I looked forward to this class every Monday and Wednesday, and hope to cross paths with Jordan again in the future, if our world lines intersect. The Physics Department has done a great job at hiring new faculty in the past few years. It would be a honest shame, and Harvard's loss, if they can't keep Jordan around.
This course will not only show you the beauty of physics, but it will guide you through discovering it for yourself. It will be hard, but it will definitely be worth it.
Be prepared for a difficult workload in the middle of the semester. Depending on your mathematical background, you may struggle with the differential geometry (I did), but the problem sets get easier once you've built the tools to actually do physics. Jordan is a very engaging lecturer, and overall I would recommend the class.
Be prepared for a lot of work, but it is a very beautiful and rewarding subject.
TAKE THIS CLASS!! This class is absolutely fascinating. Every few lectures you explore something new and interesting, from black holes to cosmology to gravitational waves, and Cotler makes every class engaging. I always looked forward to coming to this class.

The only thing to note is the problem sets for this class use a LOT of Mathematica at times, and there isn't a lot of support if you have issues with it (you can go to office hours for it but they're often too innundated with students to help with code issues). Because of this, if you don't have solid Mathematica experience going in you might have to accept getting docked 20% off your pset sometimes for issues with your code.

Still, I absolutely loved this class.
