ES125 is a hard and time consuming class however you do learn alot, and it is more intuitive than many other Mech E requirements. Start psets early and find a strong pset group.
It's remarkable how much you learn. The class is extremely accumulative – by the end of the semester, you will have a strong understanding of the foundations of mechanical systems. PSETs, for the most part, are reasonable. There are a couple that are too long, but that's only once or twice. Exams are also reasonable. Boris does a great job of teaching the concepts behind derivations in class. There will be times when nothing makes sense (me included, and I did well in the class!) but since the class is accumulative you are given time to grapple with the concepts. When I was studying for the final I was amazed by how much I understood. If you are interested in this class, take it.
Engineers rejoice, this is one of the good ones. ES125 was run extremely well, and was overall a super positive experience. The lectures were well structured, and all materials and recordings were posted online. The provided textbooks are great, and I highly recommend you read on each chapter before/after lecture. There will always be some confusing bits – I still went to office hours just about every week – but that's how it always is. The biggest strength this class has are its psets. They aren't easy, but they are super fair, well structured, and give you the chance to practice the same ideas at least a couple times so you feel like you've gotten the hang of them by the end. This class won't be easy, but I never once felt like the work I was putting in wasn't going anywhere.

ES125 is exactly how all of your engineering requirements should be – they will not all be this way, so just enjoy 125 while you have it. Conversely, if you take 125 early on and really don't like it, I would seriously recommend you reconsider engineering. It doesn't get much better than this, and it sure gets a lot worse.
This class can be a slog. Take it with at least 1 much more manageable class.
This is a difficult course. However, the content is quite interesting and applicable to mechanical systems across industries. Boris is a great instructor, but he can go through a lot of topics very quickly. Make sure to go to office hours, collaborate with your peers on PSETS, and take the practice tests before the exams. Overall, I will say that this is one of the most well structured engineering classes here considering the professor provides practice tests, lecture slides, and the TFs offer plenty of office hours. The same cannot be said of courses like ES120.
If you are taking this course for Mech E requirements, some tips to do well. 1) Start homework earlier if you can, as they may have confusing questions that you need to go to OH for. Homework would usually be about 7 questions, but one of the PSETs required 4 pages of math for just one problem....so make sure to at least glance at the questions beforehand. 2) Prep for the two midterms early: review lectures, go over old homework, understand important equations and systems and their conceptual importance, as you will be tested on that. 3) The end of the semester project is not that bad, mostly just participation. 4) Start studying for the final early as well! It is a good chunk of your grade, and it had a lot of conceptual questions (more so than the midterms) so you want to make sure you understand the "why" behind the math you're doing.
To plan your time around this class. Psets are super hard and the exams are also super difficult. Would recommend doing psets again as exam prep because the practice exams provided are not close to the caliber of the exam.
For the most part, I actually did enjoy this class and learned a lot, although that learning did come with a lot of time, effort, and frustration. The depth of this course is impressive, it was fascinating to see how deep the course went with each system and the various responses it had. Overall, the lectures were well–organized and made sense, and it was cool to see everything build from previous topics, but they also moved really fast. I wish that the parts of the lecture that went over how to solve the problems were slowed down, because once I got to the problem set, I would usually be stuck on how to solve the problem set. The problem sets are really challenging and take quite a bit of time, but usually, it's a similar method of problem–solving for the problems, so once you figure it out the first time, the rest of it becomes easier. My biggest complaint though was that the algebra in the homework is usually heinous, and it can be frustrating when the answer is so long and tedious, or if you make a mistake somewhere in the calculations.

Office hours can also be quite overstimulating because there are not that many of them, so I would recommend going to the earlier ones in the week (if Soy is a TF still, she's wonderful). A homework group for this class is also so extremely helpful, especially when checking answers. The egg drop was quite fine and a cool application of the topics in this course, and the exams were pretty fair, although they were short on time. I wouldn't be too scared going into this class, there can be quite a bit of suffering, but in the end, you gain a lot out of it and you will be able to get through it!
Overview:
This class is worth taking. If you understand mechanics well, I recommend taking it in your Sophomore year. But if you don't, I recommend taking it later. There is a little bit of linear algebra; if you understand eigenvectors and linear combinations, you'll be fine. Everything you learn is very cumulative, as the course builds upon everything. This is a heavy problem–set course with not the best TFs.

Problem Sets:
The problem sets are HARD, and the class's office hours are inconvenient. As someone who was a sophomore and did well in the class, I would recommend only going to office hours to check final answers. DON'T rely on peer solutions, as you do most of the learning through the problem sets. My grades on problem sets weren't the best, but I always was comfortably above the average on exams (I wouldn't describe myself as a cracked student either).

Boris as a teacher:
Boris is an outstanding teacher, one of the best I've had at Harvard. I found the lectures fascinating; however, the lectures got very dry in the middle of the year. Once we started talking about beams and natural frequencies for multiple degrees of freedom, Boris came alive again. There is NO ATTENDANCE policy, but YOU SHOULD GO to class. Every lecture is recorded, but you will not learn as much on Zoom. I missed a few lectures, but I could watch the recordings. The textbook is impossible to understand, as the lectures take only 5% of the textbook's information.

TFs:
The TFs in this class aren't the best. They all have very inconsistent office hours; some are passionate, while others are not. Further, grades were returned quite late, with some feedback like: "–2pt for incorrect Algebra." Do not rely on the TFs during office hours to give answers; you should always try the Pset on your own. USE CHAT GPT (which is quite good in this course) to help you check answers and give the class notes to Chat before asking it questions.

You will do well in this class if you
1) Do the problem sets on your own.
2) Go to lectures in person
3) Understand Mechanics well
This course teaches you how to analyze systems of springs, masses, and dampers in one, two, and many degrees of freedom. While the course does a good job of teaching you to solve equations of motion and find out many useful things about such systems, it does not put much emphasis on the math behind these techniques, instead expecting students mainly to just copy the techniques used in lecture to solve problems. These techniques can be mastered with sufficient practice, but you might not come away understanding the math behind all of the techniques used in the course.
The class isn't too bad, if you start the psets early. I would recommend looking at them when they are assigned, because some of them are significantly longer than others, and cannot be done last minute. I think the class is useful, though not the most riveting. The labs are pretty simple, and can be done quite quickly. The egg drop project at the end will take more time, but is also relatively straightforward.
