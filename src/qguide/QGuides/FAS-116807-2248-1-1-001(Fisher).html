If you're considering English or Literature as a concentration, I highly recommend you take this course. This seminar is unique in that it can be either an extremely light workload or extremely challenging. You'll be reading two of the most rhetorically loaded works of art known to the english language, the latter of which, Joyce's Ulysses, being a novel notorious for its impenetrability. You'll be writing weekly response essays, about three pages in length, to be turned in before each discussion. This class, if you pour yourself into it, can be one of the most demanding freshman seminars.

That being said, you can get by in this class if you don't. If a week rolls around when you just can't get through the readings, your life isn't over. Class seminars are engaging and enlightening even without having done the readings. And because both of the texts you'll be reading are steeped in allusions, you can use your prior knowledge in the weekly response papers. This isn't a course you should be stressing over. It's just a seminar. Professor Fisher and the rest of the kids in the class are there to help you advance your knowledge without killing your energy.

Should you decide to take the class, here are a few tips and tricks:
– do all of the Hamlet readings. Do them twice, if you can. Compared to Ulysses, it's not a lot of work, and Prof Fisher is incredibly versed on this front. You'll want to know what he's talking about in class :)
– For the love of god, use the UlyssesGuide. Prof. Fisher posts it on the main page of Canvas. It will actually save your life. (plus, it analyses a lot of the key ideas from each chapter in Ulysses—you should use some of those in your response essays!)
– Start each reading early. In the weekend, if you can. It'll save you stress and make the work fun, as it should be.
– Some of the chapters in Ulysses you can skip. Trust me. Chapter 15, for instance, is 150 pages of nonsense that only Joycean scholars can take to. Read the UlyssesGuide so you can still partake in discussions.
– Please please PLEASE, if you do nothing else, read chapters Six, Eight, and Eighteen. Give them your time of day and then some. They will change your life.

One more thing I want to mention: I came into this course as a writer. I wanted to use this seminar to flesh out whether English would be a good fit as a concentration. What I realized is that english majors think differently from writers, so if you're looking at this course with the same background, just know it will be different and it's up to you to make your own takeaways. But they are worthy takeaways. I would recommend this course to anyone looking for a challenge, and to fall a little bit more in love with the world of literature as we know it.
Complexity has set the bar for humanities at Harvard incredibly high. This is the most transformative and enlightening academic experience I have ever had, and I doubt that it can be surpassed (though I wholeheartedly hope that it will). I cannot recommend this class enough. I mean it. I do not know how to convey just how much I loved this course. I cannot be more grateful to have ended up in this class – it's the best thing that has happened to me academically and I will do my best to convey why it is such a special course.

Complexity realizes the full potential of what a First Year Seminar can be. A First–Year Seminar is a pass/fail course, which means that you should treat the course as if the grade was completely irrelevant. For some, it may mean that you can put in a minimal amount of work to get by. If this is what you expect, you should consider another course. Not having a lettered grading scale offers the opportunity for you to learn for the sake of learning: there is no upper limit to how much work you can put into this course. There is no assurance of "having done enough" that an "A" offers. This is a class where how much you put in is how much you get out of it. I mean this in the best sense possible – as long as you are willing to put in the work, there is simply no limit to the amount of intellectual gratification you can get out of this course. That's why I appreciated this course so much. I spent an average of ten hours per week on this course; it is my biggest time commitment of the term. I can say that every single ounce of effort I put into it was worth it. My point is that you should take this course if and only if: 1) you are passionate about the content, 2) you are willing to learn for the sake of learning, and 3) you are willing to put a lot of work into it because you enjoy learning.

What makes Professor Fisher and Complexity phenomenal is his willingness to treat you as an intellectual equal and his care. From the very first week of class, the guiding questions were complex and they never stopped being complex. One can build a course based on the questions listed in each week's handout alone. Professor Fisher does not dumb down the content so that you can understand; he presents Hamlet and Ulysses in their full complexity and thereby illustrates their greatness and their full interpretative potential. Each weekly lecture was the highlight of my week. Professor Fisher cares a lot about the content, and he gets you to care, too. I would always walk away feeling ineffably inspired, even if I had spent the night before writing my response paper until 5 AM. The lectures were so engaging and for the lack of a better term, so, so, so intellectually stimulating. It is truly a privilege to be able to take this course, and knowing that this course has ended makes me feel very sad.

Professor Fisher also provides very helpful feedback on your weekly response papers. His detailed annotation of my weekly response papers motivated me to push myself to write better and gave me the critical perspective I needed to do so. That alone makes taking the course worth it. Furthermore, I have also gained so many invaluable insights into literature and into what I want to do with my undergraduate years from talking to him during his office hours. Again, I cannot overstate my appreciation.

As a cherry on top, you will also be reading Hamlet and Ulysses, some of the greatest works of art ever created. I had no idea what Ulysses was before I started and I will never regret reading it. But if I hadn't read it with the guidance of this course, I do not think I would have been able to get nearly as much out of it as I did; Ulysses is, as Professor Fisher says, a scholarly book that is best read in an academic setting. If anything I said resonates with what you want academically, you should take this class.
Taking this course was one of the best decisions I have made this year.
This course is truly phenomenal. Professor Fisher is a genius and it was the opportunity of a lifetime to be able to take this class with him. The way he conducts the class and speaks about the texts is eye–opening. It is totally normal to complete a reading for the week and be completely lost (particularly with Ulysses), but hearing him speak about the text in class with so much passion, insight, and thoughtfulness makes you suddenly understand exactly what happened in the chapter including both explaining the content and analyzing details of Joyce's writing down to single lines describing Bloom. In every class I wondered how I could have missed such an extent of details and allusions in the text, and if I had even properly read it at all. I will say that this class is very self–selecting in terms of students who are passionate about the extent of literary analysis that it dives into. If you are not comfortable writing a relatively short response to the weekly readings (which are simply assigned chapters/scenes from the two texts), then this class might not be for you. But if you have any interest in becoming a strong reader and critic of difficult texts, this course is an absolutely brilliant masterclass taught by the most phenomenal instructor in the field. I think the pass/fail nature of seminars can make students feel less strongly about investing their time into a course, but this course is an exception. You get as much out of the class as you put into it, and this was such an extraordinary experience that I would do anything to take again.
Prof. Fisher’s “Complexity in Works of Art” was an ideal freshman seminar, and I recommend this class for anyone with any sort of interest in English (as a concentration or otherwise). Be ready to read some tough works of literature—Hamlet and Ulysses are no joke—and to write weekly essays. However, the discussions are fantastic, and Prof. Fisher is a great lecturer (and an expert in both these works of literature… absorb all you can from him!). I don’t plan to major in English nor anything adjacent to it, yet I gained a lot from this class, and am very glad I decided to take it, and it was awesome to study under Prof. Fisher. I’ve made some good friends from the course, and it was a worthy introduction to Harvard. Thank you, Prof. Fisher!
