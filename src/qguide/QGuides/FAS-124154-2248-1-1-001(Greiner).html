Great class! Definitely more difficult than I was expecting, though. The exams were not hard in the normal sense, more just challenging because they were weird, at least compared to other physics classes. It was hard to get partial credit on several problems, and the topics seemed a bit niche. The exam reviews were super helpful in highlighting what would be important, though. Also, there were no practice materials for the exams, which made it even harder to know what to expect. I would not say the exams were unfair, but they were hard to prepare for in a way that maybe could have been fixed. It is possible that future iterations of the course, if taught by Morii, will have better materials because they will have exams from this year to use. P–sets were also a bit weird and sometimes a little niche, but they were not as grueling as I have found for other physics classes, and office hours were extremely helpful. Lectures were effective, if occasionally moving a little fast, and the lecture notes provided were pretty thorough. Labs were definitely a substantial time commitment, but I found them to be far more enjoyable than in previous 15–series classes. The teaching staff cares a lot, which really made a difference. I came into this class with no particular affinity for physics (chem student) and walked out with a much greater appreciation for physics and a chem/phys concentration.
I felt like the lectures focused far too much on mathematical derivations rather than physical intuition and understanding. For the exams, I usually knew how to do the problems but I do not feel like I have a good intuitive understanding of waves in general. The problem sets were kind of silly sometimes in that you just had to look through the notes and find a specific equation that we used and do some algebra or calculus with that equation to get a desired result, and none of that really required any physics understanding. Overall it was an alright course but I came out of the class still feeling like I don’t really know much about waves.
This course is one of the best in the intro sequence. Of all of the classes, I understand the material from this class the best. The lab component is also by far the best of the 15 series. Morii is a great professor however pset difficulty oscillates WILDLY. There are some weeks you spend 10+ hours in office hours and other weeks the pset takes 3–4 hours. The exams were all reasonable and psets were graded generously.
Chances are that, if you are taking this class, it is a concentration requirement. You will get through it just fine–it is less challenging than 15b and 19/15a for sure, but equivalently somewhat less interesting. Morii is an amazing person and lecturer, and his lecture notes are very clear and concise. The tests are fair and the psets are fair too. The one major problem I had with this class was that the psets, while fair, were essentially just trivial calculations (i.e. plugging numbers in or doing long algebra for no reason), which did not enforce the concepts very well. As this is Morii's first iteration of the class in a while, I would expect that this will not be a problem in the future if he teaches it next year; otherwise, I do not have many complaints about this course that anyone can control. The lab component is really fun, too, but also much more of a time commitment than 15a/b.
The course surveys a lot of material. It's all oscillations and waves, but in different settings. Staying on top of lecture material and keeping track of big ideas/derivations is helpful.
The professor is very sweet, the main TF was so helpful with any questions I had on PSETs, and the Lab instructor I had was wonderful. I genuinely really enjoyed taking this course and learning the material even though it's still an intro course because the teaching staff I always found were incredible helpful and understanding. I don't think I'd be telling people to take this course if their field of study isn't related, but definitely not a course to be scared of!
Choose Physics 15C! This course is highly engaging and it is well worth completing the entire 15–series. Physics 15C challenged me with new topics, allowing me to learn physics concepts that are relevant for future classes (e.g., 143A) as well as explore interesting real–world applications. I particularly enjoyed the demonstrations and problem in the problem set with application to music and not only. The lab component is also very interesting. You will have the opportunity to conduct your own project!
This course definitely solidifies your intuition about wave phenomena. Taking it concurrently with 143a was very possible and I think it added to my understanding, as both courses covered many topics (like Fourier transforms) around the same time.
This is a very fair but challenging physics course. I found it to be by far the most interesting of the 15s. The content was definitely easier for me to grasp than 15b and the psets were not terribly time consuming. That being said, there were definitely still some challenging concepts (polarization, resonance, optics) and sections/office hours were necessary for me. Lab was surprisingly enjoyable. Choose a project you're interested in / a group you like because you end up spending a lot of time on the project (but I actually looked forward to lab every week).
TLDR: THIS IS HARD, BUT WORTH IT.

It is nothing like 15b or 15a/19. It will drain your soul and want to reconsider concentrating if Physics (as it was my case), but it is extremely fulfilling since you will be learning a lot. Make sure to ask early on for practice materials for exams (we lacked them for the most part) and you'll be all good.
good class! psets were difficult but doable, tests were fair, content was interesting. morii is very smart but his lectures can be a bit dry. he is very nice in office hours and truly cares about helping his students learn.
I don't think this class looks exactly the same when different professors teach it, but I thought Professor Morii's approach was quite good. He built up to the wave equation in a natural way, progressed through different types of waves, and ended the course with more light/optics–oriented material. I thought the course towards the end explained a decent amount of common phenomena too (e.g., polarization of reflected light, rainbows, diffraction, lenses, etc.).
