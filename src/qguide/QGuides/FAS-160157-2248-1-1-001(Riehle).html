Comments
This has undoubtedly been one of my favorite classes at Harvard. Though the assignments can be difficult, Professor Riehle is an excellent teacher; his lectures are very clear and engaging, and he is also an incredibly kind person. He answers emails promptly, encourages office hour meetings, and seems to genuinely care for each student. Through his teaching, I went from being unable to read the most basic manuscript with solely uppercase Greek letters to some of the most elaborate manuscripts that I may not have never even recognized as Greek before taking this class. Professor Riehle throws you headlong into the assignments from the start, which definitely requires a bit of a learning curve, but, even by the second week, the assignments begin to feel manageable. All in all, I highly recommend this class for anyone slightly interested in the idea of being able to read ancient manuscripts.
Difficult but very interesting course on book culture that imparts a niche but useful skill –– take it if you're interested in Byzantium or especially working in Greek archival materials
Definitely take it if you're looking to fulfill your language requirement for the classics department. The professor's really nice and funny. The workload's not too bad, though you will be spending around 4 hours a week doing the transcription assignments and readings. Midterm and final (I'm assumingI'm writing this before I take it) very straightforward, no surprises.
Take this class! Alex is incredibly knowledgable and teaches the course well. He acknowledges that the course is inevitably difficult, but is also understanding and is willing to accommodate any struggles people are having. The assignments, while difficult, are incredibly fun to do and are very rewarding when you finally finish them. The workload is very doable as long as you stay on top of it, and it definitely feels different from other language courses!
Prof Riehle is patient and very knowledgeable, and the course is structured in a way that emphasizes practice, not overloading students, and allowing knowledge to sink in. Students also get multiple opportunities to view and handle manuscripts in Houghton's collection.
