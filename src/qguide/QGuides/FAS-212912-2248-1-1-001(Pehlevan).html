A good class to take even if you're only interested in the machine learning aspects and not so much the neuroscience aspects. Many topics covered and one can be exposed to various interesting ideas that may be useful for their own research.
This class covers a wide ranging set of topics dealing with machine learning theory and computational neuroscience. There is a good level of depth in many topics, like studying neural networks in the infinite width limit (neural tangent kernels) but I felt overall that the course had too much breadth and could have instead focused on covering fewer topics in greater detail.
The lectures are interesting and the instructor is great at explaining hard concepts. The first few problem sets were quite long and somewhat hard, but later ones became easier.
I would definitely recommend this course for anyone interested in learning the historical progression of deep learning, since this course starts from the perceptron (which itself demonstrates very interesting behavior) and builds up from there. This perspective also helps build intuition about many things taken for granted in modern deep learning, such as model architecture, learning rules, etc.
226 is a great course for anybody who either 1) wants to go into theoretical/computational neuroscience or 2) is very good at math/physics and wants to see an interesting application. All of the curriculum is really interesting, but sometimes there isn't a coherent narrative and we seem to jump around a bit week to week.

The problem sets are comically difficult, but they are very interesting and relevant to the course. I would recommend that prospective enrollees are very good at linear algebra and calculus and have some stats background.
The psets are so hard compared to lecture it's cool material but kind of unrelated to the homework which makes the homework take so much time
Cengiz introduces very interesting topics in modern deep learning and neuroscience theory, and he brings you up to the frontier of theoretical efforts.
This is a great course if youre interested in doing theoretical research in computational neuroscience or machine learning from a physics inspired perspective. The first two problem sets are quite intense, but the remaining problem sets are considerably more manageable. And the course project is a great opportunity to work on a research topic that youre interested in.
The material is interesting and really brings you up to speed to the cutting–edge research going on in the field (specifically in the professor's group). However, I didn't find the lectures or provided lecture materials to be super cohesive, and sometimes the lectures were literally a research talk that wasn't made super clear or accessible. In addition, I often felt like the problems on the homework were a hit or miss. Overall, I think I still learned some, but was still left wanting a bit more in terms of having a better overview of the field as a whole.
Cool class between neuro, AI, and stats. Very time consuming problem sets but most of the difficulty is just doing algebra to rearrange equations to match a certain form.
Get started early on the psets!
