For all of you who are paying for this university out of pocket, in terms of being efficient for your tuition, this is negatively efficient for your tuition. This course's prerequisites are that you have done a CS internship, but CS 1060 reteaches you the least important concepts in the most confusing way possible or YouTube–able way possible. As it's the first time this course is being offered, I want to give the course staff the benefit of the doubt as they have a large task at hand –– managing a ton of student teams and ensuring that they all work together and work well to create a fully tested and developed product. However, the course itself can do with a lot of restructuring and improvements to narrow down the scope of what is taught and in handling the assignments in the course.

To begin, the homework is extremely wordy and most of the time difficult to understand (with points taken off arbitrarily for things like naming, etc). The biggest investment you have to make in this course is the number of times you have to re–log into Canvas after Harvard changed their login to Okta to open the 10 tabs you'll need to understand a single portion of the poorly worded homework problem where you'll be spending 2.5x the time you actually spent on the homework on deploying your app. The in class attendance reading quizzes were inconsistent at best and extremely undoable at worst, specifications for the final projects were given late, the lecture topics weren't planned well, and the TFs were unknowledgeable about fundamental grading criteria regarding projects, and feedback itself was generally unspecific and SAT/UNSAT homework assignments, although well intentioned, encouraged team members to just 'get the work done' regardless of the quality of the code or work. The extra credit for talking in class, responding on Ed, etc. was vague and there's not a great way to track this.

Harvard is in desperate need of an industry standard software engineering course especially with all of the theoretical and research focus in the degree, and it's great that we're moving towards a more application direction outside of the systems and HCI courses (61, 161, 178, 262, 265, etc). This class attempts to tackle a lot of topics, and although a prerequisite of the course is having done multiple CS courses or an internship, the course has a fault of either introducing fundamental topics such as git way too late in the semester, assuming an understanding of fundamental software engineering concepts (i.e. code reviews, testing, deployment, etc), or in having a hard time finding a balance between teaching software engineering and generative AI. Until we have a more robust foundation for software engineering concepts in the coursework at Harvard, it can be difficult to assemble groups and assume that everyone is on a similar knowledge level on engineering concepts. I was surprised that even some general architecture / writing usable code (concepts such as KISS, SOLID principles, factories, etc) weren't covered, although this is more on the general software engineering side rather than software engineering and generative AI. I wish there was a focus on more fundamental topics in order to help teammates as people are coming from different backgrounds, and being in a team where half of the team doesn't know how git works can doom you to merge conflict hell.

Regardless of all of this, this is one of the most useful CS courses that you'll be able to do if you want a good understanding of software engineering or just want to be forced to work a LOT with generative AI (if you haven't been doing so already.) It introduces people to something that will be very important to know for the future, ticketing with Linear, working with Git with many other people, and more. It's a very well intentioned course that can be successful and a fundamental one to take for every CS student in future iterations. Christopher, although he can be a bit of a personality that some people may not like, is knowledgable, helpful, and always available and understanding. Christopher clearly cares a lot about the course and is a great person to get to know –– we do need more courses like this at Harvard. However, for the course as it is now, going in with a good team that's excited to work together on a project idea will make or break your experience. The actual experience of working with different generative AI tools and learning how to prompt engineer, debug, and write tests is extremely helpful for the future. However, if you're less familiar with some software lifecycle concepts or are going in alone, I'd be cognizant of taking this course if you don't want to be hunting down teammates to submit assignments.
