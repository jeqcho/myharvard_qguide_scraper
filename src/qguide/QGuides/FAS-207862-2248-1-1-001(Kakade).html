If you're interested in foundation models, I would really recommend this class!

The real strength of the course is the lectures. The lectures Sham/people from Kempner did were really great quality and had good slides that serve as an excellent reference. The lectures from guest lecturers in industry were really incredible and gave a really good understanding of what's going on in the foundation model space. This course is overall a great opportunity to get a better look at what's going on in terms of foundation model research and really interesting perspectives on real open problems in the space that have practical applications.

The homework assignments were not particularly illuminating – they were mostly taken off the internet. I think the staff could have done better if they actually made interesting homework assignments that pointed to key learning outcomes of the course. But the assignments were fine in terms of difficulty/time commitment.

The projects were fine, though I am unsure how they will turn out in terms of grading (everyone probably got all/almost all the points on the homework assignments, so I think the project is really how they will grade the course). I hope the grade distribution is not totally out of line of what is typical, though I don't imagine they would deviate much from typical CS graduate course grading. The TFs did hold office hours for help on both projects and assignments, but feedback was only provided on the milestone and pretty late in the game, though I do think the feedback was helpful.

Overall a nice course. The guest lectures are really a strength, and I think the slides are great for the other lectures. Doesn't seem to be a lot of effort into the assignments/projects from the course staff, but I suppose this is not necessarily atypical for a graduate CS course.
One of the only classes that's about some cutting–edge stuff. It's basically a bunch of practitioners giving lectures on their area of expertise so quality of class can vary week to week. Jonathan Frankle from MosaicML was amazing!
Take it. The lectures and guest speakers are absolutely fantastic. The assignments are not too bad either.
Very rigorous and goes into substantial technical depth. Deserves its place as a graduate–level CS course.
This class had a couple real gems in lectures, but could overall use some improvement.

CONTENT

The course covered a wide variety of topics in training foundation models. In particular, it began with basic backprop + gradient descent, edge of stability, optimization techniques and theory, sharding, training pipeline (pre/mid/post), parallelization, generative AI, inference, with interspersed topics taught by guest lectures: Jonathan Frankle (databricks), Roy Frostig (jax), Julia Neagu / Deanna Emery (QuotientAI), Vahan Petrosyan / Leo Linden (SuperAnnotate), and Jacob Austin (Google Deepmind). The main instructors for the content were Sham and various Kempner postdocs.

The material is largely interesting, but not very organized. Some of the lectures were quite interesting, for instance Frankle discussed some relatively original takes; the first two lectures had some intriguing theoretical content, etc. However, several lectures depend critically on familiarity; for instance, I think it is very difficult to take anything out of a jax lecture if one has not worked with the language before. For many lectures, without references or implementation exercises, it was difficult to take something concrete; for instance, in either the parallelization lectures or the generative AI lectures (both of which were interesting, but some exercises would have been nice).

Overall, a fun class to take which is low–workload and is essentially a sequence of seminar talks, and not necessarily an organized and very rigorous course.

HW

There were three HWs which constituted two of Sasha Rushs puzzles (available publicly on GitHub). This caused the class to be very easy in terms of workload; however, I wish there were HW assignments which more accurately described the content. For instance, it would have been nice to actually train a model using parallel compute, actually implement some of the optimization techniques, use JAX, implement generative models (e.g. diffusion, etc.).

PROJECT

There is a project at the end of the course. This project is pretty open–ended and is a good opportunity for students to engage in some original work in ML, and I appreciate it as the only major assignment of the course. There are a few milestones where we received decent feedback. Again, I think it would have been nice for there to be some starting guidelines, possibly in the HW assignments, for places to start; generating ideas was an interesting but relatively nontrivial process.
This was a good class on foundation models and modern machine learning. The lectures are predominantly led by guest speakers, which was very valuable since many of them offered a practical or applied perspective from industry. Sham is also clearly very knowledgeable about the subject. There was a high learning–to–work ratio, which I think is ideal for classes. There was minimal guidance on the final project from the course staff, though, and it was unclear what they were really looking for.
