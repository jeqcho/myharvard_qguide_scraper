After a one year hiatus, MCB 165, a class on viruses, is officially back for students. Taught by Victoria D’Souza, this class specifically looks into the fields of virology, biology, and structural biology as students look into the mechanisms behind viruses and their mechanism of infection.

Personally, I have really enjoyed MCB 165 and found it to be an extremely interesting class. Although the concepts taught within the course were without a doubt difficult, Victoria does a great job of breaking them apart into smaller, manageable, bit–sized pieces for better comprehension. Additionally, Victoria’s passion for the field is incredibly evident and clear, with her excitement often shining through in her lectures.

For assignments, the class comes with weekly reading summaries on discussion articles, which account for 35% of your grade, lecture and discussion session quizzes, which account for 15% of your grade, and a final presentation on a paper of your choice, which accounts for 20% of your grade. The final 30% comes from participation within the section, where students are responsible for engaging in conversation on the weekly discussion articles. In my opinion, these assignments are extremely reasonable and are not hard to complete – resulting in an easier to balance course.

A major weakness of this course came in the form of the Discussion Sections, which happen every Monday. In these sessions, the TFs lead students through a weekly article related to the course topic. However, the sections have become increasingly unengaging and miserable as the semester went on. As opposed to promoting insightful conversation, the TFs instead just go figure by figure, sub panel by subpanel, asking students “what is the main idea here?” and waiting until someone responds. Unfortunately, this is just very unengaging and fails to facilitate interest amongst the students, instead making it more like pulling teeth in order to get a response from students. Personally, I would’ve liked to see a more natural conversation structure in these sections, as opposed to a rigid panel–by–panel analysis structure.

Overall, I think that MCB 165 is interesting and is a good course for anyone that wants to learn more about viruses. Although I maintain my point that Section has quickly become one of the worst components of the class, I think the content itself and the information you take away is extremely interesting for the course at–large. I think this is definitely a course worth considering.

This class is super chill overall–––highly recommend it if you are looking for a manageable upper level MCB course during a busy semester. The weekly workload is just reading a (relatively more complex) research paper and writing a 500–word summary for it. Sometimes it is a bit tricky deciding what to fit into your summary, but it is excellent practice for being concise and getting the broad picture when discussing a complex topic. There are not too many instructions for these assignments, so you have a lot of freedom in terms of how you approach your work. This could be helpful but it can also be confusing exactly what is expected of you. But I would say they are super chill graders, so if you put in decent effort and show you are doing your best to understand and grow, you'll be fine. Besides, you can always reach out with questions and they are usually very fast at answering emails. The quizzes during every class can sound daunting but usually they turn out fine. Just study your materials a bit before class and it shouldn't be a problem. The final presentation at the end of the semester, like all other assignments, were pretty flexible and you are not given that many instructions. So overall, I would just say give all assignments your best effort–––participate in section–––and you will do more than fine. The lack of directions sometimes can feel overwhelming at first, but I think that is supposed to help you and not hurt (or restrict) you too much. Lastly, the lectures can be very fast–paced and confusing sometimes. The slides could have been more clear and informative for someone who does not know that much about viruses and HIV (the main virus we talk about throughout the semester). A lot of the times, I had to do more research after class to clear up what was discussed during class, but overall, no big problems.
Take this course if you want to improve your ability to read and understand scientific papers/figures. Lectures were interesting (although not recorded so make sure you take good notes). Section is amazing! Overall, the class is not too heavy in regards to workload and it gives you space to actually learn without feeling too stressed. I would highly recommend.
