TAKE THIS COURSE. Amazing professor that exudes passion for the course material. He takes an approach that takes into consideration the passions of each student. Each weeks discussions and readings are engaging and help you see psychosis in a new light.
If you have the chance to take this course, do it! This is one of my favorite courses I’ve taken in the psychology department. Professor Knutsen is by far the best professor I’ve had the opportunity to learn from during my time at Harvard. He is easy to reach out to and communicate with, does an amazing job of facilitating discussion, and cares about his students’ wellbeing.
Dr. Knutsen is so kind and willing to help you!
If you're looking for a class to teach you all there is to know about psychosis, this is not the course. It can be frustrating that there's almost never a 100% definitive answer to most questions you have. But if you're interested in mental health, this course will change the way you think. A few weeks ago I probably would've said I don't really even know what I've learned, but now I see a lot of facets of my own life differently, because of the exposure and deeper conversations you have in this class.
This was a pretty solid course, especially for its first year being offered. I really appreciated how flexible the professor was and how responsive he was to student feedback (seriously, Prof. Knutsen was awesome and so understanding!!). The guest lectures were engaging and easily one of the best parts of the class. I also really liked the anti–stigma campaign proposal project. It felt meaningful and gave us a chance to apply what we were learning. As for cons, the class felt a bit dry at times. The readings toward the end of the semester became repetitive and less interesting. It did not feel like we explored many new or challenging topics in the emerging field, and there was not much critical discussion about the material. This is not entirely the professor's fault, as this seems to be a common issue across most courses in the psych department. Seminars often lack deeper intellectual engagement or space for real dialogue. There was also a lot of time spent on lecturing. While I understand that some students requested this format early on, I personally found it ineffective. It took time away from discussion and made class feel more passive. Discussions themselves were usually slow, and it seemed difficult for the professor to get students to engage. Still, I think the topic is interesting and the professor is committed to improving the course, so with a few changes, I'm sure this class will become much more engaging and effective in the future.
