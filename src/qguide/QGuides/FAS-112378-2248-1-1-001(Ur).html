Professor Ur is a great lecturer, and there are some fun sections where you get to visit places like Peabody and the pottery studio. There is no midterm and final exam, but reading quizzes every class + quiz at end of each lecture on what was discussed. There are also section assignments most weeks, which can take an hour. Definitely one of the more work–heavy geneds, but is super interactive and well taught.

In the end this class should be one you enjoy by thinking about the past, and grappling with how to interpret it.

This is an exceptional GenEd class! The content is introductory, not requiring any prior knowledge of anthropology or archeology, but it covers a broad range of content, from modern archeological techniques to the interpretation of historical artifacts. The sections, unlike any other class that I have taken, were the strongest part of this course—they were often interactive, having us go all around campus (e.g., Harvard graveyard, ceramics studio, Peabody museum, etc.), and I found them to be remarkably insightful! Lastly, the assignments for the course are reasonable, and the Capstone project was quite a joy to finish! This is a great course that I would recommend to anybody looking for another GenEd.

This class is incredibly interesting, even for someone with no prior background or interest in the topic. I decided to take it after a friend convinced me, and I’m so glad I did! The topics are highly relevant to issues today. Explore the syllabus and see if the topics interest you. If you’re even mildly curious, I think you will really enjoy it. The workload includes one section assignment per week, which usually took me 1.5 to 2 hours to complete. A key tip: start your capstone early! It requires a significant amount of time since you’ll need to write a 2,500–word essay, learn how to use the ArcGIS platform, and format and add visual elements. Both the section assignments and the capstone are engaging and rewarding. The TFs are friendly and helpful, and Jason is an amazing professor—so approachable and kind. If you have the chance, invite him to Classroom to Table! Overall, take this course if you can—you won’t regret it!
I recommend taking this course, especially if you are slightly interested in archaeology! Not only did I learn more about the past, but my beliefs in it were challenged as I better understood the process of archaeology and how professionals in the field interpret the past. I learned that while many conclusions about the past could be made, there is much caution and attention to detail paid to every artifact, site, writing, etc. This course made me more open–minded when thinking about history and the past, as well as made me consider new historical discoveries with a grain of salt, as we can be wrong about our past too. The workload is not excessive, but necessary in understanding archaeology and in the context of the course, and with the two weekly readings, one weekly section assignments, and the two in–class quizzes, you can absolutely get by by putting in the time and effort, as you will feel the rewards!
