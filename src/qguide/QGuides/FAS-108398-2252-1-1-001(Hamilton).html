DO NOT TAKE MIGUELS SECTION! Unfortunately, my interaction with Miguel as a section leader has been incredibly negative — one of the most negative during my Harvard career. Various recurring issues greatly impacted learning and attendance:
Unprofessionalism and Reluctance to Take Responsibility: Miguel was regularly late to the section. Several times, he failed to attend scheduled meetings or suddenly terminated them, even when he had asked me to stay late after work to talk to me. When I tried to seek help, he typically responded offhandedly or seemed unconcerned, rolling his eyes or giving curt responses. It was as if providing general academic support was a task to him rather than something he did as part of his work.
Misaligned Expectations: During the first class, Miguel mentioned that only 1–2 students in each section would ever achieve an A — and that it would require doing "a publishable research paper." This is incompatible with either the guidelines for general education courses or the rubric set up by the course staff. It set up an overly intimidating and discouraging environment, especially for those without prior academic research experience.
Unfair Grading Practices: Even as we were repeatedly instructed to "follow the rubric," Miguel repeatedly deducted points for his additional criteria that were never informed to us in advance. This created confusion and undermined the transparency of the grading process.
Unfair Section Expectations: Miguel imposed additional workload and expectations (such as requiring submissions of annotated reading before every section) on no other section. While the assignment per se was not unfair, it wasn't reasonable to impose it suddenly or without appending it to the syllabus — especially when this was a general education class with an equal format throughout the sections.
Hostile Learning Environment: In the early part of the term, I attempted to contribute frequently to the discussion. Though I raised my hand each time, I was often overlooked or not asked to respond, and this exclusion ultimately led me to stop contributing. Ironically, Miguel would ask me to participate in the latter part of the course. These sporadic and seemingly punitive measures rendered the section a hostile learning environment rather than an open academic community.
Overall, even with the excellence of the professor and course materials, the quality of the section — and, by extension, the course experience — was significantly impacted negatively by Miguel's behavior, grading, and conduct. I strongly recommend reconsidering his role as a section leader, particularly in an undergraduate Gen Ed course, where supportiveness, fairness, and clarity must be the highest priorities.
