You are probably taking Physics 15b because you have to. I would say a few things about this course. Number one, Carlos is an extremely kind professor and cares so much about their students. They put a lot of work into making the class community strong and I think that it shows, so I would commend them very strongly for that. I do think that lectures move very, very fast. There is so much content crammed into this semester – you show up to a lecture, then you come to the next one and we're already talking about something completely different with a whole new set of equations and rules. Keeping up can feel overwhelming at times. The PSETs are very difficult, and you will need a strong study group and/or office hours to get them done. The exams are challenging but you're given a lot of opportunity to get credit, including a group portion that counts for 20%, which was a nice cushion. For the exams, I strongly recommend studying from the PSETs. It will really help prepare you for the kind of questions you can expect. Generally, I was very stressed out about this class – both in terms of the PSETs, understanding the content, and the exams. It can get overwhelming really fast if you don't plan carefully. However, sections were quite helpful, and the grading is very fair – the course staff is definitely looking to give you as much credit as possible. So overall, I think that the structure of this class is quite good and supportive, but don't have any illusions about the fact that the content is extremely difficult.
15B is conceptually a very hard course. Lectures are often proof sessions where students become lost 20 minutes in (occasional demos are so much fun to watch, but don't replace much of the confusion throughout). The real learning in this class occurs during section, office hours, physics night, and generally while you are working on your PSET. The nice thing about this class is that basically everything you ever need to know shows up on a PSET at some point, making exams straightforward to study for. The 15B experience is definitely a stressful and difficult one, however I feel rewarded at the end of it and definitely am walking away with a level of understanding in E&M that satisfies me.

Regarding grades, the head instructor of this course told my study group that students in this class often fall into one of two groups: One group is a group of students who score very close to the average and have some level of understanding in the class. The other group is the students who don't really understand much and are at a much lower point relative to the average. According to the head instructor, falling in group one means that you will get at least an A– if not an A, meaning this course is actually very doable even if you feel like you don't have true mastery of the material.

The class has its moments (especially with the demos done during lecture), but personally, I was hoping for a little more rigor, especially with some of the proofs done in class. I feel like I learned more about how to solve physics problems than actual physics. To summarize, don't feel afraid to shop the course, and it's a decent way to get the Electromagnetism requirement out of the way, and its not a bad introduction to the field, especially if you plan to take Electrodynamics the following spring.

Speaking on the class in general, 15B is a time–tested course at Harvard and it shows. The textbook is bulletproof, the content being taught is well thought through, and the psets do a good job encapsulating the week's content.

Speaking on my personal experience, it was, in many ways, shaped by the fact that Carlos is not a good teacher. Carlos is a lovely individual who excels in their field, and also so happens to be completely ineffective at delivering lectures. Carlos often mumbles through most if not all of what they are saying, and makes it REALLY difficult to pick up anything during lecture. This has nothing to do with their accent, Carlos just does not speak or explain things clearly. I would seriously argue that there is almost no point in going to lecture – you are much better off reading the textbook. If you can hold off until a semester with a different prof, I would strongly recommend you do so.

Carlos also makes you resubmit each of the exams online as a group 24h after the test is over – this does ultimately serve to inflate your grade, but I just found it to me a massive slog. The last thing I wanted to do after finishing an exam was to have an extra assignment where I go over everything again. We already learn the content on the psets – I just think this is completely unnecessary and a waste of time.

Also, I know it says you can take 21A concurrently with 15B, but I really cannot discourage you from doing this enough. You need a STRONG understanding of multivariable calculus to do well in this class. In fact, if you have one, I would say it's relatively easy to do well. If you aren't comfortable with multi though, this class will cause a lot of unnecessary pain, assuming you can just hold off until you have the right background. Also also, this class doesn't list 21B as a prereq, but then goes decently heavy into differential equations. If you can hold off on 15B until you've seen diffeq's in another more formal setting, I would also seriously recommend that. Ultimately, as someone who had taking 21A and 21B coming into this class, I felt very prepared, and thought the workload was more than manageable.
