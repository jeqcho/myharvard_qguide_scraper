It's 8 hours in class every week, and the only work outside of class are the papers. There are 3 of them and if ur like us, each paper absolutely destroys you for 3 days and then you can go back to not doing anything for the class again (averaged out to 7 hrs per week outside of class).
It's a good class, and as a theorist I enjoyed getting to try out the cool experiments. Joe and Jieping are absolutely amazing and make the class a great experience
The class with Silvera and Yacoby is interesting. They make about 10% of the class experience, while the TF's make the rest. Joe and Jieping teach you how to use the equipment, how to collect data and analyze it properly. They give advice and actually "get their hands dirty" along with you. They are the ones that will teach you the most. The unfortunate part is that the professors do the grading, and the grading is ONLY based on the two papers of your last two experiments (the first one is not graded). So make SURE you either know how to write a paper or learn fast, because the professors gave very little guidance but demanded the level of a paper that gets into APS. I know this is unfair and it felt like it, but I am trying to help you out, since this is an expectation that doesn't match the level of effort the professors put in to actually teach you how to write a paper. I thought this was gonna be the place to learn how to write one, but it was mostly the place where I learned that I don't know how to write a good one. Unfortunately I am still not confident in my paper–writing skills, but I am much more confident in my lab and coding skills, and I made many friends in this class. Also make sure you get along with your partner! Doesn't have to be a friend, but a good colleague who will not let all responsibility fall on your shoulders.
You get to do some REALLY cool experiments in this class, some of which are representative of quintessential discoveries from the past century. Regardless of whether or not you’re an experimentalist/going for honors/going to grad school, I would recommend you give this class a shot since being able to perform these experiments is really a special opportunity. One small downside was that the introduction to the experiments given by the lab wiki/manual could do with some updates and improvements, since a lot of time you might have to do a substantial amount of outside reading and research to understand the theory/experimental background. The highlight of this course was the teaching team: Prof Silvera and Yacoby are the best. Prof Yacoby pushes you to deeply understand the relevant physics behind your work, and some of his explanations and lectures in this course have been the most enjoyable and illuminating expositions I’ve ever experienced. Prof Silvera is very sweet and caring, bringing liquid nitrogen ice cream and pie on the last day to share with the students. Joe and Jieping though, are the true stars. Not only are they genuinely some of the kindest and most caring folks I’ve met here, always wearing a smile and brightening the mood at the lab, they’re also so knowledgeable and helpful about all the experiments. I have no clue what we would’ve done without them. Overall, highly recommended!
It's pretty tough. I think people have different experiences depending on which experiments they do. I had never done any research before this class, so I really had no idea what to expect, and I definitely felt a bit behind everyone else on the first round of experiments. Very much so feels like trial by fire. When it comes time to write the paper, this course becomes super time intensive outside of class. You are definitely working against the clock. You really need to be able to do data analysis in R or Python, that part isn't taught at all. The best advice is to start doing analysis as soon as you can so you can maybe have time to go back and try your data collection again if it seems off (though in practice I don't think many people do this). There is really no grading criteria, you just get comments on the three papers you submit. Supposedly only the last two count towards your grade, but who knows if that's actually true, it seems like the final grades are just given based off vibes. I did learn a lot though and got way better with lab procedures and electronics.
This class is hard, regardless of how much you know, because there is always room for your work to be better. Entering this class I had not done any experimental physics before, and now I know that it is actually a bit enjoyable. Pick a lab partner you are friends with, if you can. Your relationship may be tested through the semester, but if you are with someone you are not friends with, it will be worse. Also– do not procrastinate starting the paper!!! We all did. Even if it is just starting planning an outline or doing data analysis the same day as you collect the data, start early on. Otherwise the paper becomes overwhelming and a couple days of your life are completely swallowed up. (And you'll probably not have half as much fun as you would if it were an extended process.)
Very exciting class that exposes you to physics from a variety of research fields. Paper writing at the end of each project takes longer than you expect, start it early.
This is a fantastic class. It's certainly not easy, but the types of experiments you work on and the lessons you learn from conducting the experiments on your own is truly valuable for any physicists. It also provides a great deal of support to ensure everyone can succeed in the course.
