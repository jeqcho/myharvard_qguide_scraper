I thought Professor Logevall's lectures were fantastic – probably my favourite lectures in my time at Harvard so far. The course is structured chronologically, starting with French colonialism in Vietnam and ending at the war's conclusion in 1975 (before looking briefly at the legacies of the war). Because of this, the lectures felt like one continuous story, and I was always excited to hear the next chapter of the story each week. I especially loved the use of different media in lecture – Logevall loves playing clips from various White House discussions, and it was really interesting to hear what leaders were thinking about during pivotal moments of the war.

Readings really varied week to week – some weeks had 250 pages, others only about 50. I found the readings interesting and accessible, and particularly enjoyed the opportunity to read fiction. I do think people who were less interested in long–form reading may have found the reading a little dry / intense.

Sections were not particularly well–facilitated, but I think this will depend on TFs. The 'weekly brief' assignment was a little confusing, and I didn't receive any feedback on my writing which was a little frustrating. So I think the value of the course mostly comes from lectures and readings – your experience will probably depend on how interested / bought in you are.
This is one of the best classes I have taken at Harvard. Learning about the Vietnam war from both the American and Vietnamese experience was incredible. Professor Logevall is amazing and challenges you to think deeply about historical questions, the value of history and how the war shaped the America (and world) we live in today. It is a challenging class in the sense that there is a lot of reading, but I have never enjoyed reading as much as I did in this class. It felt meaningful, purposeful and really made me challenge what I thought I knew about the war. I felt moved by the class and I realized how understanding this period of history really enhanced my understanding of the America we live in today.
Amazing amazing class. Logeval is a great lecturer. Learned lots. Section is less interesting but class is still worth it in my opinion. Midterm and finals weren’t so bad at all — very doable.
Overall, the lectures are great and Professor Logevall is incredibly knowledgable on the subject. I found the section formats to be helpful as well. However, there is not any feedback given on assignments. It is definitely on the harder end for an intro course.
Particularly if you’re a history major would highly recommend taking it. It’s a good bit of work but for sure worth it if you’re at all interested in the topic.

Would recommend this class to any history concentrators or people interested in learning more about the Vietnam War. Professor Logevall is very honest and transparent that this course is designed more heavily around the US POV of the war. For those who might have been looking for a more Vietnamese–/Southeast Asian–centric course, this will probably not satisfy that, but it is still very interesting, and he does do his best to incorporate the history of Vietnamese nationalism and a Viet guest speaker. The Vietnam War is such a pivotal historical event to examine, both for its impacts on American politics, as well as the outcomes of geopolitics and post–colonial history of the Indochinese region. This course had a lot of great potential to explore the intersection of the perspectives, but I felt like untimely communication of test standards, as well as the inaccessibility of readings, diminished the learning experience greatly.
This is a great class, but really not indicative of the history department or the undergraduate history concentrator experience. And if you want to get to know Prof. Logevall (understandable; he's great) –– good luck. A good course to take your junior or senior year, to fulfill a departmental requirement and to check one of Harvard's classic history courses off your list.
The good thing about this class is that truly no prior knowledge is required or expected. Sure, there are certainly Vietnam War buffs in the class, but knowing very little going in, with this not being my subfield of interest, I did just fine. The weekly briefs do play kind of a deciding factor in whether or not you'll get an A, as do the midterm, so stay on top of those. Logevall is also famously a great lecturer. My reservation with this class is that if you're looking for more Vietnamese perspectives you simply won't get it. It's very US centric which course staff acknowledges but doesn't see a problem with. Overall, if you work hard you'll do fine!

Take this class, it is one of the more enlightening courses you'll take here. Professor Logevall is an excellent lecturer, and very approachable. The content will teach you a lot about things very relevant to today. Do the readings, they're interesting (but you'll also get tested on them).

This course gives you an in–depth analysis of the Vietnam War from the American perspective and also introduces historical thinking (counterfactuals, how to analyze texts, etc). I really enjoyed the weekly readings and the weekly briefs were very manageable. You need to attend lecture because that's where most of the course content comes from and the materials are not published outside of lecture.

I would consider the course grading before enrolling. The class has a midterm exam, final paper, and final exam which makes up majority of the grade. There is no feedback given after weekly writing assignments and no rubrics for the assignments which makes it difficult to improve work over the period of the course.
