Courseload is extremely manageable – just make sure to attend as regularly as possible as it constitutes a large part of your grade. Feedback is sparse and grading is slow, hard to tell how you are doing until the very end.
It is a chill course with not a lot of weekly assignments. You do learn a lot from reading a paper every week. However, you will not receive much feedback throughout the semester. Grading is pretty much non existent since they take forever to get a grade for a single assignment back. As I'm writing this report I have not received grades for half of the assignments I've submitted. However, work is minimal and you will not be stressing over it.
Take it! The course staff prioritizes your learning, and therefore all assignments are very intentional. You learn a lot and become a better scientific thinker through this course.
You take out what you put in. You can either walk away from this course learning a lot about adolescent brain development, or you can learn absolutely nothing if you don't engage yourself. I think what this course is really good at is having an extremely light workload, so most of the time is spent fostering your own curiosity and discussing articles during section. Extremely extremely extremely manageable course.
I would probably advise against taking this class. Professor Hensch is nice once you get to know him, but the class is just not well structured/organized.

Background: This class covers ways researchers try to preserve neuroplasticity in the brain, even after critical periods. The content in interesting, but presented in a bit of a dry way in lecture. In total, there is very little work for this class. Each week, you read a scientific paper or two in depth and write a couple of deep discussion questions to submit. One week, you have to lead an hour long discussion on one of the papers and essentially run the section. Then the final assignment is a short grant proposal (about 4 pages double spaced).

That said, THIS CLASS IS NOT EASY AND THERE IS NO FEEDBACK ON ANYTHING. Each week, the discussion questions are evaluated on a 0–3 scale, but we only got 2/12 weeks of discussion questions back, so I am not sure how I did on almost the entire class. Also, the scale is not "a 3 is an A", "a 2 is a B" and so on. The professor just says "2 and 3 is good" and "1 means you need a bit more work/depth in your questions." Professor Hensch says, "we will contact you if we are concerned about you." I then never received any feedback on leading the section discussion. I definitely don't think that I will ever get the final grant proposal back. Its annoying that everything is so cryptic. I could be doing amazing or failing right now, which is a bit stressful. I really hope I do well because otherwise I will be very upset that there was no feedback provided along the way.

That said, I really enjoyed how high–level some of the discussions we had in section were. This felt like an advanced Harvard class, and I grew a lot in my ability to read papers and talk about them. These are important skills I will carry with me.

It's up to you whether you want to take it but that is everything you really need to know.
Great course and learned a lot about super interesting topics in early life development! Only work per week was reading a science paper and submitting questions based on it. The other major components of the course grade are a research proposal, leading a 75 min class discussion on a paper, and participation (which they really emphasize). The only thing I would be aware of is that we didn't really get much feedback on assignments throughout the course, so I never knew what my grade was lol. Nonetheless, it was a super manageable, interesting course and would recommend for an upper level mcb/neuro class, especially as a senior.
This course is wonderful. The grading is very confusing; to be honest, I didn't ever figure out how discussion questions were graded throughout the course. We were told we'd receive a 1, 2, or 3 on the quality of our discussion questions, but not whether it would go into the gradebook that way. I did experience a good deal of stress during the course about how my grade would turn out at the end.
However, the course itself is wonderful. Low workload for major academic gain. I learned a lot about new neurobiology topics that I never would have gotten exposed to in the same way in other classes. The information I learned was very useful, too; it was all new, cutting–edge stuff that was relevant even to biotech/healthcare finance jobs. The course is a little difficult; you really had to grow in learning how to critically analyze dense scientific papers to make it through the class on top. However, overall, a wonderful course.
I loved this class and really liked how the content was based on existing research papers. The section component of the class is super cool, because each week a different student presents a paper to the class and everyone engages in discussion together.
Course is a pretty great overview in concepts and mechanisms underlying neuroplasticity and development. It is best if you feel comfortable analyzing and reading scientific papers. Every week, you will discuss the papers in a student–led "journal club" which is enjoyable if you are invested in the papers and have questions and critiques you like to share. Light course load for advanced neuro class but still learned many new stories in the field of neuroscience.
a generally good neuro class for those who prefer reading scientific literature over the more traditional lecture–discussion model. every section is a paper discussion led by a student. this course definitely sharpened my scientific literacy skills in terms of reading papers effectively and efficiently, as well as how to facilitate a discussion around it. as long as you do the weekly paper readings, this class is highly manageable. lecture content is pretty interesting if you are into critical periods or topics around it. that being said, course structure could have been a lot more organised. canvas organization started off poorly (assignments posted irregularly, wrong deadlines on assignments, a mix of email and canvas communication) but got better over the weeks, and i hope it starts that way for future semesters. assignments were posted pretty late, but as long as you did the reading notes on another doc you can just copy paste it in. course readings came out very late the first few times, but got better after student feedback. i hope they continue these improvements, but in case they do not and go back to the same structure they had at the start of the course: (1) read the syllabus early and often. all dates, deadlines, and expectations are there, and the course staff expect you to know it. the class structure can be weird and assignments WILL sneak up on you if you do not keep track of them. (2) advocate for better systems – ask for a sign–up sheet for paper presentations, for papers to be uploaded at the same time weekly so you can plan ahead, for your reading grades, etc. they seem to expect the students to either have every deadline in their mind without reminders, which i suppose is a fair assumption, but it couldn't hurt to have some earlier nudges for a midterm proposal rather than on the day of. the course staff are relatively accessible and supportive, but it definitely took a few requests for these changes to be implemented.

would recommend for those with a strong interest in critical periods and those who enjoy/want to get better at reading scientific literature, but this course was definitely frustrating at first for students who value organized course structures and frequent communication about deadlines. as mentioned, i hope this improves because the course content was genuinely interesting, and all my frustrations were purely logistical. but in the event that these problems persist, as long as you keep an eye on that syllabus (literally pin it on your wall or something) you should be fine.
