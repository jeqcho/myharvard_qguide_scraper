This is a great class. Substantially improves your modeling toolkit and exposes you high level to a whole host of interesting and connected areas of applied mathematics, which you will have seen perhaps elsewhere in other courses. I recommend you take this toward the end of your AM degree –– I took as a senior –– because it lets you become acquainted in depth with the subjects and techniques in other classes (ie stat, optimization, coding, etc) before being asked to tackle them and apply them for yourself in this class. The projects are super interesting and very free form – you can work on what you are interested in (you will know better what interests you as a senior than as a freshman!). The grading does not seem excessively punishing at all, the Prof is super passionate for the subject and just wants to see you give it a good go. The first p set is very challenging and daunting, but dont be afraid – p sets are not weekly, there are only four total, and they get easier. Overall would strongly recommend the class even outside of its implications for the AM degree, modeling is a fantastic skillset and is taught passionately and not arduously here.
the course was extremely disorganized. assignments/notifications about assignments were not posted in one place. the content is not very difficult, but please don't take this course if you can since it's so disorganized. i hope something will change in future iterations.
Consider this course only if you are genuinely interested in learning mathematical modeling methods. The content of the course was interesting, however, the class was pretty disorganized. There were many errors in problem sets and project prompts a lot of the time which would cause confusion. Questions would go unanswered a lot of the times which would just perpetuate confusion.
This was the first year where it was a combined/hybrid class with 215 (graduate mathematical modeling), and as a result, it was pretty chaotic and mismanaged. The lectures are fairly interesting, and the majority of the class is working on open–ended projects, so it can't really be that bad.
This class is the bridge between all of the applied math classes at Harvard and real world applications/research. Super useful class!
Oh my god what a disorganized class. Every lecture you learn a new modeling technique, so the class doesn't build on itself and I don't think I learned much at all. Good thing I guess is that you can't get behind in the class. The psets were vague and I often did not know how to start, at least chatgpt can help you give a framework. After the first two lectures class attendance dropped significantly. TFs did not have solutions to psets and many times they would be orienting THEMSELVES when you go to OH. Nice thing tho is that there are only 4 psets the entire semester, so you could go a week or two between psets and projects without doing any work at all for the class. I'd say the modeling projects are graded generously, and that's the bulk of your grade anyways. If you have a good group for the projects then you're golden, especially as there are no sit–in exams. I would have thought that because this class is required for honors that it would be more organized and structured. Overall the class could definitely improve, but for what it's worth, it's not that stressful or that much work. You get what you want out of it: if you're just trying to get honors, then just take it and get it over with, period. If you're thesising and really interested in modeling, you can definitely get a lot out of it!
This course was run quite poorly this year. It was disorganized and support was lackluster. Hopefully it'll be better in the future!
Have some background in statistics/inferencing, as well as python familiarity, before taking the course. It isn't utterly necessary although it is very helpful to avoid a learning curve on the psets and to follow the lecture. Coursework is reasonable and fair relative to the content – projects can be really fun but that's totally up to you! Brenner is truly so kind and excited about what he teaches – you should get to know him !
This is the perfect class for seniors. If you work well on team projects, have enough creativity to brainstorm interesting project ideas, keep yourself accountable, and engage with the course material, you are bound for success. Professor Brenner is awesome, and he empowered his students to get what they wanted to out of the course...by choice, I spent the whole term learning about sports analytics. Other groups did stock portfolio optimization. Some people looked at the energy sector. The range was broad, and because of this, every group was passionate about their projects.
AM/Ec students have to take this class to get honors, so you may have to take it anyways, but this course is very disorganized. The canvas is full of random assignments with the same name. The teaching staff is also very hard to get a hold of: questions are never answered on Ed and they take over a 6 weeks to grade problem sets. The course itself is not super difficult as long as you have a good background in coding. Class is also not required, so it can be very easy to slip into a routine of not showing up. By the third week, less than a third of the class was showing up

The biggest piece of advice I can give is to get in a good project group from the start (group of 3). If you don't do this, you will get stuck doing the entirety of each of the 3 projects without much recourse. If they fix the disorganization and you get a good project group, this class can be very interesting. You learn modeling in a few very different and interesting contexts (sports, epidemics, traffic, etc.). Overall, recommend with reservations.
I would highly recommend taking Stat 110 and maybe a diff eqs class before this. You can get by without it but you'll have to learn some stuff on your own. Great class, prof Brenner is very enthusiastic and fun to listen to. Can feel a a little unorganized at times but not too bad.
The content was really interesting and there's three opportunities (group projects) to apply the math we learn in class to any real world problem of your choice using any form of math modeling. However, the projects aren’t very well structured because we didn't get substantial feedback/support on our specific projects so it really depends on your group’s effort. Overall, the professors are enthusiastic and passionate about what they teach, but the class was really disorganized. There's an Ed for this class, but course staff answers questions pretty slowly, and it's also really hard to get an email back from professors, and TFs will just tell you to email the professors. The 115 office hours were at awkward times and some 215 TFs would largely deprioritize 115 students who showed up. For psets, as a tip, the Colab lecture notebooks were super helpful for understanding how to use content.
Definitely take this class if you'd like a nice introduction to mathematical modeling with a bit of python involved! It presents a wide breadth of applications––so whether you're interested in sports analytics, stock portfolio optimization, or epidemiology, this course has something to offer for everyone. Psets and projects are chill for the most part––the biggest gripe I have with this course is that the teaching staff is quite disorganized and questions asked over email or Ed often go answered for long periods of time. Prof. Brenner is also quite enthusiastic and entertaining to listen to, but his lectures could have a bit more structure as well.
This course is different from other math courses you will take as it is largely centered around three group projects. The group projects are very rewarding and interesting as you are essentially allowed to explore any topics that you and your group find interesting. However, it is important that you find good partners early on in the course, or you will be left doing a majority of the work in the projects. Aside from the three projects, there are four problem sets, which I found to be difficult and sometimes confusing as the wording was not clear. The dates and schedule for the course were changing quite frequently, and by the end of the semester very few students attended lecture. However, I do believe that if you are willing to put in the work and if the organization of the class itself improves, then it has the potential to be a must take applied math course.
