This course is, frankly, an embarrassment to the Harvard CS program. While the material is both essential and fascinating, it is being held hostage by disorganized instruction and inexcusable sloppiness. I’m a graduate student, and when I reflect on the best courses I’ve taken, I usually favor the ones that have challenged me. This course did challenge me—but for all the wrong reasons.

The course started out promising. After the first lecture, I assumed the harsh Q–reports from previous semesters had been exaggerated. But there were early warning signs: major typos in the homework assignments that required several corrections and derivation mistakes during lectures. Unfortunately, after the midterm, the course completely unraveled. The midterm itself was a mess, and the quality of the lectures sharply declined afterward.

–––––––––––––––––––––––
TL;DR
–––––––––––––––––––––––
This course feels like a slap in the face to Harvard students. It is shocking that it has been allowed to continue in such a dysfunctional state for multiple semesters, despite consistent and negative student feedback. If you take this class, you’ll likely survive it—but you’ll be learning in a disorganized and chaotic environment. It is high time that the CS department intervene.

I would strongly consider taking the course at MIT if you are able to.

–––––––––––––––––––––––
Homework
–––––––––––––––––––––––
Typically, I start homework assignments the day they are released. In this course, I delayed by a week to avoid the initial wave of typos, broken code, and vague or poorly phrased questions. Even waiting didn’t always spare me from those issues.

Content–wise, the homeworks are average. Grading, however, felt inconsistently nitpicky—not the biggest problem in the class.

–––––––––––––––––––––––
Teaching
–––––––––––––––––––––––
It is no wonder that the lectures aren’t recorded. There can’t be video evidence of this slop. Finale is a capable lecturer, but David was an inconsistent mess. His insecurity and incompetence are a large part of what is making this course so frustrating. His motto should be “good catch,” because that is what he says when a student points out a glaring error in his math. At this point, I don’t trust what’s written on the board until I verify it against the textbook myself. I don't fully trust the textbook, but at least it's a concrete thing I can refer to.

It's clear Finale is delegating much of the professorial work to David because of her demanding schedule. It is also clear David is not rising to this challenge.

–––––––––––––––––––––––
Midterm
–––––––––––––––––––––––
The midterm was a disaster. It was far too long, riddled with typos that had to be corrected in real time, and included poorly constructed questions. Nearly everyone I spoke with didn’t finish it in time. I left the exam demoralized.

What was the pedagogical reasoning behind assigning an exam that most students were bound to fail? Was it some kind of hazing ritual? A test of ego? Why not simply assess the material as it was actually taught?

Before the exam, I overheard Finale mention to David that a particular question “really made me think” and “took a while to work through.” I’d ask the professors to reflect on how long it took them to solve that problem—and then consider how long it might take a student seeing it for the first time under strict time pressure. How is that pedagogically sound? What is being accomplished?

–––––––––––––––––––––––
Final Exam
–––––––––––––––––––––––
The final exam was also long and very difficult. I read the entire textbook, reworked every problem, and reviewed the lectures multiple times—and still received a poor grade. At least the time limit wasn’t as much of a constraint as during the midterm.

–––––––––––––––––––––––
Final Thoughts
–––––––––––––––––––––––
As of now, final grades have not been released. Without a curve, my grade is around 75%, despite earning A–level marks on all non–exam material. I genuinely have no idea what my final grade will be, because the course team has failed to communicate that information. That lack of transparency reflects a broader disrespect toward students.
Unfortunately, this class ended up being one of the most frustrating experiences I’ve had at Harvard so far. I genuinely struggled to take away much from the semester, and I often felt overwhelmed, not because the material itself was particularly difficult, but because of how it was presented. The lectures were often confusing and hard to follow. Compared to other CS classes like CS124, the structure of this course felt disorganized, and it was difficult to figure out what we were actually supposed to take away from each class. I found it challenging to follow some of the lecture notation and derivations, which often shifted mid–problem without clear explanation, so much that students regularly had to interrupt to clarify what was being written. It made it really difficult to take consistent notes or develop a solid understanding of the core concepts.

The textbook, on the other hand, was incredibly clear and well–written. I only started reading it during reading period before the final, and it was like a whole new world opened up. Honestly, if I had known how helpful it was earlier, I would have relied on it from the beginning instead of attending the 9:45AM lecture (which aren't even recorded). If you do take this class, I highly recommend using the textbook as your main resource. I really think this course has the potential to be so much better. The content is interesting and valuable, but the delivery needs some refinement. I hope future iterations can take better advantage of the strong textbook and aim for greater clarity and consistency in lecture.
