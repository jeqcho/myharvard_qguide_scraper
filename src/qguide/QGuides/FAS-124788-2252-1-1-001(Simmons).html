This is a class that deserves the Harvard name. It has seriously set my standards for every other class. My think is substantially clearer. (I will be changing my concentration as a result)

I am not exaggerating: if you want to *learn* here, take this class.

The reason is two–fold: the nature of the topic and the class itself.

THE TOPIC

We have NOT solved what these early modern writers were discussing. We have, in fact, made little progress. We've tried to ignore metaphysical questions as if they cease becoming relevant. But ignoring something does not make it go away, and serious problems with current scientific research and the general "misinformation" and extreme political polarization can be traced back to this. It's insane and fascinating.

The writers in this class invite you into the conversation. The works of the early modern period are IDEAL for beginners because a) you'll need to know something about Aristotle for any of them be intelligible (+1 classic!) b) much of what they started continues today (+1 modern!). It's a 3 for 1 deal.

You WILL see the same ideas discussed now, even if using different language (it's about the *substance* not the *word* people!). Things will make more sense, you will be able to *formulate your own thoughts* on them and assess where we simply do not know the answer. (what is a scientific "law"?)


THE CLASS


1) Teaching approach. Hands down.
– Working through the arguments of the text. This is so important. We learn the PROCESS the writers used and NOT "Oh Descartes though this". When you go through the actual steps, the conclusion stop appearing mystical or like the result of revelations—though they still remain impressive. Finally: the historical context and the "raw materials" they were working with makes it click into place. I can confidently reconstruct most of the arguments we tackled now (which will make me very fun at parties).
– Questions in class: Prof Simmons would ASK THE STUDENTS TO CLARIFY THEIR QUESTIONS so she answer it meaningfully and have students engaged in a conversation (wild!). We use clear terms (insane!) She was also able to judge when a question would steer us off the main topic too much.
– Relevant section topics. People were not just talking past each other (a miracle!).
– Format Prof. Simmons using chalk is the right move. We are actually thinking through ideas instead of going through a prepared presentation.
– I guarantee every other response raves about Prof. Simmons, so—yes, what they said.


2) Workload
– Short assignments: these were the perfect balance of challenging and doable. The key part is that they had PURPOSE. The canvas page clearly states what skill each assignment targeted, and it was clear to judge where you fell short and see improvement throughout the semester. Many of my friends complain about busywork or vague assignments where they feel lost, this class is free of that.
– The questions for the final are released beforehand so you can prepare them together. Great discussions
– The amount of reading is very manageable. There mix of required (og works) vs optional reading (secondary sources)! You do have to actually read the original works (if you don't want this bit, do not take the class. Full stop.)

3) THE OTHER STUDENTS <

This class attracts curious interesting smart people who actually want to be there. I have formed the most actual friendships out of this class than any other, to the point where altered the trajectory of my social life here. We don't have shallow conversations and respect each other's ideas. It's great.
