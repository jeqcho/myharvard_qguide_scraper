TAKE THIS COURSE!!!! You can learn a ton and do very well regardless of whether you are studying CS or applied math or classics—just ask for help when you need it (there are TFs everywhere and office hours happening all the time, and you can go to any section you want each week) and don’t fall too far behind (though I knew people who were multiple weeks behind at some point and were still able to catch up).

Class content/what you’ll be doing: The class largely focuses on learning various distributions—such as the normal, exponential, binomial, hypergeometric, poisson distributions—and how to interpret/use them in stories and math. You’ll learn the story of a distribution and also the math functions associated with it. For example, a random variable X that represents the number of times a fair coin lands head in 5 tosses is distributed by the binomial distribution—and you’ll use this fact in lots of creative ways to understand how to find out lots of things about that X. You might need to find its mean, or its variance, or you might not know the distribution of X at all but you can still describe it with the story of the distributions, or find other parameters of the variable.

Importantly, the first two weeks are rather distinct from the rest of the semester (counting is important to understand, but it’s very different from the other things you’ll actually be doing, even if they’re technically built on counting). Some say the first two weeks are the hardest, some say they’re the easiest—I think counting is just a different idea from the probability stuff you do later which is more about creativity in math and problem solving.

Tips:
1) Skim some of the problems in the chapter of the textbook that your homework is on. The book, generally, is really really good (and actually is relatively fast to read and understand, all things considered).
2) Go to office hours, especially in the first few weeks, and work on the homework with other students and a TF around—strategize how you’ll tackle problems and then just do it.
3) Have faith in yourself! Nothing is unlearnable, every problem can be solved, and almost half of all problems (especially in the second half) have quick solutions where you might not have to integrate a big ugly thing—you can just use tricks based on knowing probability rules. (You’ll solve math problems but because they’re about probability–related variables, all the probability–related properties of those variables can help you solve it.) When you think a problem is hard, know that it’s not impossible and you can solve it—when you’re in the second half of the class and you look back at an earlier question that stumped you in week 2 or 3, you’ll think it’s relatively easy.
4) Take the practice tests, as many as you can. do NOT need to take all of them since you wont really have time, just take as many as you can and it’ll help–by far the best way to spend study time.

I tend to be dissuaded when I read reports that say a class is a horrible, stressful experience that will painfully forge you into being more able to problem solve—and I don’t think that fits this class. I think it’s a difficult class, but likely manageable if you find what works for you, and you will certainly learn a lot and probably see the world differently and have better confidence in your creative problem solving!

Professor Blitzstein is a great teacher too and the TFs/sections are all super helpful.

Having said all this, there are many reasons not to take this class—if you have major extracurricular obligations or other classes you might be nervous about or just really don’t like the subject material (and don’t need it for a major) then it doesn’t make much sense to put yourself through it. Also, if you try it out for a couple weeks and hate it, then dropping it might totally make sense before you’re stuck with it.

Best of luck!
