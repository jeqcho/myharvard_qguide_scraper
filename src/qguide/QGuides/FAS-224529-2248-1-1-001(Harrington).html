Comments
Please take this course––a structured think tank on identifying and excavating spaces through which we may study consciousness. It's a wonderful cornucopia of thoughtful interdisciplinary, real–world perspectives. The assignments were very self–directed and left so much room for personal interest and exploration. Professor Harrington is just as thoughtful as she is brilliant and eloquent. What a true treat this was! I will think about this course for a long time.
A lot of reading. Very rewarding class. Can be transformative if you let it be. Professor knows everything and everyone––super nice, very encouraging and warm.
I deeply encourage students from all backgrounds and concentrations to enroll in future iterations of this course! I valued my experience interrogating consciousness debates through the lens of historical consciousness movements and the clear, persistent stakes of these studies in the clinical encounter and the social world. This is a course that encouraged collaborative analysis and critical, generative thinking. This is a course that also positioned patient voices, experiences, realities, and adversities at equal value if not more than removed, academic perspective on consciousness. The exciting scope of our intellectual lectures/topic orientations, discussion exercises, and debates in class are necessary for students from all Departments and pre–professional backgrounds. We gave great attention to medical ethics and vulnerable constructions around what we *think* we know about the brain, the body, and such an entity called consciousness.
This is a great class to both get an introduction to a variety of different spaces related to consciousness and to dig into an area of interest.
The class subject, its presentation, and class discussions are extremely engaging. Professor Harrington is a truly inspiring teacher and provides wonderful insight into the subjectone of my most meaningful classes.
This is an excellent course!! It really allows you to engage with different spaces where consciousness can be discussed, and develop the ability to engage with other students on class material to sharpen understanding. It being an upper–level course means you can engage with students from other Harvard schools and they have valuable input to offer! The assignments were also low stakes but very engaging and really allowed for creative control over what you wanted to get out of the course.
I loved this class. You learn to think in a different way and have the freedom to change your mind on difficult topics!
Professor Harrington is an incredible, dedicated professor! I really enjoyed this class and felt that it increased by ability to think beyond the realms of everything I knew to be true! Of course, a three hour class is always difficult to maintain constant attention in, but it really helped me think about conciousness differently!
This class will not teach you what consciousness is (and it will hardly even ask that question). I felt most engaged when I thought of it as visiting a variety of spaces and seeing what talk of the mind was like in those spaces. It's sometimes most helpful to suspend judgment about the talk itself, and just focus on characterizing it.
Take this class, Professor Harrington is the best.
There are few opportunities in life like the one you have in taking this course. The topic is important from multiple angles, yet such examinations of consciousness are hard to come by. It's important because it's about who, why, and what we are, all questions one is bound to confront in life. Additionally, there is broader applicability to understanding how scientific methods differ across disciplines to tackle a hard problem that doesn't fall neatly into a single one.
One of the most life–changing classes I think I have ever taken. I genuinely came in one person and walked out another. Professor Harrington is the absolute best and she designed this class so well. She has perfectly picked out settings in which to examine consciousness. There are a couple of hours of readings/videos per week, but a lot of them are actually very enjoyable to read/watch. The blogs (500–700 words and you only need 6–7) are a great way to recap what you just talked about. This isn't a class that is going to give you a concrete definition of consciousness, but rather one which will open your mind to so many different possibilities and simply allow you to appreciate life. You will talk about consciousness on drugs, in the ICU/hospice, in sleep, in AI, and in so many more.
