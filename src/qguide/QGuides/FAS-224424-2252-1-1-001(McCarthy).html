This is an overly ambitious class. I appreciate what it was trying to do, but the syllabus needs some serious paring down. With only one or two lectures dedicated to each novel, each weaving in theory and other sources, it was impossible to look at any of the material closely. It often felt like lecture focused very little on the text at hand, but more on related theories or texts we hadn’t been assigned. Sections also felt overwhelming—how can we possibly discuss both a film and a novel in one hour? And section discussions often felt stifled by students' lack of closeness with the material. I felt like I was constantly racing to keep up, only for us not to talk about what I was racing to watch or read, which started to feel disappointing. Professor McCarthy is no doubt a phenomenal lecturer and a brilliant academic, but I would have loved to learn more in depth and less in breadth from him. I think it has the potential to be a wonderful course in the future.

Take this course! You will not regret it. Not only will you get to read a deeply interesting set of novels –– and watch a deeply interesting set of films –– but you will also get to know Professor McCarthy, who is a fantastic lecturer and is highly accessible to his students. Speaking as a graduating senior who had never taken a course in the English department, I highly recommend this course to anyone who wants to "dip their toes" in the world of the novel (and learn some literary theory!). As are his materials, Professor McCarthy's lectures are consistently engaging; I left every lecture feeling like I had learned something that would stick with me. Plus, Professor McCarthy infuses his syllabi and lectures with pop culture, which makes every class session even more interesting.
TL;DR ––– If you are interested in delving more into specific types of critical theory, with a special focus toward the anticapitalist progressive lens, then this course is for you (and I believe you will sincerely enjoy it!). If you are hoping for a more diverse set of opinions or a wider critical exploration––– or trying to warm yourself up to critical theory, as I was––– do NOT take this course.

The best advice I can give regarding this course is "Know thyself." I entered this course knowing I didn't usually like critical theory, but hoping that Prof. McCarthy's excellent pedagogy would change my mind or at least teach me how to engage well with this sort of criticism. Unfortunately, I was wrong, and by the end of the semester I sincerely wished I had not taken the course.

The reading load was a bit heavy some weeks (though light during others), especially when having to watch long films each week. Though they offered screenings of some films, these screenings were incompatible with my class schedule, and the teaching staff never asked for student availability, so there was no chance to rectify this. However, the midterm was fun to write, and otherwise the workload was very light. We read two novels that quickly jumped to the top of my favorites list, but also two which are firmly at the bottom. I enjoyed some of the films but not others. Lectures were engaging, and section was also enjoyable.

The problems I had with the course are mostly problems I have with critical theory in general. Lectures presented extremely narrow, rigid critical views of the novels we discussed, often in ways that, in my view, severely stunted further discussion or analysis of the works. (This was despite Prof. McCarthy saying, on the first day of class, that he hoped to remain apolitical and would welcome open dialogue.) Much time was devoted to specific recent literary theory, but comparatively little time was spent on other modes of reading and understanding the texts, even when we read older works that would have benefited from at least passing references to other critical modes. In my section, the TF attempted to broaden the conversation, but ultimately the lecture content was too distracting.

Some of these problems are certainly due to the makeup of students in the class, as the vast majority clearly held very similar views about capitalism. But even in this case I think the teaching staff/course reading list could have been structured in a way to stimulate conversation: when all the critical texts on the syllabus advocate for specific views, it's impossible to expect students to engage with alternative views. Furthermore, even if one wants to center anticapitalist arguments, there are many conservative anticapitalist arguments that would have been well worth exploring.
