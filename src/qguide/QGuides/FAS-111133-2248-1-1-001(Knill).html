This course is great for students who have come out of the 21a/b sequence and are just generally curious about differential geometry. With that being said, it's not a rigorous introduction to differential geometry. If you want something more rigorous I'd recommend the grad version or the one at MIT. I was expecting it to be a more proof–based so I'm a bit sad about that. We also worked almost exclusively in Euclidean space, which isn't great for me as I'm a physics concentrator who wanted to take this class in preparation for taking General Relativity. Oliver is also super passionate about graph theory and spent a lot of time on that which personally felt irrelevant to me and the course, but the problems on the psets were kinda cool.

It's a bit sad because I feel like we didn't get the standard course where we learn from day 1 the definition of tensors, manifolds, etc. Instead we did a lot of calculation and plugging in formulas. It's cool and the course felt a bit on the lighter side but I was wishing I'd get a more rigorous introduction to such a cool topic. I say this with a bit of reservation because Oliver is such a sweet and passionate teacher. He really means well and I think he might've tried too hard to make the class accessible to students of all math backgrounds.

I liked the final project component of the course –– allowed me to explore something I'm more interested in. Overall, it was a cool course and I think I would've liked it more if he just stuck to a more traditional teaching method.
Oliver Knill is a godsend! He is such a fantastic professor, and was the true highlight of this course. I do think that Math 112 is necessary for this class, or at least math beyond 101. The assignments and midterm were both very fair given what we had learned, and the course staff was great! If Knill teaches again, this is definitely one of the best 130s in the math department!
Keep up with the problem sets, and you will be fine.
Oliver is a great guy and very knowledgeable and enthusiastic. The course itself is not overly structured and the psets are just simple computations. Maybe good as a survey course but not so much for an in–depth exploration of diff geo
Oliver is fun and diff geo is cool, it's all about the prof when you're choosing math classes.
Oliver Knill is a lovely instructor and human being. He is passionate and makes you love the subject. But there is real risk that you may not learn anything too relevant to the topic.
The class is pretty good and Oliver makes it very enjoyable. It’s relatively easy and not too much work. The content is also pretty interesting.
Oliver is one of the most engaging professors you will ever encounter. His enthusiasm for math and his passion for spreading that enthusiasm is infectious, making you genuinely want to learn the content. However, this strategy falls short if I feel I have not gained any rigorous understanding of differential geometry after an entire semester taking his class! I gained a lot of perspective on math history and applications of the course content, which are both awesome, but I still do not feel I have a good sense of what differential geometry actually is. That is unfortunate. I still would recommend this class for the Oliver experience, but if you're looking for rigor, this class will not deliver.
course was both enjoyable and unfulfilling. oliver is an amazing lecturer and very fun individual, he had great stories and plenty of fun asides. that being said, the course approached the material in a roundabout manner, often using unconventional notation and introducing ideas in an unorthodox and therefore less clear way. many of the homeworks were very computational to the point of not further illuminating the content –– many times we were asked to use mathematica code provided by the course (which doesn't teach mathematica), but it didn't work. exams were not very rigorous. overall, there were many fun moments in this course, but it is probably more enlightening to take the grad version of diff geo or physics courses that cover the content.
This is a quirky class, taught by Oliver, who is a great person and super enthusiastic, but a bit unclear in his teaching. Overall, it is a great way to fulfill the 130 level requirement for the math concentration.

Oliver:

Course:
– the version with oliver was less theoretical than previous versions of what i know

Very VERY numerical approach to differential geometry, so if this is with knill don't expect a whole lot of proofs (although there's maybe one on each homework). If it's not I hear it's very different though, so also be weary of just looking at qscores of this year.

This is definitely a great class to take to fulfill the 130s requirement. Oliver is also very sweet and is very enthusiastic about the material, which I really appreciated about this course.
*** I think anyone who places into 22/25/55 should take this class freshman fall since it is so motivated by what is covered in each of those classes and isn’t terrifically hard. I also absolutely love Oliver Knill every class he works so hard to make everyone excited and enthused. Homeworks are extremely good and take 4–8 hours. Lectures are engaging and you should NOT skip them since Oliver is so Oliver. And the quizzes were really helpful, honestly Oliver’s pedagogy is like top notch and he knows exactly what he’s doing. That said I did feel like I lost a bit of understanding in the second half of the class perhaps just because everything became much harder in all of my classes.

I have two reservations about this class: Oliver’s notation and his use of Mathematica. Sometimes I literally had no idea what Oliver was asking for on the homeworks without examining his notes from when he taught 22b. His notation and names for things are not very standard across the internet and other math textbooks which cost me a lot of time doing the homeworks. Additionally Mathematica is a horrible program that no one should use. It is weird, hard to work with, and frequently doesn’t compile.

That said everyone should take this class, when puskar taught it it was excellent when Oliver taught it it was also excellent. Enough has been said!
