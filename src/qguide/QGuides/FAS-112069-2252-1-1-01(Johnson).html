What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Absolutely take this course. (Yes, I know it's required for US history PhD students, but for those not required the recommendation stands.) Professor Suarez is helpful, knowledgeable, and an exemplary member of Harvard's history department.
This was an excellent class for cultivating historiographic background!
I didn't realize how greatly this class would impact me. Because I don't specialize in 20th Century US History, I assumed I'd learn more about theories and methods that I can apply to my own work—and to be sure, I did—but I thought that would be the extent of the class's enduring mark on me as a scholar. Instead, the patterns and theories we discussed (especially Gramsci), as well as the in–the–weeds–content, helped me better understand the communities I engage with for public/activist work, as well as my own family. Before this class, I often felt frustrated when trying to understand their views and unable to communicate effectively. But when Professor Suarez and Johnson so thoughtfully helped us walk through the 1910s through present, I suddenly see my own family and community in the changing contours of political thought. As a result, I've been able to communicate with them more effectively and also engage with advocacy in a more meaningful way. This course changed my life and is really going to stick with me going forward.
This course provides an excellent introduction to what historians have written about the 20th century United States history. The readings each week offered entrypoints into the major debates in the field, and over the course of the semester we learned not only how historians think about these subjects but also the stakes of those debates—and even what actually happened (not always the case in a course dedicated to historiography!).
