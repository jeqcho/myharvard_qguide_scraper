Be ready to learn a lot about the beauty of chemistry.
This class is excellent in every way and is probably the best chem class Harvard offers. Jacobsen's lectures are both thorough and engaging and do a great job of tying in recent developments in the field, and beyond that Jacobsen is just a fantastic professor to study under (I highly recommend finding time to chat with him outside of class in some capacity). If you took Chem 20/30, the course content is quite manageable, since most lectures just build on topics from those courses with a deeper level of analysis. The exams are brutal, however, especially the final, which includes both a take–home and a three–hour in–person exam. I'd say it's worth the difficulty, especially if you're interested in chemistry research as a career.
This class is difficult but its so worth it! I learned an incredible amount and feel like I gained a deeper understanding of all the mechanics behind organic chemistry. Eric is a wonderful professor and clearly cares a lot about the subject matter. Sections are also great and help you practice reading papers, which I didnt have that much experience in. Overall, highly recommend and do not shy away even though its a difficult class!
A highlight of the department's course offerings. Toughest organic reasoning in the department, but succeeding is not harder in principle than a course like Chem 17/20/30. Prof. Jacobsen is present for section discussions, which is a brilliant opportunity to discuss the literature and the field with an unbelievably experienced researcher.
Its very hard but you can get a lot out of it. Its worth it if you want a challenge.
This is a dense, thought–provoking course that probes the boundaries of the field of organic chemistry today. The instructor is passionate about these topics and his research and it shows during the lectures. Be prepared to be challenged by the coursework and the exams. However, the knowledge gained is worth the time put in.
Truly a great course. There is a lot of information that gets conveyed throughout the semester, but it is not often these specific examples that matter, but the principles behind them. If you can recognize this, the content becomes much more accessible, and does not often require too much stress.
CHEM 105 will change your life!
1) During section, we read some seminal papers that gave me not only an understanding of the open problems in organic chemistry, but helped me go from skimming papers to extract procedures for synthesis, to being able to critically think about the of their mechanistic proposals and data. Also, thinking about kinetics, rate laws, and catalytic cycles over and over in this class ended up helping me troubleshoot my reactions in the lab in a much greater level of depth.

2) If CHEM 20/30 has convinced you that reactions are named reaction from one pattern to another, 105 will give you the flexibility needed to think through the mechanism for just about any transformation. In lecture, the emphasis on generating complexity in just a few cascade steps gave me a sense for the "flow" needed to see disconnections that might not be intuitive from undergraduate classes. The only caveat is that arrow–pushing is tested on the midterm, but only becomes a focus of the class afterwards, so I would brush up beforehand.

3) Not only is Eric incredibly knowledgable and passionate about the material, but he has also personally witnessed/contributed to so much of the research covered in this class that you gain an appreciation for the evolution of the field and the process of making these discoveries. His open office hours were one of the highlights of my week, and talking to him about the course material, research or things entirely unrelated to chemistry was both approachable and enlightening.
Take this class seriously, especially if you are a graduate student planning on doing any type of organic chemistry research. While it may be hard to motivate yourself to study for a graduate class (since grades aren't as important as in undergrad), I feel that taking the time to study content in this course will be useful for your graduate career in the long–run.
There is a large variety of material in this class, so its important to not get behind. Discussion section is really fun!
