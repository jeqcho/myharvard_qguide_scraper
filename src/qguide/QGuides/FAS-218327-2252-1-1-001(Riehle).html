I took this course with only general knowledge of Byzantine history but having studied Latin and history. I would say having some more familiarity with Byzantine history will augment your experience with the class but it's not necessary to have that knowledge to get a lot out of the class. This course is great for thinking about identity as a whole and in the Byzantine context as well and both taught me new things about Byzantium and opened up more questions for me about our current time.
TAKE YOUR TIME ON YOUR PAPER, THAT MEANS START EARLY
Dimiter and Alex are both wonderful. Best ratio of reading length to discussion quality. Twenty page paper isn't too scary, and they give you ample scaffolding.
Take it if you have an interest or are curious otherwise it will probably be a drag. Otherwise it's great. Good for students of any period of history as it gives you the tools to talk about personal identity and worldview and epistemology and metaphysics for people of very different backgrounds to us.
Fantastic course for the medievally inclined, but maybe not so introductory as the course page might suggest: it's worth taking another course on Byzantium with Profs. Angelov or Riehle prior to this one to get full value out of it.
Even if you have absolutely no prior knowledge of Medieval Greek/Byzantine history, you should take this course –– you will engage with surprising and illuminating authors and texts that you will otherwise probably never encounter, and you will be able to do so in a highly engaging, constructive, and appropriately challenging environment.
Take this class if you are interested in Byzantine society and want to read a lot of primary sources. The readings aren't excessively heavy, but it is imperative to read them closel to get the most out of the class. You should come to class prepared to discuss what you found interesting.
