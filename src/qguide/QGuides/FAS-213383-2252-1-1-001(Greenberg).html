What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
This is, at its core, a philosophy of social science class. It’s incredibly helpful if you’re trying to situate your thesis within one or more disciplines, and it pushes you to think much more deeply about what a piece of scholarship is doing—and why. It helped me appreciate and critique academic work more effectively, because every method has its limitations, and once you understand a scholar’s approach and rationale, it opens up an entirely new way of reading. After taking this class, I felt much better equipped to identify the kind of intervention a scholar is making in the literature. The process of developing a research question into a full prospectus also offered a phenomenal roadmap for framing and structuring research of any kind, and is absolutely essential for your thesis.
The best part of this course is really building the foundation and skills for your senior thesis. Along the way, you will also read some very interesting theorist who will challenge and expand your mind. Definitely recommend for any Social Studies students looking to write a theory paper.
If I could tell 60 students one thing about this course, it's to choose a thesis topic BEFORE enrolling. All of the assignments are geared towards developing your thesis (topic, method, advisor, etc), so if you aren't settled on, or have a good idea of, your topic before starting, the assignments won't be useful for you. For those choosing between 50 and 60, this course is almost entirely theory/history, so if you're planning on doing any sort of empirical component, definitely choose 50!
This class is one of the required methods classes for Social Studies, the other option being 50. The syllabus was helpful for getting a broad sense of important questions for historical/theoretical research (what does it mean to explain something? How do you write a satisfying history of something? What should objectivity look like in social science research? Etc.) and main strains of thought in political theory and intellectual history. Given such a broad project, I think they paced the syllabus relatively well, even though their choices to "represent" a particular kind of theory (eg feminist political theory) were sometimes odd or random to me.

The two instructors varied in their approach to odd effect. Harpham was fond of cold–calling and would go off into digressions/spend a lot of time making redundant points. Greenberg usually covered more of the material and seemed to have more of a plan but was quite pedantic in her approach. Grading for the class assignments (which were basically scaffolding for a thesis prospectus) also depended heavily on which instructor handled your work.

In general, I think 60 did as well as a methods class with so broad a scope could do and I found the readings and assignments genuinely useful. I came out of it with a much better handle on the concrete work that goes into a thesis, and I refined my question and approach quite a bit, which I suppose is the goal of a methods class. It was just a bit of a baffling experience sometimes.
This course is great! You're exposed to some great authors, work on assignments that help your senior thesis take shape, and have terrific mentors in Dr. Harpham and Dr. Greenberg.

However, I'd tell future students that this isn't a course solely devoted to hammering out the details of your senior thesis, nor is it merely a course in theoretical and historical research methods. These two projects operate in parallel, and careful thought is required to integrate the two such that they inform each other. Class meetings are more devoted to discussing the course material (e.g., methodological debates, interpreting readings, etc.). Office hours with your instructors will be more helpful in having the course content bear meaningfully on the direction of your senior thesis.

Because this course is made up of fellow Social Studies concentrators, I'd encourage students to learn about their classmates' projects, the research methods they'll employ, and the topics they're curious about. It'll broaden your intellectual horizons and make for a more fun semester!
This is a great course! Drs. Greenberg and Harpham are fantastic instructors who bring a lot of liveliness to class discussions. The readings can be difficult/take a lot of time but they definitely understand that they can be a struggle. The best part of this course is that it is excellent preparation for your thesis, as each assignment will truly force you to make exponential progress on your thesis research.
