Do not take this class if you don’t have to. Seriously, avoid it at all costs. Do not take it as an elective unless your idea of a good time is sitting in the back of the room, doing online shopping, or counting ceiling tiles to stay awake—because the lectures are ABSOLUTELY HORRIBLE. Dr. Chen relies entirely on her PowerPoint slides, reads straight from them, and stutters so much that it’s hard to follow what she’s saying sometimes. It feels like she’s just winging it half the time, and when she runs out of material (which happens often), she turns the class into an open discussion to fill time.
But here's the thing: nobody really talks. Except for one student—literally one kid—who somehow becomes the center of attention. And she lets him go on for like 15 minutes straight, every time. Don’t get me wrong, I love that kid and I think he’s literally a genius. But at that point, I’d honestly rather he teach the class. At least he sounds like he knows what he’s talking about. The rest of us are just stuck there, zoning out and praying for it to end.
It’s a complete waste of time, and you won’t learn anything useful. I will give credit to the interesting reading, but that is literally a textbook you can purchase everywhere. Go get it and read through it yourself. Save yourself and take something—anything—else.
I was not a fan of this class. Lectures were often just about the readings and felt a little redundant. However, the topics themselves are pretty interesting, and you can get a lot out of your paper–writing experience by fully thinking through your arguments. Professor Chen is also very nice
only take it if you're serious about the content
If you are genuinely interested in the subject I would definitely recommend this course – Prof. Chen is great, very accessible and clearly very engaged in the subject, and the atmosphere of discussion is great. The readings are very manageable in length but very dense, so it feels like you gain a lot from very little reading. The assignments are quite reasonable, just short exercises for each lecture and 3 essays.
This field has always been interesting to me so it was nice to get the introduction to it. Good starting place.
Class is fun if you enjoy philosophy. Lecture can drag on and not super informative. Workload is extremely manageable if you plan around the three essays that aren't too long. Section is really informative and prompts interesting discussion
The readings are incredibly interesting, and thinking through them genuinely made me a much better thinker. That being said, lectures sometimes move quite slowly, and it can be a challenge to stay engaged unless you are incredibly interested in the material and/or willing to participate.
The readings for this course were very interesting. The pacing of the lectures was often off; they become sidetracked a lot.
If you're at all interested in philosophy of mind, or have ever wondered "what am I?", "why am I conscious?", "what is consciousness?" etc., you should take this course. The readings and sections are the highlight. You should be prepared to fully do every reading, partially because you will have to answer a response question for homework, but mostly because you will take away a lot from them. You should also go into section ready to discuss and debate. There are three six page papers, which aren't particularly bad, but certainly make the class not a gem. Lectures can be a little boring (often just rehashing the readings), but the rest of the course makes up for the relatively uninteresting lectures.
not sure I would recommend thisclass, interesting subject matter but lectures are pretty boring and mostly just recap readings. Sections were very helpful, though. The papers weren’t too bad – three six–pagers, which felt much less stressful than having one big final paper.

If you are interested in this topic and philosophy, especially the metaphysical side, this is a great course.
