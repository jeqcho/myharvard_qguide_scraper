This class is quite easy if you have any experience with Calc 2/Calc BC or equivalent, and completely manageable if you only have Calc 1/Calc AB experience. I self–studied Calc BC in high school and placed into 21a but was nervous about starting there as I didn't feel I had a strong enough foundation. If this is you, don't do what I did. You're probably fine just going to 21a if you need it for degree. If you don't, by all means take 1b. But, for me the class felt like a lot of review.

If you are taking this class, here is my general experience. Midterms/Finals are a lot easier than the practice tests but follow the same sort of structure. So, if you feel comfortable with the practices, you'll likely do well on the exam. I didn't really utilize MQC or office hours, but I do have friends who found them super helpful. Even if you're solid on the material, it still really helps to get a PSET buddy who will help catch your silly mistakes. Also, make sure you mesh with your TF's teaching style. I had Jonier and he and the CAs for my section were really good. But if you don't feel engaged or like you're learning with your TF, switch to a different time/section. All the learning is small–section based so it's essential you feel comfortable in the class and with the instruction team.
This class will be hard, mainly because of the different structure compared to high school. It's more of self–guided learning, especially at the beginning. Get a PSet buddy –– this will help A LOT.
this course is very hard and moves incredibly quickly. everything builds on itself so please if you are confused about something, go to OH asap.
This class is a great course if you need to complete a math requirement and have taken all the necessary courses leading up to calc 2. I will say, the course will definitely be easier if you took Calc BC in high school, but it is not entirely the same. The type of learning experienced in this class is very different than my high school math classes. Rather than simple memorization, this course made sure we understood the foundations of calculus and knew where formulas originated from or why we followed the processes we did.
I would recommend this course if you need it for a concentration or for pre–med requirements or honors. If you want to fulfill your quantitative requirement, find an easier course. I would tell students to GO TO OFFICE HOURS AND MQC. I would always do my homework in office hours even if there wasn't a TF there so that I could focus, which I recommend. It also makes sure that if you have a small computational question that pretty much any TF can answer, you can just get it answered. Go to office hours as early as possible and just crank out the pset as close to the end of class as possible so that you don't have to worry about the day/night before it is due when everyone is at office hours or MQC. Go to MQC on the day you get your pset because on the night before it is due, you won't even be able to find a place to sit. Also, finishing your psets in office hours/MQC kind of helps you guarantee that you get it all right and then you don't have to go and try to get it checked (which they can't really do anyway). It also helps you just stay on top of your work and get a better understanding of the material. So I highly highly recommend office hours and MQC. Also, do all the practice tests and go over/redo questions from practice tests and problem sets that you missed when you're studying for tests. I highly recommend replicating the test process for at least two practice tests and I recommend going over every question, and especially the questions you don't understand, in office hours or MQC on the practice tests if you want to get a good grade. I would also say that not every TF is made equal. If your TF doesn't work for you (I had a great TF so I didn't have to do this), then try to switch to a TF whose teaching style works better for you because that can really affect how you're able to learn and how you'll do in the class.
Make sure you really put in the effort into understanding the material as you go, and ask questions!
Go to office hours. Psets take a long time, especially at the beginning of the year. They would take at least 5 hours at the beginning of the year, but then they get more manageable, and you take at least 1 – 3 hours on them.
be prepared to work hard, start your psets the day they are assigned, utilize MQC, ask questions in class because material moves fast
This class is a perfect freshman fall class. It may sound like a lot (3 meetings per week, three PSets per week) but the teaching team's only goal is to facilitate your learning.
This class is an extremely well–taught class. There are 3 psets a week so it takes a bit of time. I do recommend this class, however.

Three psets a week is rough. If you are debating between this and 21 and above, go for 21 and above. I wish I did. The class was good, and the professor was great! It's just the amount of time you have to dedicate to this class that is insane.

You get like 4 practice exams for the midterms so if you do them seriously you should be fine. I really loved this class– it made me realize math is so much more than regurgitating techniques– there's creativity and real problem–solving involved.

If you do not like math (I despise it) I can still see the benefit in this class.
