Every lecture and section came with dozens of pages of dense reading, plus multiple worksheets that could be tested at any time. These weren't light, supplemental readings either—they were essential to understanding the material and had to be memorized in detail. Once my departmental courses ramped up, I simply couldn't keep up. It was overwhelming and demoralizing.

It’s hard not to feel like this course was symptomatic of deeper problems in the Gen Ed program. The disconnect between the passion and clarity of the instructor and the actual learning experience speaks volumes. This class demanded far more time and stress than it was worth. I came in genuinely curious about the topic, and I left exhausted, discouraged, and frustrated.

I really don’t understand why the College is trying to intensify the Gen Ed program at a time when so many undergraduates are already stretched thin—balancing full course loads, research, jobs, internships, and extracurriculars... on top of everything that is happening in the world and at the university right now. The idea of Gen Eds is supposed to be about broadening our horizons, exposing us to new ways of thinking, and encouraging intellectual curiosity outside our major. But instead, they’re becoming another source of academic burnout.

There is a way to make Gen Eds rigorous and meaningful without overwhelming students or turning them into yet another box to check under duress. A well–designed Gen Ed should challenge students intellectually without competing with the courses that define their actual academic and career paths. When a general education course begins to demand more work, more time, and more emotional energy than departmental classes, something has gone seriously wrong.

The College seems to be clinging to an outdated ideal of the "well–rounded scholar" while ignoring the reality of student life today. Yes, we want to learn across disciplines—but not at the expense of our well–being, or at the cost of disengaging from the courses that matter most to our goals. At some point, insisting that students struggle through arbitrarily intense Gen Eds just to prove some institutional point becomes counterproductive—and frankly, tone–deaf. If the College really wants students to embrace learning beyond their concentrations, then the Gen Ed program needs to earn that enthusiasm—not demand it through excessive workloads and opaque grading. Right now, it feels like the College has forgotten that most of us want to learn and is punishing us through the recent revisions in the Gen Eds.
