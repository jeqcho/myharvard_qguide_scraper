Parts of this class were amazing. I found the material super interesting if you are at all science–inclined (looking at you pre–meds) or even want to question how academic material is communicated to people without a highly educated background. We also did a ton of museum trips and speaker visits, which were all very low–stress, purely interest–fulfilling outings. I especially loved the museum trips because we got a behind–the–scenes view of Harvard, which was great for my first semester here. Emilie Raymer is an absolutely wonderful and sweet human being. She was super accommodating when people were sick or needed extensions or anything of the sort. Overall, this class was much easier than most of my friends' Expos. The final paper, being a capstone project, also came in quite handy during finals season.

Some cons of this class: the reading was super heavy at the beginning (upwards of 200+ pages in two days), and it was challenging for me to learn how to read efficiently as someone who didn't have a lot of long, academic reading experience in high school. Additionally, with the pro of it being a relatively easy class, there was the negative that it didn't push me to become a better writer. We didn't do a ton (if any) of additional assignments or receive additional feedback besides our essay drafts. While this meant less graded work overall, it also meant I didn't really note any improvements in my writing overall. So if you're looking for a low–stress, interesting Expos and already feel confident in your writing abilities, this might be the one for you.
I would tell future students that this is a fantastic, fun class with a wonderful, hardworking and vivacious professor who truly goes above and beyond to make the class' activities interactive (in this class, you can expect field trips to the Art Museum, guest speakers/authors of the readings, documentary watches and donuts). The readings can feel overwhelming in the beginning especially for those, like myself, who are not used to reading 100+ pages of reading per week. However, fear not–it is not as intimidating as it sounds because everyone else is going through a similar adjustment period and my Prof. was very understanding. Moreover, all of the readings are intentional to cultivate the lively discussions that you will long remember after the course and to craft nuanced essays! I still remember the discussion that my Expos section had about the best way to present HIV/AIDS information through graphics as we compared numerous images from real life campaigns. It is definitely not a course that you can just breeze by in but the work is so rewarding.

Course caused a lot of stress and anxiety, but that just may be expos in general. start you work early, and do all the readings. Discussing the readings in class (even though there are SO many readings) is what make the class fun.
Also, Dr. Raymer is an amazing teacher and is so understanding. She tried her best to not make this class even more stressful than it is.
Instructor: Professor Emilie Raymer

Class Name: Visualizing and Communicating (name is being changed slightly starting next term)

Course Background: two classes per week (1hr and 15min each), 150 or so pages of reading per week, two essays, one capstone project, one capstone project reflection (due in the middle of finals)

My background: 4 years of English instruction in high school, AP English Literature in 12th grade (received a 5 on the exam)

My recommendation: As a STEM person coming out of high school, I liked the premise of this class quite a bit. Unit one featured a deep dive into space images and how they are produced while unit two featured a variety of "crisis" moments in science that everyone was able to find interest in. With that being said, I don't know that I can recommend this class to others. I feel that my writing barely improved compared to high school due to instruction time mostly covering the content of readings (which is important, but arguably not the main focus of a writing class). Grading is hard to evaluate because nothing has been graded as I am writing this review. Professor Raymer is great and super nice, but the class is not the most effectively run for proper learning to occur.

Don't take this class if you need to know how you are doing at a given moment to evaluate how hard you should work in the future. Don't take this class if you are looking to dramatically improve your writing. Don't take this class if you are not interested the intersection of science and ethics.
