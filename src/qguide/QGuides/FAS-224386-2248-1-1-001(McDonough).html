If you're interested in what it means to talk about science, or how we know things generally, this is an awesome course. It's very straightforward (four response papers, easy midterm/final, a chapter of a book to read each class), and many concepts we learned live in my head rent free now. If you like or study histsci, TAKE IT –– something along these lines should be a concentration requirement imo.
Take this course if you're remotely interested in the subject at all. Jeff is such a great professor; he makes the material very accessible and he's funny.
Pros; Sections are very interesting; fun to discuss ideas; the TF was awesome. Feedback on essays was prompt and helpful. There were many essay opportunities of which we only had to do 4 (or 5 to replace a grade), and there was a lot of choice in the prompts. Office hours were acceptable, but most assignments were reading based. Homework involved reading or listening to science podcasts, which were fun to listen to. Midterm was fair, MC and some short essay questions that were graded pretty quickly. The professor makes missing class for illness pretty accessible, posting videos and lecture notes.

Cons; It is at 9 AM, essay questions on midterms are handwritten only, and your grade is almost entirely based on the midterm and final.

** TLDR ; Fantastic course, would recommend if you enjoy philosophy/the physical sciences
Fantastic overview of the most important topics in the phil of science. Professor is great and extremely nice. readings & podcasts are interesting and not too tough. discussions will make you question your view about science and knowledge in general. Definitely take if you are interested in learning about a wide range of philosophical arguments in epistomology
This course is really interesting. As a STEm concentrator (bust passioned about philosophy and metaphysics) challenging me with topics I had not previously explored, such as sociology. I really enojoyed my time in the course, during which I developed new critical thinking strategies and acquired knowledge that I can apply not only in my academic studies but also beyond the classroom. Finally, as a STEM concentrator, I found it particularly rewarding to explore the intersection of philosophy and science. The course assignments were very interesting. The number of writing opportunities throughout the semester was fair (it could have been slightly more), and the questions asked were both challenging and rewarding
Jeff is an incredible lecturer with deep knowledge of the field and has evident passion. The material itself isn't too difficult as long as you pay attention in lecture and do the readings. In addition Jo leads a great section, including great discussions that made me think further about course material and truly analyze and question my beliefs. No philosophy background is needed to succeed in this course.
amazing course and amazing content and amazing lecturer! only reservation with this course was the grading style –– assignments seemed low–stakes but realistically the grading this semester was a little unstandardized and stressful to navigate (though the TA was sweet and did her best, it was still a bit part of my grievances with the course). hopefully it's rectified in the future as the course actually teaches some very useful concepts and really opened my eyes towards philosophy of science. would encourage anyone going into science/research or even with a vague interest in it to try, but be careful –– this is far from a gem.

This is a super chill class. You don't even have to show up, just make sure to do the writing assignments (you only need to do four all semester, highly recommend doing them early) and then there's basically no work. Phil 9 is one of the most generic classes I've ever taken. The lectures are essentially a list of theories (presented without the depth they deserve), and the exams simply ask you to regurgitate those theories. In fact, I got penalized on the writing opportunities for trying to offer an actual thesis. However, this may make the class appealing to some people. If you're looking for something low–effort and somewhat brainless, this is the class for you.
This course is an introduction. It covers a variety of interesting topics in the philosophy of science, but it covers nothing in depth. It helped me organize my thoughts about science, which I found fruitful, but if you're looking for a course that talks about, say, scientific models for a few lectures and reads technical philosophical papers and asks you to write critical philosophy papers on them, this course won't do that.
Jeff is a great lecturer and the materials are easy to follow. If you have not taken a philosophy class before but would like a light introduction, this class is a great option. The mid–term is very reasonable, so are the writing opportunities.
