Chapter Two: 2 Fast 2 Continuous
Life had been hard for Denis lately. Stripped of his cozy fifth floor classroom and banished to the Science Center basement, there were many new realities he had to adjust to. He missed looking out the windows, he missed being a quick trip away from the Math Lounge coffee machine, and most of all, he missed the feeling of solidarity with his students that came from sitting in a cramped classroom.

One day after class in Lecture Hall D, Denis stared at the empty rows of seats, his mind abuzz with musings on Stokes Theorem and its corollaries. He thought back to his old days at Paris and how far he’d come. His eyes began to water as he remembered how his professors and classmates always cheered him on. How could he ever make Harvard’s undergrad math community feel the same way?

Just then, the door crept open. A tall, slender figure slipped in. He was dressed head–to–toe in midnight black, making his pale skin seem almost translucent in the harsh lighting. Upon his entrance, Auroux’s mind snapped back to the present. “Ahh, my favorite student, Twilight character and notable vampire Edward Cullen!”

“Professor, why do you look so pensive?” Edward’s deep voice resounded through the room. He glanced at the crumbled chalk Denis’ hand, which had broken into pieces in his clenched fist.

With his signature French melancholy, Auroux responded. “This whole year, I’ve been thinking about the importance of math in real life. Now, the year has almost come to an end, and I’m no closer to an answer.”

“Life is complicated, professor. There’s more twists and turns than the covering map of the reals to S^1. In my opinion, what matters is being there for each other throughout all of it.”

“Perhaps you’re onto something, Twilight character and notable vampire Edward Cullen. Perhaps it’s all about the people doing the math. It’s about the mathematicians having lively academic debates, and the students bouncing ideas off of each other.”

Edward nodded. “And when we lift those connections to the universal covering space—suddenly, everything’s clearer.”

Auroux’s eyes lit up. “I think I finally have an answer. Perhaps, then, we’re all simply connected after all.”

I recommend 55B, especially if you enjoyed 55A. There isn’t much in the way ––– mathematically or socially ––– of joining 55B without having taken 55A. It would help to be familiar with finite abelian groups and the integers before we begin algebraic topology and to feel comfortable with the notion of a linear map before we discuss differentiation in several variables.

Topology and analysis encapsulate very different mathematical ideas than those I had encountered in algebra, and it took me some time to get used to. For me, the difficulty of reaching a sufficient level of understanding was comparable to that of 55A, but the road to a satisfying level of understanding felt a lot steeper. It took me a lot more work and thinking to make arguments precise, and justifying why a theorem or result or something else in our toolbox was applicable sometimes took a decent amount of effort that didn’t come with much intuition. As compensation and based on consensus, our psets generally called for less brute force problem–solving compared to those from last semester.

The 55 community sometimes fails to be as inclusive and thoughtful as it should be, but most people are kind, and many 55ers become close to a good number of their classmates. Taking the course is a good way to get acclimated to the math department. There’s a lot of careful support built into 55, which I underappreciated before I took other math classes. The class comes with a lot of CAs and Office Hours, a lot of people to pset with, and a lot of classmates hanging around the math lounge on Wednesday nights. Prof. Auroux does an incredible job of explaining complicated math, and is really generous with his time and his willingness to help. I struggled to get the details down this semester, and I couldn’t have gotten close without his explanations in lectures and his patience in walking us through the concepts.

Exams in this class: we had an in–person exam in place of a traditional take–home midterm this semester, which resulted in lower–than–usual test scores. The test was pretty straightforward content–wise, and was not difficult at all relative to the 55A take–home midterm, but the time pressure seemed to get to people. Our final was tangibly easier than the 55A final. Assuming no improvement from this past year, there is about a 40% chance that you can trust Prof. Auroux’s estimated difficulty of and time required to complete a given test; he often underestimates how much we end up struggling.

The 2.5–week real analysis component of Auroux’s version of 55B is sort of notorious for being extremely fast–paced. We definitely moved quickly, but the pace wasn’t insane, and complex analysis felt all the more magical after cooking up diabolical counterexamples in real analysis. I think of the real analysis and point–set topology components of the course primarily as foundational content ––– a language/framework for a lot of important mathematical ideas. My impression is that the heart of the course lies in the algebraic topology and the complex analysis and the connections that we saw between these two subjects by way of covering space theory.
