This is an incredible course, but take it with caution. 1) Know that it will take up most of your time. I spent 90% of my time on this class and 10% on my other classes. 2) You HAVE to study as you learn the material in lecture. Otherwise, it is IMPOSSIBLE to do well on the exams. 3) I heard many people say that they took this course because they couldn’t take LS 2, and they highly regretted that decision. Both courses are fantastic, but this one is much more anatomy than physiology (it’s not a substitute for LS 2).

I just want to reiterate that although this course was a TON of work, it was also the highlight of my semester. I really loved learning the material and I would definitely recommend it, but with caution.
WOW! This class was a bit crazy! HEB 55 is not for the faint of heart! This is the second hardest class that I have taken at Harvard behind Chem 30. Know that if you are taking HEB 55 and want to do well, this class will have to be your highest priority! From attachments, insertions, innervations, and actions of dozens of muscles, to all the bones in the human body, to biomechanical concepts, to physiology, to evolutionary ideas, there is so much content to learn in every unit that it is overwhelming. YOU WILL STRUGGLE AT SOME POINT no matter how good of a student you are, but THAT IS OK. I really learned what I was made of in this class, especially at these moments. From what I understand from other students in this class who had taken LS2, this class is much harder, so be cautious before just treating HEB 55 as a replacement, which is what I thought it was.

Here is my advice to succeed.

1. YOU HAVE TO STUDY AT LEAST 3–4 DAYS A WEEK. Make Anki flashcards with the lecture slides and muscles on them and practice them over and over and over and over and over and over and over! I mean it! YOU CANNOT STUDY FOR THESE EXAMS THE WEEK BEFORE!

2. Don't waste time learning all the muscle attachments unless you understand everything else in a lot of detail already. The muscle attachments matter for so little that understanding the evolutionary concepts, the physiological conceots, what the muscles look like, and what the muscles do is much more important.

3. Do well on the first two exams because the limbs exam is WAY HARDER than the other two, so you will not be able to do well at the end of the course.

4. Go to office hours and ask questions! Cary and Jo are great teachers, so definately use them as a resource.

_______________________

That said, this course does have some problems.

1) The course is a bit outdated. In the age where the internet is so accessible and everyone uses AI, there is really not much of a need to learn the muscle attachments. Anyone can look that up in 5 seconds. The emphasis should really be on what the muscles look like and what they do. Because both instructors have a PhD in anatomy, I understand that theythink everything is important, but as a result of focussing on a lot of the more useless material that can not simply be looked up online or asked to Siri, some of the important biology concepts were lost.

2) This course focusses too much on anatomy and not enough on physiology. I understand that the course is titled Human Evolutionary Anatomy, but what the instructors don't realize is that 90% of the class is looking for a physiology class like LS2 in the spring because they couldn't fit it into their fall class schedule. That is why I took the class too, and I was a bit dissapointed because I was hoping to learn a bit more physiology that I could use in Med School and beyond. I think this class should focus more on physiology, and if students want to learn about anatomy exclusively, they can cross–enroll at the Med School.

3) There are small inconsistencies between the lecture slides and the section handouts and the muscle tables, which is a little bit annoying. Often the start of the next lecture would be correcting mistakes on slides in the previous lecture. This was frustrating, as I was trying to learn the material along the way. It would be great if the instructors tried to avoid this issue going forward.

4) Finally, the grading policy for this class is kind of brutal, with the difficult exams making up 60%. That doesn't sound bad, but that means that if you want to get an A– or A in the class, you are gonna need upper 80s or higher on the exams, which is hard considering there are 75–100 questions on each exam and there is very little (if any) partial credit on these questions. You either know it or you don't. I appreciate that this year the instructors only had 3 exams rather than 4, but even so it was still brutal.

Good luck if you decide to take HEB 55

I took this class instead of LS2 because LS2 didn't fit into my schedule during the fall. I learned so much from this class and really loved the people (Jo and TFs Alex and Cary) along with my classmates, though part of me wishes I took LS2 instead. This class involved an insane amount of memorization, with the most difficult part being muscle tables (aka memorizing the origins, attachments, nerve innervations, actions, origins, and more for dozens of muscles for each of the three exams). It took me an hour to memorize the muscle table for five muscles alone, and I ended up giving up on them because you can still do well on the exams without fully memorizing them. Definitely consider how much time you can spend memorizing for the exams and be prepared to fully leave entire sections blank even after dozens of hours of studying for the exams. The chance to redo one select page from each of the two midterms on the final exam was a lifesaver for my grade. Another thing to note is the fact that this class has both a 5–10 page final paper AND a final exam, so definitely take that into consideration when planning your courses. 

I also wanted to mention that the TFs (Alex and Cary) are very knowledgeable and great people, and lab was easily my favorite part of the course. Overall, I'd definitely recommend taking an anatomy course (either this or LS2) to prepare for medical school. I think this class had a great small class environment, teaching team, course structure, and even community, but be prepared to spend more time on this than you would for LS2, from what I've heard.
