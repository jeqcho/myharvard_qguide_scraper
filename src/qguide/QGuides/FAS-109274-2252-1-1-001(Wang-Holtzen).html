Okay, so here’s the deal. I took this class coming off of AP Physics 1, and honestly, I wasn’t sure what to expect. I’d learned the basics, but I was looking for something that would push me further without turning into an all–consuming nightmare. Physical Sciences 12a totally delivered on that. It’s not just a repeat of high school physics—it’s a chance to build on what you already know, dive deeper into some cool stuff like fluid mechanics, and leave with a stronger understanding of physics than you walked in with.

It’s also just a great course if you want to understand physics but not dive into the deep end. For someone like me, who’s not majoring in physics but needs a solid foundation for engineering, this class was perfect

Pros:

A Real Review That’s Actually Helpful:
Coming from AP Physics 1, I thought I knew the basics. Like, I knew how to do some basic mechanics, forces, and energy problems. But when I jumped into 12a, I realized that there’s a level of depth I hadn’t even touched yet. This course didn’t just breeze through what I already knew—it helped me really understand it. Fluid mechanics was a whole new world for me, and I got to see how the math and concepts actually fit together. Honestly, I walked out of this class with a much better grasp of physics than I had going in.

Demos & Labs: Total Game Changer:
Let’s be real, I didn’t expect the demos to be as entertaining as they were. They were straight–up cool. We didn’t just watch some pendulum swing back and forth—these demos brought the physics to life in ways that made everything feel real. And the labs? They weren’t just a way to pass time. I actually enjoyed them. I got to team up with people, test out theories, and see how physics works in the real world. It wasn’t some dry, boring “let’s just get this done” experience—it was genuinely fun. Plus, the labs were a good way to meet people, so there’s that bonus.

Stars System: Not Perfect, But Solid:
I was a little skeptical about the Stars system at first, but once I got into it, I saw how it helped me stay on top of the material. The idea is simple: prove you know your stuff, earn a Star. It gives you smaller, more manageable goals to work toward instead of one big exam stress fest. Now, I’m not gonna lie, the Stars system can be intense. If you mess up even a little bit, you miss the Star. But there’s a silver lining: if you miss a Star, you can meet with the course leadership to show that you’ve got a handle on the material. It’s kind of like an insurance policy—you get another shot to prove you know your stuff. This kept me from panicking about exams and helped me focus on actually learning.

Incredible Support from the Course Leadership:
One of my favorite things about this class was the course leadership. They weren’t just checking boxes—they actually cared that you understood the material. Any time I had a question or felt like I was falling behind, they were there to help. It wasn’t some generic “go to office hours” vibe; they’d actually meet with you one–on–one, break down concepts until they clicked, and make sure you were on the right track. It was refreshing, especially in a course that’s got some heavy material.

Confidence Boost Like No Other:
Before this course, I felt like I understood physics, but not in the deep way I wanted to. After 12a, though? I felt like I could actually do physics. The new material, like fluid mechanics, was challenging, but the course gave me the tools and understanding to tackle it head–on. By the end of it, I was ready to keep going with physics. If you’ve taken AP Physics 1 or have some basic understanding and are worried about taking that next step, this course will give you the confidence you need to keep pushing forward.

Shoutout to the CAs:
Alright, let’s talk about problem sets. They’re no joke. But what made them manageable was the support I got from the CAs. Seriously—if you’re struggling, get to office hours. The CAs (NeNe, Rohil, Will, Tyler—you guys are the real MVPs) were always ready to break things down and explain stuff until it clicked. It made those PSets way less stressful, and honestly, I probably wouldn’t have made it through without their help.
Cons:

9 A.M. = No Fun:
We all know how it goes—9 a.m. is way too early for anything. Especially when you’ve been pulling late–night study sessions, dealing with other classes, or just living your life. Getting up for this class felt like an Olympic sport in itself. Don’t let the early start fool you—if you’re not a morning person, prepare to drag yourself out of bed.

Stars System (Again):
As much as I loved the Stars system, it’s not all sunshine and rainbows. There were times where I felt like the smallest mistake—something minor—would cost me a Star. And that little bit of stress over missing Stars can get to you, especially if you're someone who’s a little on the perfectionist side. It’s a great system in theory, but it can sometimes feel like you're walking a tightrope.

Coding Projects (Sigh):
Okay, I get it—coding is part of the physics game now, and I see the value of integrating it. But honestly? The coding projects were a bit of a headache. It wasn’t a huge deal since they’re not on the final, but they still felt like extra busywork. They weren’t terrible, just kind of annoying.
PSets Are Time–Consuming (But What Else Is New?):
Let’s face it—problem sets in any physics class are going to take time. These were no exception. Some weeks, I’d be buried in them for hours. It’s manageable, but if you’ve got a packed schedule, they can start to feel like a never–ending cycle. Again, office hours help, but they do take up a good chunk of your time.
