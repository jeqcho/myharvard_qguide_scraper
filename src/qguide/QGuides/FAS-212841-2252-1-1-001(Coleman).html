I have a lot of thoughts. On the one hand, I deeply appreciated the way the course wove together poetry, classical texts (including readings in Latin and Greek), and even etymology. Professor Coleman was incredible—a true humanist who clearly loves what she teaches. Her lectures were moving; I actually shed a few tears during some of them. The field trip to Mount Auburn Cemetery was a highlight and added a tangible dimension to our discussions. That said, I did find the class occasionally vague in its focus. For example, we spent only one lecture on the topic of losing a loved one—arguably the most central theme of loss—while dedicating three lectures to how different religions experience Loss through practices like the Requiem Mass. While I understand that the goal was to highlight the multifaceted nature of loss, I think the course sometimes strayed into overly abstract territory, such as discussions about animal grief, while skimming over more human, relatable experiences like the loss of a romantic partner or close friend. One downside: the grading could feel arbitrarily harsh. While I understand Harvard’s concerns around grade inflation, I felt there was a lack of clear criteria at times. For example, one of my section writings—a letter of condolence—was marked down for being “too cliché.” That kind of feedback, while perhaps well–intentioned, felt subjective and frustrating. So, while I valued the class, I’m not entirely sure I’d recommend it over other electives in the Aesthetics and Culture Gen Ed category that might offer similar insights with more structured expectations.
