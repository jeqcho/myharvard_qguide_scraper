As difficult as it is, MCB 65 is incredibly well taught. The slides are excellent, and each concept is covered in depth. I do feel like I've left the course a much better biologist. Just be ready to decide the necessary hours.
Background: I took this class as a sophomore who took the LS1 series, MCB 60, and CHEM 17 before this and CHEM 27 concurrently. This class was so fun, but it was pretty hard for me without the LS50 primer in stat mech. I feel like my brain is bigger now than it was at the beginning of the semester. MCB 65 was the class that finally got me to learn the amino acids, structural biology lab techniques, and how to be kind of good at using PyMOL. It did take a lot of work to keep up with everything, but the grading isn't insane if that helps, and they're nice about giving out opportunities for bonus points. LPS assignments (PSETs) are due every ~2 weeks and they're pretty big. I'd recommend getting started with each question as soon as you learn the relevant material so you aren't staying up until 4 the night before they're due. The two midterms and final are tough, but they give you a cheat sheet and a bunch of review material to work with, and I felt like I got the hang of it by the end of the semester. There aren't really free points (even attendance is marked for correctness) but as long as you're willing to commit I think the experience is worth it. The community also rocks. It's a small enough and hard enough class that you kind of have to make friends with everyone else. The lecturers are also so cool. Monique is honestly just incredible I love her so much. Max is good when he's lecturing about things he cares about. The TFs were also super solid, very knowledgable about the material and happy to help. Guest lecturers were hit or miss, but some of the opportunities (tour of a crystallography lab, computational protein design) were super super cool. This is my favorite MCB class I've taken so far. It took full commitment at some points (RIP my CHEM 27 grade) but if I could go back in time I would take it again.
Only take this class if you really, really want to learn, have not taken similar courses in the past, and have a lot of free time to dedicate to this course and this course alone. Otherwise, it will feel like drudgery most (if not all) of the time. Course policies feel specifically designed to penalize students for caring about anything other than this course, so you will suffer if you do not make it your first priority.
This class is definitely the most challenging course I’ve taken. Attending lectures and office hours in itself is not enough to do well, but if you are willing and able to put in the extra time it takes to succeed, it’s definitely worth the knowledge gained. Very helpful for understanding protein systems as well as MCAT preparation as material is very relevant.
The course has been improved significantly, from what it sounds like. Fewer psets and consolidated (although longer) lab sessions. I think the course takes itself a bit too seriously for an intermediate level, but I suppose it's fair. The final lab report is a bit onerous, the first lab LPS assignment of answering questions about a paper within a certain time limit was sort of high pressure for the first or second week, and the pop questions are frankly annoying as an attendance mechanism. Professors and course staff care deeply about the course (more than most courses), but the course material itself is not particularly exciting. It hovers between a math/biophysics course and a molecular bio course. I wish the course was one or the other. I don't know that the alternatives for the physical chemistry requirement are much better, but I suppose MCB65 is not a bad course.
This is an excellent course, and also quite fun! It allowed me to integrate everything I'd learned in physical chemistry (PS11) and basic stats/calc for application to molecular/cellular bio problems. Lots of analytical tools/lab techniques were introduced, and you'll definitely expand your PyMol proficiency. Overall, this course solidifies an extremely useful toolkit for any CPB/MCB concentrator. Lectures and labs were always engaging and, in my opinion, the content was at a pretty ideal level of depth. The bi–weekly psets were manageable, and the problems were well–curated. Pop questions were useful as a pre–assessment leading into the pset. The exams were tough for me– they're pretty time–constrained, especially if you're prone to test anxiety or are a slow writer (especially if you're also "wordy"). I'd say that practicing under test conditions (timed/on paper), and practicing as many questions as possible, is crucial here. The exam format is similar to MCB60 in that you're given a new context and need to be able to quickly look past the narrative itself to identify the problem to–be–solved. That being said– I felt extremely supported by every member of the course staff! I really feel that they go above and beyond to support students when they see they're having difficulties. I never felt "stranded" when I fell behind, given how proactive they were in assisting. In sum, I had a great experience in this course, and I'd highly recommend it to any CPB/MCB concentrator. It's ultra–useful content, in an environment highly–conducive to supporting students through any difficult patches they may encounter :)
Class is really interesting and it's important to pay attention during lecture, that will save you a lot of time studying and prepping for the test
LPS and lab report will take a large chunk of your time but the teaching staff is incredibly helpful and really want you to succeed.
MCB65 is a challenging course that is going to require a big time commitment. There are three lectures a week, section, frequent LPS assignments that are very long and can be challenging, a large lab report due right before the final, difficult exams, and other small assignments throughout. I believe that the course is worth it for the vast amount of knowledge that you will learn. The class is small so you will get to know the other people in the class well, and the teaching staff is very supportive and accessible. Something that I really appreciated is that the teaching staff truly listens to our feedback and cares, and made changes throughout the semester (especially after Midterm 1) based on student feedback and suggestions. The exams were definitely very frustrating with low averages, and it is still unclear how they are going to grade the course on a whole as grading is often stringent, so if you are most concerned about getting a high grade I would not recommend this class. However, there are a lot of preparation materials available and I don't think the class as a whole was unfair–just a challenging course with challenging exams. If you're interested in gaining an understanding of a wide range of topics in physical biochemistry and exercising many skills applicable in a real research environment, this is a good class for you–I feel that I learned so much material over the course of the semester that I will actually remember and carry into future courses/research experiences. I'm sure most people reading this have to take this course for CPB and I would recommend this class over the chem/phys options if you enjoy wet lab because lab is a major component of the course and the grade. Definitely make sure you are not pairing this class with another challenging, time–consuming course–for example, a lot of people this year took it alongside Chem 27 which I would not recommend.
MCB 65 is not easy. You are in for a ride.

If you are MCB and would like a more relaxed way to get your intermediate MCB requirement done, don't take MCB 65. If you're CPB, happy birthday. 65 has been my most hardcore trauma bonding experience at Harvard to date, with late night LPS sessions (we are talking 2–3am and beyond, for context) but also immense support from all the course staff. The class really prompts you to think like a researcher and constantly develop ways to improve your experiments and approaches to problems.

MCB 65 also is quite heavy on the Math component (shoutout Max!!), which is something you should definitely take into consideration when thinking about this course. Exams are not easy at all, and unless you are extremely cracked, I think lectures are very helpful and you should try to focus on them as much as you humanly can. LPS biweekly assignments are an improvement from last year, but budget 10–ish hours for each LPS just to be safe (it's so painful finishing writing up all the questions and realizing you have to make 2 graphs and write a full 3 more pages of lab discussion...).

Pros about this class: class size is generally not large, so you get to know all the course staff and your peers pretty well. Monique, Rebecca, Max, George, and Serena are all amazing!! Highly recommend you use the course slack more often (we unfortunately did not do this for some reason), as it is a great resource if you can't make it to office hours.
This is extremely interesting content. But the class will be an enormous amount of work and oftentimes a bit demoralizing. You'll make it through, though!!
This class is very difficult. Psets and exams truly require you to think and apply what you learn in lecture; nothing is ever as simple as lecture, and it will feel like you did not learn whatever you are being asked to solve, but some thorough thinking will get you to the answer. All of this thinking made it difficult to complete exams in time; it even when they said students should finish early enough to have 15 minutes to check over their work, I barely finished the exam in time. Get all the partial credit you can by writing down equations and thoughts, but you won’t get much, and points will be taken for the tiniest of things. The course staff are wonderful and really make the class enjoyable despite the difficult material!

I maybe struggled in this class more than most, but I found this course to be really difficult, and not the most enjoyable. The psets are less frequent than in years past (biweekly as opposed to every week), but are still long and take a lot of time, and the lectures can involve long derivations of different equations, which are cool in theory, but which aren't really relevant for application (psets and exams). It also felt a little disorganized and there was not as much support as I would have liked (I couldn't make it to a lot of the office hours because of other commitments, and we only ever had one course head available at a time due to illness or paternity leave, which obviously wasn't in their control but made the experience a little less positive). Some of this isn't the fault of any of the course staff AT ALL, and they're all very kind and do their best to help, but it was a continually frustrating experience. This is all coming from a very subjective perspective, so take it with a grain of salt, but I came in looking forward to challenging myself and accepting that I wouldn't know everything and might struggle, and ended up leaving the class feeling not only dissatisfied with my own performance, but also with what I felt I'd learned.
The class has a lot of components and the psets + labs can be very time consuming, so I would recommend this class only if you are really interested in the intersection of math/physics with proteins (in which case the class can be really interesting!).
