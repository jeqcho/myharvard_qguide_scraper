This is a really great class! Xiao–Li is a phemonenal lecturer, super fun to listen to, has really interesting ideas, and presents the cooler side of applied statistics in an exciting way. Kyla is a great TF – go to her office hours!

Important note: If you are planning to take this during senior spring, be advised that this class has an intense final project exactly at the moment you will care least about academics. This isn't a knocn on the class at all, but something to consider for the part of senior. year when the sun is finally out and you want to just have an incredible time.
A really nice opportunity overall. Take this course if you're interested in ways Data Science and geospatial data can be used for humanitarian interventions. Great concepts learned in general about Data Science that are widely applicable. Also, the chance to learn about all of these from Xiao–Li is such an amazing opportunity – he has lots of experience and he is very interested in helping the students succeed and be more interested in the high–level view on Data Science too. As you'll find out if you take the course, there isn't ever any free lunch. In anything.
The grading can be maybe just a bit harsh, especially on the project, so do make sure you start it early and ask for feedback from the teaching team. The written feedback is always extremely extensive on all the assignments and they will help you a lot with any questions you have.
Do not take it. The work is not much, but the material is beyond boring. Assignments are overly long (at least there are only 3 of them), and the project expectations are vague.
Stat 288 is a great class for anyone looking to cover machine learning and big data from a more statistical approach. The lectures cover a healthy mixture of applied and theoretical, and the homeworks–although difficult–reinforce the concepts from lecture well. And, at the end of the day, any class in which you get Xiao–Li lectures is always worth it. The final project at the end is also very open–ended and a great opportunity to conduct research that interests you.
Lectures are mandatory and a 3 hours long chunk per week. Though sometimes the lectures can be interesting, most of the time most people are disengaged, and the lectures can drone on. The 3 homeworks are fair and demand good effort, though it is unclear how much value we gain from it. The highlights from the course are perhaps Xiao–Li's lectures (I don't think the guest lectures are particular insightful), and learning about the following things
– Viewing sampling mechanisms by randomizing over the index, instead of the response
– A thorough treatment of the Simpson's paradox
– Learning about DHS and the ML studies that use it to predict poverty levels, with privacy challenges.

So these highlights can be condenses into 3 weeks of material, and the rest of the 10+ weeks can be a bit of the bore. However, you get to work on a final project of your own choosing, so it's pretty good if you wanna work on something that interests you.

Grading is fair by our amazing TF Kyla, so as long as you do all of the psets and the extra credit, and ask questions during lectures, and do the final project seriously, you should get a solid good grade.

STAT 288 was a manageable and interesting grad class! I took this class junior spring with STAT 110/111 and without STAT 210, and felt sufficiently prepared. Some of the theory was difficult to grasp at first and the theoretical lectures are fast–paced, but spending some extra time with the lecture notes and working on the psets was helpful. The workload is very manageable (one class meeting a week, 3 psets in the semester and a final project), but each pset is pretty hefty so make sure to start them early and go to office hours. I took this class because my research interests lie in the intersections of ML/stats and climate, but the sustainable development focus is mostly on a case study about poverty estimation. I still think the theoretical background from the course was helpful and the application was interesting.
Super interesting concepts. Lectures are great and the concepts are very useful and applicable.
