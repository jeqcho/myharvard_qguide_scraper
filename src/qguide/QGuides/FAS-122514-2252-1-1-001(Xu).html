This class has so much potential and the content was genuinely interesting, but lectures were not very engaging (lots of superfluous, technical details and reading off slides) and the psets were frustratingly tedious. For instance, on one of the assignments, I spent at least an hour trying to transcribe a three minute video for conversation analysis. There were approximately 30 other questions on that pset to complete. In addition to frustrating assignments, the grade breakdown was never published or even mentioned in class, and the course felt quite disorganized overall. The take–home final, for example, was supposed to be due on May 13th, but was moved up to May 8th the week prior which caused a significant amount of stressful rescheduling. Overall, this was not a hard class by any means, but it was a bit annoying, and I honestly didn't get a whole lot out of it.

This is a great class to take for your divisional distribution requirement, and professor Xu makes the course pretty fun. He is a good lecturer and will make you laugh on a regular basis. Overall, the course does not take too much time outside of lecture, and the homework assignments count for the majority of your grade. If you are shooting for an A, it is certainly doable if you work alongside a peer or go to weekly office hours. Great course!
This course is quite tough and only for those who know they are interested in LING. The level is quite far above an intro course. Nonetheless, Robert and the course staff are fantastic.
I didn’t really enjoy the first few weeks of the course since I found the material too subjective (coming from a CS background). For example, in the biweekly problem sets, there could be more than one correct answer depending on how you pronounce words or what dialect you speak. That made it hard to know what the "right" answer was. Sometimes during office hours, the TFs couldn’t fully answer my questions on the problem sets because they were confused on it as well. Even when I got my answers checked, I occasionally still lost points after grading. That said, office hours were generally very helpful overall. But as the semester went on, I started to get used to the course and found the later topics more interesting and engaging. I think I definitely learned more once I got into the rhythm of the class. One thing to keep in mind is that the professor changed this year. Because of that, the assignments were harder than in past years. I’d recommend checking who is teaching the course in future semesters, as that could make a big difference in difficulty.

The course provided an accessible introduction to linguistics, covering a huge range of topics including phonology, morphology, grammar, discourse, semantics, sociolinguistics, philosophy of mind, language acquisition, and machine learning. The professor is very light–hearted and makes the material interactive and engaging. There are always in–lecture exercises where we stop, try a linguistic effect ourselves, and then collectively gain a better mental understanding. The psets were also fun to do, and encouraged learning rather than nitpicking. This class does a great job at selling the field of linguistics, but also the material I think is just intriguing to think about, as you know all of this intuitively but sometimes it just needs to be spelled out for you.

I didn't really like this iteration of the class, so I would recommend with reservation at best. The first few lectures were somewhat interesting and did feel like I was learning something about linguistics, but I started losing interest more and more as we progressed in the semester. Some of this might be a fault on my end because I'm particularly more interested in historical linguistics, but even in general I didn't feel like I took away anything from the class. Topics that did feel like you're learning linguistics (historical linguistics is a great example) did not get a lot of time in the semester. The lectures were somewhat boring, the assignments were incredibly dull and section felt practically useless. I did enjoy (some) parts of a few assignments, but some other parts were so particularly frustrating and felt so useless that they ruined my overall experience (example, sitting down and transcribing every word from a TV segment that was incredibly cringe and painful to listen to, attempting to write observations from an extremely pixelated YouTube video where the subject you're supposed to be observing is practically a blurry pixel). There were some inconsistencies in the syllabus, the final project somehow turned into a final project, and the syllabus made no mention of the nature of some assignments that I thought should be mentioned – for example, you are expected to do fieldwork and talk to people for one particular assignment. Which, if you like fieldwork, that's wonderful, but for someone who does not enjoy talking to random people, I definitely would have appreciated the chance to be aware of this assignment. All this being said, I thought the instructors definitely meant well and they were nice and hard–working enough, but that still didn't make the class an enjoyable experience for me. Overall, not my favorite class.
