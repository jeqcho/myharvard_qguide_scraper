Prof. Wang is a brilliant scholar and just as good of a teacher. His class was truly a pleasure––a mix of literary texts from all over the Sinophone world and theoretical texts, mostly Foucault, Benjamin, Derrida, ... Highly recommend if you want to learn about Chinese literature and are willing to spend time reading and writing.
enjoyed both fiction + surprisingly even the critical theory; discussion around violence, history, representation, (political/critical texts/theories) necropolitics, bare lives, Giorgio Agamben, Walter Benjamin
Not necessarily strictly for Humanities students–––decent number of stem students in the class as well
Really enjoyed the texts–––deep–dive into Chinese literature,
this year only around 7 undergraduates (majority graduate students in a class of 30ish students), but I think the discussion was quite balanced, and I learned a lot from the older/more knowledgeable students
go to office hours (Prof. Wang is very eager to help, but outside of office hours, you might not get a whole lot of feedback from him)
not necessary, but helpful to have some general knowledge of Chinese 20th century history coming into the class (like, some of the main political events/social movements)
This is a great course to learn about Chinese literature. As a student from China, I unfortunately have not read much about modern literature, and this course became my introduction. It is very accessible for anyone interested, and we have the option to read either in Chinese or English. I really enjoyed learning about each author and their works, in addition to the philosophical/theoretical frameworks that Professor Wang provides. Having graduate students in the class is also such a great experience because they all offer interesting comments for me to learn from! And Professor Wang is so accessible and responsive with assignments and office hours. He made sure to know everyone and read their responses carefully, and is so well at facilitating discussions in class. Would really recommend!
This is a challenging class but worth it. Professor Wang is a fantastic lecturer and you will get so much out of each class meeting. The readings are a lot but are also incredibly rich and interesting – I definitely recommend doing the full readings if you have the time. I will say that this course is more geared toward MA/PhD students and can be a little intimidating for undergrads, non–EAS concentrators, and students who don't speak Chinese. However, I still think that every kind of student can get something out of the class, and Professor Wang understands people's different experience levels as long as you make a good faith effort to engage with the readings and discussions.
TAKE THIS COURSE! Prof Wang is amazing and genuinely think that I would have never read some of these authors/works if not recommended by this class. Come with basic understanding of Chinese as it enhances the class.
This is a great course that will challenge you with a wide variety of texts, some of which are particularly graphic and difficult to read. However, Professor Wang is a super dynamic professor who always creates excitement and energy behind the texts discussed in class. Do keep in mind the grading – 70% of the grade is from a final essay that is 10–15 pages, so if this scares you maybe don't take this course.
Prof. Wang is a really great lecturer and class is always filled with interesting stories! The final paper is worth a lot so get started early.
This was an excellent introductory class into 20th century Chinese literature; for a legacy Chinese speaker who never had formal experience engaging with fiction, this class felt like an excellent entry point. English and Chinese translations were provided for all the texts, which was very helpful: I could read the English in weeks where I had less time, and try to tackle the Chinese in other weeks. The backgrounds of the students in the class were also very diverse – many hailed from parts of the Sinophone world, and it's very interesting to hear from their personal experiences engaging with these texts in the past. Readings can get dense and long, but doing them and trying to contribute to class discussions really helped me process and learn.
