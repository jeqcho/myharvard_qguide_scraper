This course is AMAZING and anyone interested in computer science should take it! The material covered is really interesting, as you are introduced to various forms of computation/complexity classes and unexpected comparisons between them. Lectures are entertaining and helpful (Hesterberg comes up with various funny examples to demonstrate certain principles in theoretical CS) and he goes over proofs for many of the results in a very clear and understandable way: he introduces all necessary definitions and gives examples to ensure that everyone knows the basics, then goes through each step of the proof so that you can follow it while seeing how clever and magical it is.

It's also clear that Hesterberg is very kind, knowledgeable, and passionate about theoretical computer science as he is always willing to engage with questions and clearly explain anything you don't understand during or after lecture. Near the end of the course, he delves deep into topics from quantum computation to the complexity of games, which is a fun way to see how everything learned up to that point can be applied.

The grading scheme of the course is generous and rewards showing your thinking rather than necessarily being able to solve every single question, which allows for less stress when doing problem sets (and the problem set length is very reasonable: only a few questions). As long as you write down your ideas and try to come up with possible approaches for the problem, you can get a lot of credit. Although the problem sets and midterms have some challenging problems, a lot of preparation is provided (through review sessions and practice exams) and they help you practice the main proof techniques and realize the implications of various facts (for instance, what P = NP being unknown entails). Hesterberg also gets everyone cookies after each exam, so I would highly recommend this class!

This course often gets a bad wrap, but I believe that it's both valuable to a well–rounded CS education and a phenomenally structured and organized class. It is harder than 120, and slightly more time consuming, but it's a lot more interesting and advanced (in my opinion), and will likely prepare you better for 124 if you haven't taken it already. Furthermore, a lot of the theory that you learn is pretty applicable to industry CS, and is especially helpful for things such as time and space complexity. The course also has a ton of great resources, with organized lectures and lecture notes, wonderful sections which recap the topics learned during the week, and a very good textbook which clarifies most of the lecture material. If you attend/watch lectures, go to section, and read the textbook, you'll do more than fine in the class.
