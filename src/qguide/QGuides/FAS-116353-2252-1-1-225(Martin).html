This course is one of the most impactful courses I've taken my freshman year. Beyond developing writing skills, you also learn how to critically think and analyze text. In particular, it helps you think beyond what the mass media is saying about Supreme Court decisions and develop your own thoughts on the topic. Professor Martin really tries to develop a supportive classroom and I think everyone in the class is super open to hearing new ideas.
This class is really amazing, like genuinely the best expos! This class has definitely made me a better writer. I think this class improved my ability to argue and my precision/subtlety in my argumentation. Ultimately though what I took the most from this class was reading the actual Supreme Court decisions, looking and thinking deeply about the rhetoric, arguments, and context of the cases really changed the way I thought about them and protection of our rights is in the US. Many of these cases have very distinct reputations, and this class through reading and discussing those reputations were subverted as we understood the case on a deeper level. Another standout element of this course is the classroom environment and peer relationships that are cultivated. I feel like this class had really good vibes, and I made friends that I think will last beyond this class (a significant part of this is the name games that you paly each class). Finally, professor Martin is great!! You can really tell that he genuanily cares about all of his students and their learning. He also is just really knowledgable and passionate about the subject and really helps fill in the readings and discussion.
I would tell students to enjoy the contents of the course and truly engage with it, that is how you get the most out of it. When it comes to the writing element, be sure to go to the writing center, talk ideas over with peers and edit each others papers, and be sure to understand what is expected out of each paper.
Take this class. You won't regret it. While it's definitely challenging, it's absolutely manageable if you put in the effort, and your hard work will be reflected in your grade. More importantly, you will leave with a deeper understanding of Supreme Court decisions and a whole new perspective on them.
TAKE THIS CLASS if you have even the slightest interest in the law and want an expos experience with the kindest, most knowledgeable professor! Even if you're not prelaw, Prof. Martin makes the content relevant and accessible to students of all backgrounds and interests. Although the readings of actual Supreme Court decisions may seem daunting at first, by the end of the semester, you will be extremely confident and proficient in reading and writing about legal documents. In–class discussions are also lively and enjoyable, largely thanks to the "name game" at the start of each class which fostered an excellent class community. I truly looked forward to attending this course each week. Professor Martin truly revolutionized my writing skills for the better, particularly when it comes to "close reading analysis" and essay structure/organization. His feedback, written in the most incredible handwriting I have ever seen, is extremely detailed and productive. And by far the best part of this course is Professor Martin himself, who is the most considerate, thoughtful instructor I have had at Harvard. He is the rare professor who truly cares for his students as he would his own children –– he understands our schedules, workloads, and stressors and is incredibly accommodating and willing to adjust schedules, content, and deadlines to allow for an enjoyable and educational experience for all. Ultimately, do not pass up the chance to take this wonderful and fascinating course with one of the best professors at Harvard. Is it hard at times? Yes. But you have so much support throughout it and will inevitably emerge with not just a much stronger set of writing skills, but also some wonderful friends.
This course will be very rewarding, but you will be challenged and pushed. To get the most out of the course, you need to put in the work. Otherwise, it won't be great and your grade also may now be great. However, Dr. Martin does a terrific job in facilitating open class discussion. There are no mini writing assignments, so if that is your cup of tea this is not the Expos for you. Your writing will improve a lot and you will have a great time!
TAKE THIS EXPOS! Dr. Martin has made such an instrumental mark on my writing in a short period of time. Ross is easily the most caring professor I have met here at Harvard and truly puts so much effort into making sure that every student gains something from this course. Not only is he a diligent preceptor, but he is also a compassionate mentor. Ross is equally invested in getting to know you as a student and as a person. The excitement and attentiveness he brings to his interpersonal relationships is certainly admirable. Part of this attentiveness comes in the form of his extensive and detailed feedback on written assignments. On every essay, I received no less than thirty comments, all of which were extremely helpful and tailored to my work. This is a preceptor who wants to see you grow and actually challenges you to do so directly, which is invaluable. I distinctly recall him mentioning that he could very well identify each of his 30 students' prose from a writing sample during one class late into the semester, which speaks to how much time and care he pours into feedback and grading. Ross also fosters a loving and vulnerable classroom community where everyone is deeply invested in their classmates' success. I never felt in direct competition with my peers in this course, which is a feeling that sometimes arises in the cutthroat environment Harvard College naturally presents.

This is not an "easy A" course; in fact, you may find yourself with a low grade on your first essay. Ross grades with high standards, which pushes your work to incredible heights. His emphasis on self–advocacy will also transition you to the collegiate/professional setting by forcing you to take ownership of your own learning journey. At the end of the day, this class is going to be one of the ones I remember long after I leave Harvard. It's rare for an introductory writing course to leave such a lasting impact on people, and yet almost everyone I know in the extended network of Personhood in U.S. Constitutional Law "alums" seem to hold the course in similarly high esteem. Let yourself be vulnerable, seek out criticism, and flourish in this wonderful class!
If you have any interest in constitutional law and/or have prior experience reading Supreme Court decisions, I would highly recommend this Expos section.

First and foremost, Ross is an amazing preceptor, and he takes the class extremely seriously. It's clear just how much effort he has spent shaping the class, and he's still looking for ways to improve it. The cases we read are in a logical order and build off of each other, and he managed to foster a tight community within the span of a semester. The one (minor) drawback is that he can take over discussions during section, but it's likely due to his enthusiasm for the subject, and listening to him speak is always interesting.

However, as someone who came in without any experience reading constitutional law nor an incredibly strong writing background, the start of this class was quite rough. Ross won't hold your hand through the writing process; while he is supportive over email, he won't give explicit instructions to some questions and wants you to figure the process out yourself.

For context, I am very much a STEM person and took this with just a tangential interest in law. I struggled through the first essay and felt lost multiple times, but the second paper was better, and I'm ultimately glad I took this course.

Overall, if you are interested in the subject and are willing to put a significant amount of effort into this class, I think it has a lot of value.
