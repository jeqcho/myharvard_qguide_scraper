This was probably my favorite class this semester, mostly because of how un–theoretical and very applicable and relevant it was. Prof. Friedman is such a lovely fellow, and he's clearly very passionate and knowledgeable about the course material; he always is so open to productive discussion and questions/challenges, and he patiently responds to them with an open mind. The results of the polls that he gives us in class to ask about our thoughts on certain subjects or questions or controversies are so interesting to see, and I really like that he uses these polls not as a "are you paying attention to what we're learning" check, but rather as a "given your background and personal opinion, what would you do or choose". Content–wise, it feels like an economic, psych, and sociology class wrapped up all in one; there are so many relevant topics covered (sustainability, technology, inequality, education) that involve interdisciplinary material so it doesn't feel like trudging through a singular econ course. And, the readings, while extensive, are fun to parse through (for the most part). Although the exam format/content was a little ambiguous (Prof. Friedman is a bit traditional in not wanting to give students too much info about what kind of content would be tested or how to study because he believes in using the exam as a tool for students to showcase exactly what they learned and took away from the course without the handicap of knowing how to "game" the exam), it did force us to really engage with the reading material, and I now have a pretty decently–sized repository of the main arguments of those research studies in my head. The weekly coursework is also very minimal: just readings, two op–ed essays, and 1 math–adjacent pset (but really simple once you get the gist of it)! Genuinely I learned so much through this course!

Prof Friedman, however, is very knowledgable and passionate and it shows in his teaching.
