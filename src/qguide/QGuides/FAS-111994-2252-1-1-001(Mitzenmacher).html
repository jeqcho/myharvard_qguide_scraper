[What You Learn]
– Filter data structures to determine set membership for large data (Bloom, Invertible Bloom, Cuckoo)
– Count–sketch structures to determine heavy hitters/most important data points in a large pool of data (Count, Count–Min)
– Hashing mechanisms (min–wise independent, pair–wise independent, cuckoo hashing, multiple–choice hashing)
– Compression mechanisms for large data (delta, gray, arithmetic, prefix–encoding like Huffman, LZ compression, Burrows–Wheeler) to be able to compute and analyze large swaths of data on a compressed form
– Quantization techniques (AQV, stochastic)
– Entropy calculations (to determine/predict large data)
– Erasure coding (like tornado coding)
– Advanced dynamic programming mechanisms (ex. SMAWK)

[Weekly Workload]
– During 1st half of semester, 4 weekly paper readings and summaries on each paper (half a page bullet point/paragraph summary). Usually about 1.5–2 hours per reading, so the class is about ~7 hours/week
– During 2nd half, less readings (0–1 readings) and one/two PSETS (very chill, 5–7 questions with not requiring you to do all questions, a month to do them, way too much time to have to do them but I like that relaxed vibe)
– Also during 2nd half, you'll work on the final project. This is essentially the main workload, I'd begin to ideate/be inspired based on the readings of the 1st half, and it's 1–3 people per team. Most teams do something they're interested in and then try compressing, quantizing, or implement a learned data structure to reduce data load.
This course is definitely not the most deep, thorough, topic focused course in the CS department, but it covers a really interesting and broad spread of topics. You get to read some really cool and fundamental papers on bloom filters (and variants), ranking algorithms, compression techniques, and more, which are kind of old but still very relevant to the techniques we use today and definitely increased my understanding of these topics. There's also this focus on how to read a paper and how to do research in this class, which is definitely helpful if your just starting out and want some advice or direction. The program committee bit of the class at the end was also a lot of fun, since you got to ruthlessly evaluate papers and get a better understanding of why it's so hard to get a paper published at all. Lectures are a mix of discussing the paper and more traditional lecture–y style for explaining certain topics, and sometimes can be dry but generally are pretty good. I definitely think this is a class where you get out what you put in – if you don't really read the papers or just kind of sit through class without listening, you won't get much, but if you do read the papers and listen in class you'll probably learn some neat stuff about the history of common cs techniques.
