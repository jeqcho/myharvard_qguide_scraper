TAKE THIS CLASS. You came here for a liberal arts education. This is it. I cannot recommend Hum 10 enough: you will read texts that will stay with you for the rest of your life with some of the brightest students, professors, and teaching fellows at Harvard. The governing question — what makes us human? — leads you through an odyssey of "great books," from poetry to philosophy. I have never looked forward to lecture more: it really flies by, and discussions in section are some of the best I've had. I have also made some of my closest friends in the class. (Yes, there will be a few very pretentious groups of people; in a class of 90 students, there are bound to be. It's easy to avoid them, and profs usually know how to deal with them in section.)

I know there's been a lot of discourse around the diversity of the curriculum. It's true that it doesn't encompass non–Western literature, and excludes some great works of Western literature as well. But there's value in reading these texts nonetheless. You have to know how this tradition works to subvert it; and scores of postcolonial authors/authors of color have drawn upon these traditions in their own writing (precisely because it was foisted upon them). You can also read radically: professors will often explain these canonical texts through the lens of power differentials, whether it be those of race, class, or gender. The course isn't perfect, but it's a heck of a lot better than Expos, and you will truly get so much out of it.

If you, like me, struggle to understand Harvard's "liberal arts" requirements—including its wildly specific Gened classes—think of this as the substitute; a kind of grounding, "core" class that defines your college experience alongside other staple offerings at Harvard (think Social Studies, or even CS50... though I'd hesitate to call CS50 an exact parallel). You will not regret taking this, I promise.

I really loved both semesters of Humanities 10. It was the most rewarding class of my freshman year; I read a huge selection of great works of literature, and produced four papers that made me view the texts I read differently. While my paper–writing experiences were challenging and sometimes stressful, and while I spent quite a lot of time reading the books, I never regretted my choice to take the course. It truly is a unique class and one of the best of my academic career. The community of Hum10 is also one of the greatest parts of the class: I was surrounded by 89 other freshman who love literature just as strongly, and I made a lot of close friends in the course.
If you're considering taking Hum10, know that yes, it is a significant amount of work more than expos. However, if you love literature and have always wanted to read the greatest works from the dawn of writing, this course is pretty life–changing. Read the books if you're in Hum10; otherwise, there's no point taking it. For both semesters, there was one out of the 12 books that I didn't end up finishing, but for the most part, I read each text thoroughly with annotations. Consider this class; it's worth it :)
