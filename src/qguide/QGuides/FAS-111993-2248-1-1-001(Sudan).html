If you liked 121 –– you'll LOVE 221! It's really delving deeper into the notion of complexity classes and the relationships between them. You'll come out of the course with a good understanding of where we currently stand in complexity theory.
This is a great course, and especially if Madhu's teaching it, it's probably worth taking if you think you have any interest in theoretical computer science or complexity theory in particular. This course is more about breadth than depth, which for me meant there were some topics I really enjoyed and others I wasn't super interested in; I think it's reasonable to expect this experience unless you are really interested in everything theoretical computer science. As a course, it is a great follow–up to CS 121 with many interesting advanced applications of that material and new constructions. Also, I highly recommend taking any course taught by Madhu if you are at all interested in theoretical computer science –– he is a fantastic lecturer and very open to questions both about the course material and going beyond.
This is the most difficult course I have taken at Harvard. However, I loved it. Madhu is an incredible professor with so much passion and (legendary) expertise in the subject that if you are interested in computational complexity, you must take this course.

His lectures tend to give motivation for the material, then walking through the overarching proof structure and concepts, then delving into 1–2 specific proofs in full detail and leaving some other ones as exercises. The class is quite small and I did notice a bit gender imbalanced (there appeared to be 2–3 women in the class) but all the students are very thoughtful and passionate. This is not a course for people who do not care.

The PCP unit was probably the most difficult. But in general this course is very hard. This is one of the classes where I needed to sit in lecture, review the lecture video, then review my notes and look things up before I felt like I could actually understand the material.

The staff is very kind and always willing to answer questions, whether it is on Ed or in person.
This was my first complexity course, and I loved it. Reading Intro TCS (Barak) and parts of Aurora–Barak during the first two weeks of the semester was enough to prepare me for problem sets and understanding lecture. Pf. Sudan is a great lecturer, very easy to ask questions, and his office hours are absolutely amazing. I'm now considering future work in complexity and have a bunch of cool topics for papers that I want to read in the future. Amazing course.
Pretty good class. Very interesting lectures sometimes, and some interesting problems on the homework. Not a high workload at all. In fact, I think I might've gotten more out of the class if there was more work involved.
This class presents a very nice overview of computational complexity theory, with particularly interesting topics such as probabilistically checkable proofs, pseudo–randomness, etc. The material presented in this course is particularly elegant and very well motivated, and will give you the tools to start understanding and thinking about fundamental problems in the field. If you are interested in theoretical computer science broadly, this will definitely be interesting to you. If you are interested in the design and use of algorithms, this course would still be an interesting way to contextualize the work you do, albeit at a more abstract level.
This is a very great class if you're interested in theoretical computer science.
This is a great class, and there is no better person to learn the content from than Prof. Sudan, who was instrumental to developing a lot of the results you learn about. Keep in mind, however, that this is about as theoretical as CS gets – if you're looking for useful applications you won't find them here. The results are amazing, and the class is fairly fast, but the problem sets are biweekly which makes them managable.
Take this class if you enjoyed 121! It's a really natural extension of a lot of the concepts from that class. Madhu gives great lectures, and the course is set up in a way that prioritizes learning.
What an amazing class. Every lecture taught a unique problem that we are trying to solve, and Madhu does a great job of giving the high understanding of the problem, and then the more deep–level math explanation.
