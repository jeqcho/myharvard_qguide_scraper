Julie (the prof) is awesome! She brings baked goods and other treats to class very often and the class events at the end of the semester were fun too.

The class starts off pretty straight forward but gets considerably more complicated towards the end, so make sure that you keep up with content and go to office hours regularly to clarify any confusion. Please be aware that this course is taught in a fully flipped format. You will learn the material on your own time and complete 3 questions on the material every week. A typical week in this class consists of turning in 3 assignments which are one pset, one pre–class assignment(graded on accuracy but for participation), and a lab (participation only). I did learn alot about R during this course. If you are pre–med and need a statistics requirement, I would recommend not taking this class and just take PSYCH 1900.

I would take PSY 1900 or STAT 100 if you want an intro stat class. STAT 102 is not different than the other intro to stat classes, except that the problems used are health/science–related. Julie is a responsive and good instructor, but the workload is a lot for the class. TFs are great for this semester, but I think having a good TF will make the class more bearable.
The midterm is low–key super long (problem set length, about six questions with three or so parts for each). You have a pre–class assignment (watching a video and answering 3 questions) that's due Monday night, then a problem set that's due Tuesday night. Then you have "Lab" where you do more practice problems and it's engagement credit, but you want to do all of the lab problems and get practice in with more problems even though it's optional.

I genuinely think you need a really good stats background to understand the topics well. I came in with fundamental stats and coding (Python/Java) knowledge, and this class is still hard in my opinion, especially the midterm/final, which is most of your grade (these are also open note but it's mainly just to copy and paste code, so I wouldn't worry about coding too much). You really do have to put in effort/more time than necessary to do well, and I felt like we didn't spend a lot of time on actually learning the topics and went straight to the application parts (but it's hard to apply knowledge if you don't know it well).
