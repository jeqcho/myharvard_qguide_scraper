Overall it is very cool and very fun! Take advantage of your TF by asking them questions –– I found that my TF was really, really helpful and I learned the most when I had discussions with my TF about topics.
It's what you make of it.
The material is very interesting and applicable to biological research and the assignments are fair. The in–person lectures are hard to pay attention to because of the two rooms and constant moving around to sit with your section.
Be prepared to do a lot of busy work.
This class provides a good foundation for the molecular techniques you will hear about and maybe use in your PhD. Quality of instruction varies by lecturer, so it helps to have a good TF and section. The class itself is not difficult but is quite time consuming because lectures are watched outside of class.
I would tell future students that you need to allow yourself time to adjust when taking this course. You may not be familiar with most or any of the techniques introduced but don't worry too much as it can feel overwhelming at first.
Put in all the effort you can to the chalk talks and experimental design worksheets. What you put in is what you'll get out, so the more you invest the more worthwhile the course will be for you in the longrun.
Be engaged in the recorded and live lectures! You put in what you get out of it. If you only want to complete the minimum assignments and get a decent grade, you can easily do so. But ultimately what you will learn depends on how much you personally take away from the lectures and how you engage with/think about the content.
You don't really learn 'principles' but rather several experimental techniques. I do wish the class was taught (in–person rather than recorded lectures) with a focus on 'principles' rather than all the minute details of molecular systems that each lecturer specializes in.
Fun class where if you want you can learn a lot, but if you don't invest time or effort you can pretty easily just let is pass by and still pass.
It's useful if you're not super familiar with eukaryotic biochemistry or experimental design
This course provides very detailed information on biochemistry and molecular biology and on experimental techniques that are used to study these areas.
Make sure you get your work done on time and get a good routine down for BCMP!
Easiest/most straightforward of the 3 core BBS classes. If you decently remember undergraduate–level molecular biology (basics of the central dogma), should be enough. The "challenge" of this course would be how good you are at applying experimental methods to solve experimental questions proposed in psets. How good you are at application defines how easy or how slightly less easy (but definitely not hard) your experience is in BCMP 200.
Don't take this course if you don't LOVE molecular biology! It is fairly easy to do well, but it's a slog if you don't like the topic.
Your mileage may vary depending on which instructor is teaching. Some are great at simplifying the concepts while some can get lost in the weeds a bit. Overall, the class is very helpful for learning about common techniques used to study DNA, transcription, and translation. Some lectures may introduce concepts that you want to learn more about, but there just isn't enough time to do so since the class has to cover a lot of ground. However, all instructors seem very open to talking more about their research with you one–on–one. There are also research seminars for each instructor which are helpful for first–year students looking to find a rotation lab. The big assignments are two chalk talks which can be stressful, but there are some course materials to guide you. It is worth noting that your experience can depend heavily on who ends up in your discussion group for live sessions. You may end up with a very cohesive group or a group that is a little more reserved. It's honestly what you make it, but it was helpful for meeting new people.
