What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
This was a phenomenal course, one that is undoubtedly the only of its kind in the department and is likely unique across all political science departments, at least those of which I am aware in the United States. Our knowledge of political science would benefit greatly should such a course be made mandatory for all graduate students.

This course covers a vast expanse of interesting and rather rare literature across the social sciences, including the philosophy of social science. The reading list was structured around difficult questions that researchers should yet often do not ask themselves, such as "what are we trying to do here?", "why?", and "how might it best be done?" The second week, which covered Popper, Kuhn, and Lakatos was particularly fascinating and provided a great foundation for thinking about the social scientific research enterprise more broadly. The third (on theory–building) and fourth (causality) weeks were also fantastically interesting and novel to me. After delving into these broad questions (and providing a groundwork for conceptualization and measurement), the course tackles specific methodologies—observational studies, experiments, and many weeks on qualitative methodologies that were very interesting as someone whose training in political science fell nearly exclusively into the quantitative camp.

This literature and the discussions surrounding it alone provide, in my opinion, sufficient motivation for taking the course. Yet there are two, perhaps even more beneficial aspects of the course that I will now overview.

The first key benefit consists of the number of research skills and advise one picks up throughout the course, primarily from Professor Hall's immense experience and erudition, but also from collective discussions of prominent work with colleagues. These tips concern how to write and frame a paper, which elements to prioritize, how to advance an effective and novel argument, what to focus on in your research, how to discover and expand upon initial ideas, etc. All of these are extremely valuable and almost never packaged so precisely and powerfully as they are in this course.

The second key benefit is the remarkable, almost miraculous quantity and quality of feedback you receive on your work from Professor Hall. There is a good deal of work involved in this course (as there should be in a graduate course at our esteemed institution), and essentially all of it is worth your time. In a fraction of the time you are allotted to complete the assignment, Professor Hall will return penetrating feedback on your work that is often longer (and more insightful) than what you originally submitted. His feedback has helped my research immensely and will have altered the direction of my dissertation research for the better. Feedback from kind and sharp peers was also very helpful throughout the term.

In sum, the benefits of this course are manifold and unparalleled. Not to avail yourself of such a course would be sheer folly. I am much obliged to Professor Hall and my colleagues, who provided interesting, learned, fun, critical–but–respectful interlocution, for the most enjoyable and fruitful course of my graduate career.
Take this class to better understand what kind of political scientist you are and what methodological approach would be the best for your research. This class provides an overview of the discipline and its development, in a way that helps understand not only your own but others' research better.
