I would DEFINITELY recommend this EXPOS section. Ian is a great chill preceptor, and he cares about our improvement as a writer. The readings are extremely minimal, so if you don't want a lot of daily work, this class is for you. In fact, when there is no paper, the amount of weekly homework is probably around 30 minutes. The class environment is highly collaborative and discussion–based, and it helps you think more deeply in the way you craft your arguments. Of course, the papers are quite stressful – I personally spend a lot of time on each paper to do well. Ian seems to have very high expectations for writing, but I think he is just very honest.
Take this Expos. At the end of the day, you have to take an Expos and the content of this one is super interesting and engaging. Ian is an awesome dude who is super funny and brings energy and enthusiasm to every class. Also, every class starts with a fun attendance question that lead to hilarious discussions and getting to know your classmates better. Ian provides really helpful commentary on drafts as well as good advice in conferences with him. I have absolutely no regrets after taking this Expos and it was honestly my favorite class during my first semester.
Please believe the QReport. I took this class not believing the QReport and I was shocked when one of the most difficult Expos classes actually turned out to not be a walk in the park. Ian Shank is genuinely lovely and I believe there are few professors as interesting and funny as him, but this class is not easy. Most people choose this class because they are interested about con artists, but this is a class about academic writing with a subtle con artist theme. Do not be fooled. This class is about the machinations of crafting a college–level paper, with extra focus on how structure on the micro level contributes to flow and interest on the macro level. Expect lengthy reviews on your drafts: there is always something imperfect about your writing and Ian will tell you.

It IS possible to get an A, but you have to be dedicated to not committing any of the cardinal sins of writing as stressed again and again by Ian's comments. I believe this course genuinely made me a better writer, but if you just really want to phone it in, this is not the course for you. Reading the articles/papers he posts is not essential for success in this class, and neither is having a working idea on the motivations of con artists: it is being able to write an engaging paper. Some of that can be taught (this class) and some of that is genuine talent. I am very grateful I took this class, however, because I feel leagues ahead of the other people who may have taken simpler Expos classes.
It's not a crazy amount of work overall, but you have to try really hard on the papers and probably still won't get the grade you want. Ian is great and very accessible, but his grading is a bit harsh so beware.
Make sure to turn in your best possible draft, so that you can make the most of your conference! You only get one before you have to turn in every paper. Other than that, the class is quite easy to manage– the reading is very manageable, especially since the content is so interesting. I thoroughly enjoyed this class, and I highly recommend taking it!
Just come with an open mind and prepare to discuss a lot!
I would love to 100% recommend this class with glowing reviews, as the professor is awesome and super relaxed teaching the course. The only problem I had was the grading system, as the professor Shank was kind of brutal and unforgiving when it comes to your final essay drafts. Some advice I would give is to make sure to be engaged in the class, understanding the writing structure that you're suppose the follow. Also for the essay draft assignments and teacher conferences, making sure to write a very very well done or complete draft before submitting. That way you can understand if the path you're taking the right one when you eventually talk to the professor.
The content of this class is pretty interesting. The workload is not too demanding, it's just readings, response papers (graded on completion), and the final revision of the essays. The only reservation I have is that the grading is a bit strict. If you want an A, you WILL have to work hard for it. Also, it's hard to get specific answers to your drafts during conferences. I personally, found conferences to not be very helpful.
There is no reason to be in this course. Everything we learn is interesting, but that does not outweigh the stress Ian places on us with his strict and vague grading. Just take another course.
This class is undeniably hard but also incredibly interesting and fun. You really do get out what you put in.
I would take this course if you're comfortable not excelling at first. Ian's a decently harsh grader with specific expectations for papers, so pay attention in class to what he tells you to focus your writing on and bring detailed questions to your workshops with him. Definitely don't take it if keeping your GPA high is your number one goal. If you do take this, good luck!
It is a good class but you have to be diligent in writing your essays on time and have to take the feedback very seriously.
