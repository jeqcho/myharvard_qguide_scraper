This class probably won't be offered again, but the one thing I will say is that everyone gets the most out of this class if you actively participate. Ask questions, share your opinion, this will allow you to get the most out of the class.
This class is just fine honestly. Professor McElroy is a fine lecturer and has clearly dedicated much of his life to the subjects, but honestly this class could be a lot better. The first half of the class goes extremely in depth about the science of why the Earth is warming (weather patterns, trade winds, ice caps melting, etc.) and then the second half of the semester is used to talk about how the world very broadly could try to limit the damage of climate change. I wish we'd cut the scientific explanation in half and then go much more in depth about what needs to be done. It felt like most of the solutions we learned were a bit obvious and inactionable (China needs to use less coal, India should build more solar in X region). We're all students at Harvard who chose to take this course and therefore clearly have some interest in making a difference, we should be discussing ways in which we as individuals can possibly help.
Very good course, only work is three short problem sets, but midterm can be quite tricky so make sure to listen during sections. Final paper is long but you are allowed to use LLM.
I was a little disappointed by this class because I do think the topic is urgent and I saw potential within the class. Lecture slides felt disjointed as I didn't feel they built on each other well. Every lesson seemed like its own separate piece and I struggled to see how different parts came together. I also didn't know what I was supposed to know and what I wasn't. Sometimes we would be shown a complex graph or equation and then be told, "you don't have to get all of this," but with no further explanation. Section was not helpful as the TF just read off the slides. Readings were worth the time and helped me understand the material better. Professor McElroy is a very kind professor, which I why I feel bad about leaving a less positive report. I can tell he cares a lot about his class, I just found his style to be a bit too slow at times.

Tips for doing well: Put actual effort into the homework and find someone else to study with. The HW will help you succeed on the midterm. There is NO final except for the project, so you don't have to worry about memorizing content after the mid–term.
This class was so disorganized and I didn't learn much at all. The prof and section leaders droned on and did not engage with students.
This class was incredibly average in every way. If you have ever taken a climate change class before, you will know >90% of all the material introduced. Having taken IB Geography in high school, I found all the course material to be a repetition of that content which made lecture incredibly boring. Section was often less than an hour and just recapped all the important information from the week. Unfortunately due to the attendance policy, it was not an option to just go to section. The weekly assignments were also incredibly easy to the point of being boring. The final essay was a great assignment. I highly recommend choosing a topic you actually care about and it will be a great experience. Both the professor and the TFs care a lot about the class and if you want to put in lots of effort, they will 100% engage.
This is a fantastic class, and I would highly recommend it if you are at all interested in climate change. I had very little knowledge about the mechanics of climate change, but I feel like I have left this class with a strong understanding of how climate change works. I took this to fulfill the science and technology gened requirement, and could not recommend it more as a way to fulfill this category, or as an elective. Professor McElroy is a leader in the field, and it is incredible to be able to learn from him. The rest of the teaching team is fantastic as well. The assignments are very manageable, and you have plenty of time to complete them.
Professor McElroy is an expert, and his stories are interesting. He does a very good job covering both science (largely earth science) and policy at a rigorous but approachable level. That said, I found the midterm and final project for this course to be a level of depth and difficulty that did not match the rest of it. The sections repeated the lecture content almost verbatim, and were largely not for discussion.
This class is definitely good if you're looking for a pretty easy class thats also a relevant topic. The class itself is kinda disorganized, and lectures are usually pretty boring. The assignments are minimal and very easy though.
Would not recommend. While a very easy class with minimal work, it is not worth the time whatsoever. Firstly, my TF struggled slightly with his English, making it frustrating in section to stay engaged when it was broken. However, this was not the main issue. The lectures were very tedious, as the lecturer, while clearly passionate, does not know how to engage students. Then the section was not a helpful supplement to the lectures. In the first section, we were told by our TF that section is not the time for discussion and questions, meaning there was no time in this course for questions or discussion. They actively stopped students being curious in section, leaving only lectures for students to ask questions, which not everyone will do in front of 40 other people. Not a well run course, and I hope they change many aspects as the content could be extremely interesting.
The course is fairly easy, especially if you have any prior experience with climate science.
This class can be a bit dry and technical. I think it would be better suited for a lecture hall rather than a "collaborative"–style classroom. However, it is an effective introduction to the technical aspects of the climate system and energy.
