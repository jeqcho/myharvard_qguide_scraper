This course was great! Professor Swindle teaches with such dedication and offers incredible feedback.
Professor Swindle creates a very welcoming and relaxed environment to explore sociology as a concentration and learn more about global inequality and the many dimensions of inequality in our world today. The readings were light but very interesting, the classes engaging and interactive, and the assignments minimal (a weekly reading response and a short final paper). I would really recommend this class to anyone who is interested in sociology but wants a chill introductory class to the subject.
This class is great if you need an elective or if you want an introduction to sociology. If you are a social studies concentrator or if you are an older sociology concentrator, or if you are just an older student in general, you may find this class very elementary. The class seems to be designed to introduce unfamiliar people to the field of sociology. But many of the class discussions were very surface level. I think that swindle is a brilliant professor, and he has much scholarship and experience to speak from. However, I feel like his teaching style Tends to move very slowly and fails to keep students engaged. The coursework and assignment were very low stakes and low effort was required, however, a lot of it felt like busy work and didn’t add to the complexity of the course.
Overall I really liked this course, but I think one of the challenges about discussing global inequality is that it's challenging to talk about the whole world in just one semester. The course covers different forms of inequality in different regions across the globe, which is great. I learned about so many structures that have a huge impact on inequality that I had never heard about before. Given that there was so much to talk about, I felt like we didn't talk about theory as much, so if you're hoping to dive into theory I might suggest thinking about a different course. Professor Swindle is absolutely fantastic. He has great insights and the feedback that he will give you goes so far above and beyond and is incredibly helpful. Make sure to take the paper plan seriously, because it will make writing your final paper very easy. I would also recommend going over what you're going to write before the writing applications– given the way that they are graded and their weight in your final grade, missing something can have a noticeable impact on your numerical grade in the course.
Very interesting class, the professor is very enthusiastic about the topics. Weekly writing applications are a bit annoying to prepare for but otherwise low workload.
Genuinely an incredible course–––if you are interested in studying how societies are structured and how institutions impact livelihoods, this is the class for you. I came into this class without this preexisting interest; I read the syllabus and thought exploring case studies from all over the world would be a cool experience. This class did not disappoint at all! Grading is fair, assignments are never unreasonable, and the class is structured so it's easy to create a weekly routine for the course. Professor Swindle is an amazing lecturer and great person to talk to about the material. Take this course!
This is a really great class! It is pretty light, I never really stressed about the class and the reading load was pretty light (50 pages/week max) compared to other classes. It was a pretty chill class I could have on the back burner for a lot of the semester, and the final paper affords a ton of flexibility which is super nice. The grading is fair to easy, with a ton of participation. Overall, I came away with some knowledge of some interesting case studies and having had some interesting conversations. Don't take this class if you are looking for something revolutionary or life–changing––the concepts are pretty intuitive––but take it if you are looking for a more chill, fun course that is still interesting! Professor Swindle was awesome, he was super sweet, invested in his students, and passionate. By the end of the semester, you will have gained knowledge about interesting cases and topics such as gender and economic inequality in South Korea, rising homophobia around the world, and redlining, as well as general themes that string these topics together. I took this class as a freshman and it is definitely appropriate for freshmen and/or people who are not in the social sciences. You will also get to know the other students well, as there are many projects and group in–class activities which are fun!
