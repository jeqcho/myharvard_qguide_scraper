I took this class as a math and physics concentrator looking for something light and interesting to fulfill my Arts and Humanities requirement. I've always been interested in history, but up until this course had never taken a history course at Harvard. It was a great class, and while most people in it were classics/adjacent to classics, I never felt out of place or like I couldn't contribute ideas for lack of requisite background knowledge.

It's a fairly standard survey of ancient Greek history, from the Minoans to the Romans. The primary source readings are quite interesting and read surprisingly easy. You should read Herodotus and Thucydides for section; admittedly, the secondary scholarship readings are a bit of a bore.

I did wish the course spent more time on the 4th century period between the Peloponnesian War and Philip's conquests. Prof. Kosmin is great. I think he struck the right balance in acknowledging, for better or worse, the influence Greece – or the ideal of Greece – has had, currently has, and will continue to have on Western society.
I would really recommend this course (with some minor reservations), particularly if Prof. Kosmin is teaching. He is an extremely engaging lecturer (and also just a brilliant scholar) and is always very approachable, I don't regret taking this course with him in the slightest. The course is a bit assessment–heavy, and fair warning that if there are weekly assessments again then it will likely take more time than you expect to make sure you're doing well, but it's far from unmanageable. I would recommend taking this early on, especially as a sophomore or even freshman as intended; it will be useful background, but you also will not have grown a bit weary of section quizzes just yet.
If you are not a humanities guy, this is not a gem. The Q Guide looks gemmy because all the people who take this course are Classics people who think this is easy.
This class is a great introduction to the Ancient Greek world, from the Bronze Age through the Hellenistic period. Paul is a great lecturer, with his thorough knowledge of Ancient Greece allowing him to make nuanced points and to invite students to ask/answer the right questions. Weekly quizzes ensure that students follow along with the readings/lecture, but their exact content is known in advance. Definitely take, even if your not a Classics concentrator but just want to learn more about the Ancient Greek world!
Take it if you want to start building a foundation for a liberal arts education.
Definitely take this course! While I took it as a part of my Classics concentration, the class is extremely accessible to anyone. Paul is an incredible professor, who really knows what he's talking about (he frequently uses images that he took himself at various sites as a part of the curriculum). He lectures well and gives ample opportunity for the class to slow down and ask questions. The assessments aren't too demanding: as long as you pay attention in lecture and do the readings, you shouldn't have much trouble.
I cannot recommend this course strongly enough. If you have even a slight interest, or even little interest at all, in understanding ancient Greek civilization, consider this course. Professor Kosmin is an excellent lecturer with a wealth of knowledge, and he brings his passion for this subject to every lecture. I have never taken a class at Harvard from the Classics Department that did not have a world–class instructor or Teaching Fellows. This is one of those courses that you need to take regardless of your concentration.
If I had to choose how to be introduced to the subject of Greek history, I'd want it to be through a course like 97A. Outstandingly taught, this course provides a fantastic introduction to Greek civilization. Kosmin is the rare lecturer who managed to be detailed and accurate while also incredibly interesting. The course is both fun and very informative. For many Classics concentrators, this course may retread familiar ground often— that is the nature of an introductory course, after all— but Kosmin makes such an effort to incorporate a wide array of artifacts and scholarship that I think even the veteran reader of Greek history will gain something worthwhile from this class.
do the readings
If you do the work assigned, this course is just the best. Yes, there is reading, note–taking, writing, and memorization, but when you do everything asked of you, this course will teach you what it is to truly know a subject, and it is a joy. The lectures are amazing, and the weekly quizzes, while occasionally annoying to study for, give you a grasp on the material that I have not experienced in another class. TAKE THIS COURSE!!!
Overall very very solid class. Very laid back atmosphere and super interesting. A requirement for us classics majors, but I'd recommend it to anyone! The workload is also very light – just weekly reading and quiz.
