OMG. This class was mind–blowing. I came in thinking I only liked modern literature, but this class made me convert my interests to the medieval period. There's a whole world there I have never encountered before this class.

There are two things I didn't like about the class. One is the lack of structure and rather last–minute announcements and organization. I feel like sometimes assignments were announced randomly and the deadlines were rarely clear. The second was the brutal meeting time from 6–8:30pm. I had classes non–stop from 9am so finishing off nearly a 12–hour day on a Wednesday night discussing the dense poetry of Dante was incredibly difficult. I hate that I was fighting not to fall asleep in lecture despite how intriguing it is. I really hope that they could adjust the meeting time because it falls right around dinner time, and I never got a chance to grab dinner so I often had to sit through empty stomach.

Other than those two things, the class was overall great. Prof. Camozzi Pistoja is such a dedicated teacher who treats the class so tenderly as though it is his precious child. He is so meticulous about how one should approach and study Dante, and it's really interesting to see him break down line by line. I think we spent the first lecture on literally just one line (and this lecture was 2.5 hours). You will start to think and notice things about texts that you haven't before. You'll learn how to engage with the text from the world of Dante and learn to appreciate his masterful architecture.

Some of the people in this class are so cracked and it's always lovely to hear them talk in class. To get the most out of this class you need to attend every lecture and section (and trust me, they take attendance carefully). Read the canti, and make an effort on your discussion posts. If you don't you'll only do yourself an injustice.
This class is incredible in so many ways. Ambrogio is so kind, and while a little busy and hard to reach via email, puts a fantastic effort into learning about his students. He learned everyone's name, gave so much personalized feedback, and really encouraged us to explore our interests––all in an iteration of the class that's much larger than normal. Assignments can be a little chaotic at times, so one must follow what he mentions and reach out to the teaching staff if confused. Midterm is kind of hard if you haven't paid attention in class.
Ambrogio Camozzi Pistoja is probably the most interesting professor I've had at Harvard. He is a captivating lecturer, excellent writer, has an almost–encyclopaedic knowledge of Dante as well as a firm knowledge of related philo–/theosophy, ranging from Aristotelian hylomorphism to Michelangelo's Neoplatonic tendencies to Primo Levi. On a more personal level, Ambrogio has a very warm character; he is very kind and caring, and helps you reach your goals both in–class and otherwise.

Ambrogio and the course staff are also extremely open–minded and unequivocally promote free speech in the classroom, no matter how ridiculous the idea. This bears lots of fruits: for one, you discuss ideas ranging from the depths of metaphysics to history, or even a student's relevant personal anecdotes. It also results in some particularly amusing and entertaining moments in class, although I'd nonetheless invite any future students to similarly read Dante with an open mind, considering that, firstly, Dante himself was not completely sane (i.e., the Commedia is not some rational piece of Catholic poetry), and that, secondly, lots of the odd religious, scientific, or linguistic claims he made were considered completely valid in Medieval Europe/the early Renaissance. With that being said, be prepared to meet a diverse range of students: lots of like–minded and interesting classmates as well as a small handful of close–minded (also tend to be highly religious) students who are quite confident in their supposed rational and interpretative fallibility. Such is the nature of a course on Dante, though.

To briefly mention course logistics: assignments consist of weekly commentaries on Dante's canti, which are particularly thought–provoking and allow you to spend time with the text. The final project gives lots of creative liberty, as you can do anything related to Dante: creations ranged from a recitation to an interpretive piece of music to traditional essays as well as a series of paintings. We also had a midterm at some point, which was a short multiple–choice exam that evaluated how well people were paying attention in lecture—it was a smattering of questions ranging from the content of the Commedia to the sonority of different vowels, a subject that Ambrogio had discussed in a very memorable lecture.

The amount of work you'll have in the course is completely dependent on what you put into it.

Overall, this class is a one–of–a–kind experience; something that I looked forward to Wednesday night, and which helped me broaden my horizons and improve my skills as a writer—10/10 would recommend.

Do not regret missing out on this course!

A great course, although I wouldn't recommend it to everyone. This year it was a bit large (thanks probably to previous Q reports and recommendations) and I think the quality of the course suffered from this. The flow of the class was probably a lot slower because of the sheer amount of people: I don't think Prof. Camozzi Pistoja is comfortable with such a large class. Standards of engagement were necessarily lowered, the course was kind of disorganized in general and it was hard for the course staff to scale it. So I'd say if you're not too interested in the Comedy in advance, maybe shop other options in addition to this one and consider switching out if there's so many people again.

All that said, Prof. Camozzi Pistoja absolutely exudes joy for the Divine Comedy and you will be continually impressed by his sheer love for the text, even when the observations get a bit out there. You will learn plenty about the Comedy and the broader context of Dante's life and work. Section was a lot of fun because I ended up in a smaller one with Finlay, who was a great TF and has a lot of interesting takes. I regret that I wasn't able to devote more additional time to this class to get to know Italian because the original text is really special and no translation can compensate for it. The work isn't hard because that's not really the point; you really get out what you put in in terms of the final project and readings. The class composition is really varied, so take the course with some friends (who also care about the course) and you'll have a good time and learn a lot.
If you have an opportunity to take this class, by all means take it! It was undoubtedly one of my favorite classes at Harvard for its ability to synthesize material from the medieval period in a way that is both engaging to scholars and newcomers alike. This was in no small part to due Ambrogio, who embraces and celebrates the unique interests of each of his students, ultimately making each class and section a rich exchange of ideas as all students work together with Ambrogio to dissect the nuance within the text of the Commedia. Though I had read this work in its entirety in high school, I completely overlooked the beautiful intricacies within the text, whether it be the rich body of classical scholarship from which it draws, the underlying concentric structures between Cantos, or the numerology within the Commedia. By the end of the class, I felt that the entire class had, like Dante, undergone a journey together, exploring the horrors of Inferno to the joys of Paradiso. All of us students had gradually formed a bond of friendship, through which study of the material was all the more enjoyable; by its end, I truly felt sad to leave the class.

This class was absolutely transformational, and among my favourite classes I’ve taken at Harvard.

Dedicating a whole class to focus on Dante’s masterpiece was definitely a special experience as we could really deeply understand Dante’s purpose and stories. Originally involved with politics, Dante was sent into exile from Florence, upon which he turned to Christianity and realised the corruption within politics and the Church at the time. In a way, the Comedy has been a way for Dante to not only comment on each of the particular deficiencies within society at the time, but also discover the areas in which he falls short, in doing so, commenting on the human condition and exploring what transcends the physical world. Almost all those mentioned in Inferno lived around 200m away from where Dante lived, or they were political or biblical references.

This class taught me a lot about the power of storytelling. I had already been interested in literature in classical antiquity, and noticing the intertextuality here with Virgil was magnificent. I think I came to appreciate how literature can preserve stories from thousands of years ago, and it was such a pleasure and privilege to study such a text with such an incredible teaching team and wonderful peers.

At the end of the day, this was a course about what it means to live a fulfilling life, close to God, taking in the beauty of humanity’s power for rationality and faith.
Literature doesn’t just preserve stories; it preserves humanity itself.

The seminars were quite long – nearly 2.5 hours total, so it was slightly hard to fully engage sometimes. But the final project was deeply, deeply fulfilling, and Ambrogio CP is just so open–minded and brilliant of a thinker. He’s deeply introspective and a very critical thinker, so learning from him was such an incredible experience. You really have full creative autonomy in this class and the ability to really engage with medieval literature – this was easily my favourite class this semester.
