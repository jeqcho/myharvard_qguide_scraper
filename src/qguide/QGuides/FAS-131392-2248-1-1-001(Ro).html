This is a really great class if you want to spend time learning stat mech. Vinny has put a lot of thought into the content and the lectures reflect it, treating a very dense and often confusing subject with a lot of precision and subtlety that will make you appreciate how interesting the topic is. Although the lectures are not specific to any field of application, the hw problems definitely lean towards soft matter physics and tend to be quite applied. You get to check out all kinds of cool applications of stat mech both inside and outside of physics – active matter, ML, percolation, information theory, etc. – through the midterm and final presentations. As Vinny will tell you, there is no physics in stat mech, which means we can do a lot more than just physics with it. On that note, the presentations are in private with the course staff and you should expect them to ask you questions individually, they are quite tough. Overall though a fantastic class that will teach you stat mech very well, you will learn that stat mech is a lot more than you thought it was.
This course does a great job grounding statistical mechanics in information theory. It puts the subject on a nice mathematical footing that makes concepts like entropy much easier to think about. The end of the course, which covers phase transitions, is also very interesting and goes through many difficult but useful concepts very quickly. I feel that this course gave me a very strong foundation in statistical mechanics, both mathematically and conceptually, though it was certainly a time commitment.

Deeply engage with all aspects of the course.
Professors Manoharan’s and Ro’s Physics 262 was a very interesting but very challenging experience. In terms of instruction and choice of material, the course is very well done and presents the logic of statistical mechanics (which is famously messy and poorly–understood) with great clarity. Good lectures, interesting problems, and lovely teaching staff (shoutout to TF Henry Bae who was very accessible, understanding and gave good advice). The main critique I have towards the course was its structuring – the difficulty ramping in terms of both volume and complexity of content as it went along was so ridiculous it was almost comical: a course that starts with a 1D random walk occupying an entire first lecture should not be covering renormalisation group theory in one lecture near the end, and the brutal final project (which was also graded brutally harshly) essentially involved familiarising oneself to good depth with an entire >40% of a different course (granted, of ones own choosing but still) in one week. Problem sets also ramped up similarly in difficulty and length. This structuring, though obviously not unfair as it is subjected to everyone in the class, makes for a very stressful and overwhelming final month, that for me personally was the most difficult final month of a class I have ever had. That being said, I do believe that the course was interesting and taught me a lot, and is something I would recommend if you prepare for its somewhat ridiculous structuring.
