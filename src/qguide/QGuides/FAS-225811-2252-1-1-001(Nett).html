This was a great course that genuinely impacts how you see the world around you! If you have an interest in pharma/medicine, I'd highly recommend. You'll learn about a breadth of natural products from diverse sources, with diverse applications (esp. in human medicine). This class gives a thorough appreciation for the fact that "the dose makes the poison"– an important insight for those interested in developing novel therapeutics, understanding the current pharma landscape, etc. For those interested in medicine, you'll also come to appreciate just how large the proportion of drugs derived from natural products is (both directly or through taking inspiration). There's also lots of pharma history built into the course, e.g. lots of discussion about how observed *toxic/adverse effects* of natural products gives rise to new mechanistic understandings and downstream therapeutics. There's a nice integration of basic biology/chemistry/orgo with engaging case studies, e.g. involving hot–topic disease processes. The journal club/pset component is especially constructive, since you'll likely encounter plenty of research approaches you haven't yet engaged with, and you'll learn from scrutinizing those approaches. The course staff are excellent– they're enthusiastic, supportive, and clearly focused on making the course the most enriching experience possible. I had a great time in this course, and I see natural products everywhere now! I have a perceptibly greater appreciation for the nature around me after practicing– through this class– seeing it through the lens of natural products. If you take this course, you'll never have a lackluster visit to a botanical garden again (if you had before)! :)

MCB 130 is a new class that was introduced during the Spring 2025 term led by Professor Ryan Nett on the pharmacy of life. The class is set up in a way that weighs Problem Sets as 50% of your grade, Weekly Reading Quizzes as 10% of your grade, a Final Project as 30% of your grade, and Participation as 10% of your grade. Overall, this means that there is work almost every week within the course, making it a bit of a bigger time commitment.

As a first–time course head, Ryan has done a great job of promoting an encouraging and helpful learning atmosphere. At the start of the course, the course originally had two–part Problem Sets every week, which was a bit excessive. However, upon hearing student feedback on this, Ryan immediately adjusted the course and shifted to a one–part PSET/week structure, showing his willingness to be flexible and adapt to the needs of his students. In my opinion, this shows his dedication and willingness to build a strong course – even asking students across multiple points of the semester to provide feedback on how the class can be improved.

From a content perspective, I think MCB 130 is one of the most interesting classes I have taken thus far. From talking about the mechanisms behind different drugs and natural molecules to encouraging us to seek out a molecule of our own to research at the end of the course, this made for a really engaging and enjoyable course environment.

For the Problem Sets, although they are not inherently difficult by any means, they can be tedious at times, as with the last one being 28 questions about the weekly paper reading. However, Office Hours remain extremely accessible for students, with one of the TFs (Hisham Tadfie) remaining one of the biggest sources of support. Looking back, Hisham and Ryan together make one of the strongest TF/Professor teaching teams I’ve had thus far at my time at Harvard, and have a great team dynamic within the course.

Unfortunately, one of the downsides of the course relates back to an uneven level of involvement amongst members of the teaching team. While Hisham was consistently very present for students across the semester, the other teaching staff member (Jaime Martinez Grundman) seemed noticeably less engaged. This even came in the form of Jaime being absent from Office Hours that he sent announcements regarding, instead only leaving Hisham to answer questions. Overall, I think that this highlights an unfair distribution of work across the teaching staff. Personally, this has only made me further appreciate the accessibility of Hisham as a TF, especially after seeing the additional work he has had to take on to ensure the success of students within the course.

As a whole, I think that MCB 130 has been a great course and very enjoyable. Given how it is the first iteration of the course, I think that there is room for improvement, but I have high hopes that Ryan will continue to take the feedback from students from this year and improve the course as a whole. Ultimately, I think MCB 130 is a class that is definitely worth the time and consideration of students interested in learning more about small molecules and the pharmacy of life.
