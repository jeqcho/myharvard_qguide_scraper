I came into this course expecting a rigorous treatment of introductory electromagnetism, but it was not as fulfilling as I expected it to be. Professor Deslauriers has really good intentions of trying to emphasize student learning, but the outcome does not meet these intentions. Each class had poor pacing: he spends a very long time on the first part of each lecture, and then rushes towards the end of the lecture, which is often where the hardest content is (and this content is barely revisited next class but expected to know on the problem sets). He also rushes to then do one–minute papers at the end of most classes, where students are required to write down questions they have, from which he generates an AI summary of these questions. This approach has good intentions, but it is poorly executed: because of the pacing of the class, I was often fumbling to write down questions regarding concepts that were not fully explained, and it does not give enough time for students to process the lecture and come up with meaningful questions. I would prefer he prioritize explaining the content really well, rather than trying various tools to "assist" in learning. Furthermore, when doing class activities, he would repeatedly make mistakes regarding the answers to questions that introduced concepts, leaving students even more confused about the concept. When other students would point out or ask questions about these mistakes, he would often respond jokingly by saying "just checking that you were paying attention", which seemed like he did not take ownership of the mistakes, shrugging them off and then rushing to get to the next concept. Many students felt like we were memorizing how to solve certain pset problems rather than understanding how to apply the underlying concepts, and that was really hard. It might be because this is his first time teaching Physics 15B, but it is still disappointing after hearing so many good things about his teaching style from friends in Physics 143a. The content of Physics 15B is really interesting, but the way it was taught this semester requires a lot of improvement.
