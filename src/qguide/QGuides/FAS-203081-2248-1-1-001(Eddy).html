A very famous biostats course, and for good reason. I have so much I'd like to say about this class, and I fear I might not be able to capture all of it in this comment. A few takeaways below:

It is challenging. Don't be too intimidated, but be prepared to really stretch your brain. This course's ability to connect theory to practice in biostatistics is unparalleled. It will teach you how to hand–implement many commonly used techniques in biological data analysis (principal component analysis, expectation–maximization, sequence alignment, phylogenetic reconstruction, K–means clustering, hidden Markov models, etc.). You will be surrounded by students from all different backgrounds and points in their education (ambitious underclassmen with competitive math/bio/CS under their belts, as well as OEB grad students who haven't touched a console once in their lives), and this course strives to be accessible to everyone. Unlike some science classes here that try to make you feel dumb through pretentious gotcha questions and a toxic dick–measuring grade/classroom environment, this course truly aims to help you learn, whatever your background may be. For Harvard students interested in exploring the world of biological data in tech, grad school, medical research, etc. in the future, this course is so incredibly helpful and rewarding.

The point of the course is not just to teach you HOW to implement these techniques, but also WHY. Programming packages that perform many of these analyses already exist online for any biostatistician to use, but Sean cracks open these black boxes to show you exactly what they do and how to replicate them. Sean does an amazing job of deriving bio–statistically relevant formulas and concepts from first principles, and his psets (which are basically puzzle adventures that use toy data) are fun well–written challenges that integrate the weekly concepts incredibly well to motivate your search for the answers. But be warned, they will be difficult: the EM pset was the most challenging problem set I have ever completed in my time at Harvard. Because you are essentially crunching every calculation in your console/terminal, you are forced to actually understand the mathematical rigor underpinning your analyses and their limitations, and you will oftentimes have to perform really high spatial reasoning to even begin to approach some of the biological problems in your head.

Although Sean advertises this class as one without prerequisites, I would more strongly urge you to have a good background in at least three of the four (biology, math, programming, probability/statistical inference) than he does. Of the four, I think a background in programming will get your the furthest is terms of optimizing the time you spend on psets. Unless your Python skills are already up to shape or you have reliable pset partners (which I thankfully did), do not underestimate how much of a time sink this class can be. For context, I went into this class with a background in everything except non–R programming (LS1a/b, Stat 110/111, Stat 114, multivar calc/lin alg), and I spent approx. 8–15 hrs/week on this class. The fast pace of the course and its weekly psets are almost unforgivingly demanding, but for good reason. The one–pset drop policy will keep you on your toes, but don't worry too much about your grade; the TFs will grace you with their mercy. I also ended up taking this class during my last semester as an off–cycle thesising senior, which meant that my October/November was a hell I never want to experience again, but I'm a slow worker so hey, if I can do it, you can too.

A few final notes: find a pset partner or two. Get to know your TFs: they're your friends, and they're awesome! And finally, have fun!
