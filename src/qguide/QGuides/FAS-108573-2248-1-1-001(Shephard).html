If you're looking for a fairly chill course for the stats masters, I think 242 could be a decent choice, but just accept that you probably won't take away that much from the course. The pros of the course are that it's not that much work, the actual math isn't usually too hard, and the grading was reasonable/lenient. I couldn't quite put my finger on what was wrong with the course for a while, but I think it ultimately comes down to the fact that Neil tries to cover way too much content but doesn't go deeply enough into any of it. I think the course is generally too theoretical, and I'm not super sure how to use most of the material that we covered. Also, the psets (especially the earlier ones) were not always super clear in terms of what they wanted, so we would often need clarifications in order to do certain problems.
Very interesting content. The in–depth and scope of the class was very good as well. The psets and class notes are a bit disorganized, but the psets aren't impossible to do off of the formatted "textbook."
This class covers a lot of topics and can sometimes move very fast. You should either have a sufficient background or be comfortable with doing extra work to learn background stuff required for the material.
This class is not a light load. It has a ton of material and the homeworks, while not crazy hard, take some time and thought....but the class has a high reward in that you gain a reasonably in depth understanding of a very wide range of topics within insight into their theoretical aspects and applications to data.

The theoretical level is somewhat rigorous but not at the level of stat 212 (aka very technical stochastic process content). E.g., we talk about Brownian motion and use it but don't go into all the proofs of its properties. We do some stochastic calculus but don't go through all the steps of defining an Ito integral etc.
Do not take this course unless you have to
I enjoyed the course, but think it could be better structured.
This class is honestly pretty chill. Each problem set should take no more than 12 ish hours, and are due biweekly, which is nice. You're allowed to drop a problem set, but I recommend that you do all of them to understand the material. The biggest challenge in this course is understanding what is being asked of you. Neil's problem sets are often unclear in the sense that sometimes you will have absolutely no idea what is being asked of you. I strongly recommend that you read through the problem sets and accumulate a list of things that are confusing you. Then go to one of the office hours and ask for help... the teaching staff was honestly incredible.
Great class for people interested in statistics, probably more for PhD students than people looking to apply time series in industry
Pros:
–Useful material
–Assignments were mostly reasonable and there were only five p–sets (one of which could be dropped), so the workload was relatively light
– The textbook was mostly clear

Cons:
–Lectures were extremely dreary — most people stopped coming by the end
– Parts of the textbook were incredibly difficult to parse
–Some of the p–set questions were unreasonably difficult/bashy
