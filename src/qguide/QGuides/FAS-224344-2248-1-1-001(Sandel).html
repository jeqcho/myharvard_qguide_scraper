Sandel is an exceptional lecturer, and his reputation and influence (bringing Justice Jackson to class was incredible) bring significant value to the experience. However, for a professor with such a deep background in philosophical inquiry, spending their life investigating various questions of justice, ethics, and merit, it’s disappointing that the course focuses so little on his own perspectives and refutations (his books actively criticize Rawls while the course introduces minimal critiques). Instead, it often leaves the discussion to undergraduates who may lack the depth to engage meaningfully with the material.

While the in–class debates were intriguing, the format naturally has its limitations. You have to consider the type of student who would want to share their opinion (especially on topics such as affirmative action and reparations) in front of 800 other students. The dialogues were often narrow and incredibly uninformed, to which Sandel only facilitated as opposed to contributing or elaborating.

The course seems more focused on presenting what historical philosophers have thought rather than challenging students to develop their own critical thinking skills. The course could so easily take up a “teach you how to think” pedagogy but instead defaults to “teach you what others thought.” The final exam reinforced this issue by emphasizing memorization and regurgitation of philosophical ideas rather than encouraging original analysis or thought.

The papers also fell short. Expectations were poorly communicated, with rubrics and guidelines varying across TFs. In a class as large as Justice, minimizing inconsistency is incredibly important. The paper topics seemed disconnected from the course’s broader themes and failed to provide an opportunity to “explore Justice,” as the course attempts to beg. Section was a benefit — unfiltered debates and development of opinions.

Overall, the course feels like an introduction to surface–level philosophy rather than an opportunity to inspire and cultivate new ways of thinking. A stronger focus on developing students' own philosophical perspectives would greatly improve its value.
