Don't take this class, unless you really need two and a half hours a week to sit and do work on your laptop while someone is talking at the front. Classes were very redundant because you'd do (or not do) the readings and then class would be an instructor just going through the studies you'd just read. This makes no sense. There was absolutely no added value from going to class. It's a cool concept in theory, but a brick in practice. If you're super interested in the topic, just read the instructors' book and poke around the syllabus. Also, assignments were very loose–form yet graded sort of harshly. Caileigh is great though!
The class is chill for the most part unless you have an essay due. The professors are beyond sweet and interesting to talk to in their office hours even if lectures are not particularly engaging. If you are interested in religion or cults, would recommend.
Though this was an interesting concept, the two hour lectures every week were slow. Most of this course could be self taught, as essentially every week is a literature review, and the only assignments are papers. Though section does involve a bit more conversation, this course felt very dry overall. Unless you really need a paper–only econ course, I would go with another option.
I recommend taking this course if you're interested in religiosity around the world and the ways in which economic behaviors can predict religious behavior and up–take.
This is a very interesting class with a relatively low workload! If you're curious about the intersection of religion and economics, it's a perfect fit. The lectures can sometimes feel a bit dry or hard to follow, especially if you haven’t done the readings in depth, which can be challenging given the large volume assigned for each session. I do wish the professors provided more background context, particularly since some students are completely unfamiliar with certain religions.

One of the best aspects of the class is that there are only three papers and no exams. Start your papers early and make sure to ask for feedback from the professors. Once you revise accordingly, I found the grading to be quite fair. Dr. McCleary and Dr. Barro are both incredibly knowledgeable and helpful. I'm not sure the sections are entirely necessary (since the lectures are discussion–based), but Caileigh was lovely and supportive.
Very interesting field. McCleary and Barro are giants in the study of the economics of religion––you will be learning from the forefront of this young field.
