This is an extremely well–structured class. The coding part should not feel intimidating to you as the staff does a great job of writing guides to supplement the theoretical learning that we do in class. Between theory and practical I would say the class leans heavily on the theoretical side (so be aware of those pre–requisites). Personally, I prefer the practical side but within the theory side we do discuss ways to perform analyses empirically (which we often then implement in "vignettes"––a case study that we do for each unit––or in the homework assignments. Another wonderful thing about this class is that the distribution of work is very fair and not at all overly dense, which is an issue many Stats classes have suffered from in my experience. This makes it a good class to take in combination with other more chaotic classes. That of course does not mean this class's material is easy, but just that it is much less painful. The course staff are very kind and don't want you to be overwhelmed. Interesting material and the coding is very cool and make you feel like a real professional. Recommend!
141 offers a good mixture of theory and application in spatial statistics and is not the most stressful class. Alex is a great instructor and very knowledgeable on the topic and provides a lot of help in office hours to make the course more accessible. One of the reasons I took this class was because of how highly–recommended Alex was as a professor, and he did not disappoint here. The course was very organized, and lecture notes and assignments were always available on time, and assignments were handed back in a timely manner. In fact, the final exam was graded within 24 hours of its due date. However, the level of math is greater than comparable 100 level courses and may be a bit daunting, so if you don't have a strong math background at the level of Math 112 you may get a bit confused early on in the course, though Alex will do his best to explain these complicated topics at a more simple level since students in the class came from all different backgrounds, ranging from undergraduate sophomores to biostats PhD candidates. The TF, Sarika, was also great, though I'm not sure if she'll be the TF in the next iteration of this class in 2027.

The content itself is somewhat hard to motivate, and I felt that I wasn't able to really grasp what was going on or see why it was important. Spatial statistics is still a somewhat esoteric field, though its application to economics is growing. However, it's rooted in geology and mining and so is still largely associated with those fields, though Alex does do a good job of applying it to many different areas. Still, it didn't feel like I was learning as much useful information as I was in my other stats classes, though that may have just been due to the level of math and theory in this class being greater than in other stat courses.

I think this course would make a lot more sense to take with a time series background, since it really just extends the concepts from time series (such as autoregression) to multiple dimensions. So taking a class like STAT 171 or 131 before this would be very helpful as well.

This class has 7 psets (including a short one due the first week of class), due roughly every other week. The class is split into 3 units (point processes, lattice processes, and geostatistics), so each unit has a theory pset followed by an application pset, so the class keeps a good balance between the two. Alex has "numerical examples" available on Canvas that help a lot with the coding (R) psets, and office hours are provided throughout the week to help with these assignments. They are graded generously as well and are worth 65 percent of your grade.

The course has no midterm and has a take home final exam, for which office hours are still provided if you need assistance. The take home exam is very reasonable and no more difficult than the psets, so if you understood what you were doing on the psets you'll be fine for the final.

Overall, this class probably won't be the most stressful for you due to its pset/exam structure and may be a more chill way to learn about these extensions of linear modeling. However, you might want to take a look into what spatial statistics covers to tell if you'd enjoy taking this course and to tell how much you'd take away from it.
/
If you are interested in working with spatial data in a research context, take STAT 141! I took Stat 110, 111, and 139 before taking this class –– all three were helpful background, but I think you will be fine with only 110 and 111. The class is fast–paced and blends theory and practice effectively, and the lecture notes are very thorough and helpful. Alex is a great professor –– lectures are clear and he is super accessible outside of class for office hours (he's also a helpful resource to have outside of class too, since he works with undergraduate studies / prospective thesis writers for the stat department). Workload was manageable (biweekly psets, no midterm, and a take–home final), but some of the psets were pretty hefty so be sure to start them early. That said, spatial statistics has very specific applications in epidemiology, environmental science/geoscience, and a few other fields: the class does get in the weeds, so if you're not really interested in any of those I wouldn't take it.
