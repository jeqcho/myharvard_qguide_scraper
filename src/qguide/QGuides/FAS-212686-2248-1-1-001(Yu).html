What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
This class is great overall and has a light workload. You will do paper reviews for each class and be assigned to present one paper. Depending on the number of students, you will either present alone or be paired with another person. On average, presenting a paper takes up an entire class, depending on when the presentation starts. This is the only stressful part of the semester; otherwise, the class is very manageable.

Reading the assigned paper and completing the reviews for each class doesn’t take long, as the reviews are short and require only a few words. Attendance in class tends to be low overall.

One aspect I didn’t like is that after the first two or so classes, the course shifted to feeling like a seminar, where students presented their slides while the professor and other students asked questions. It would have been better if some lectures on key concepts were included occasionally to balance things out.

As for the final project, if you have experience with other graduate–level classes and understand the expectations for a graduate–level final project, you should be fine. Just make sure your project is not trivial or minor and that it aligns with the professor’s interests.

Overall, this is perhaps the best graduate–level class I have taken, and I’m sure others will enjoy it as well.
This class teaches you all about the cutting edge work done in improving computer networking for machine learning. Before this class, I always hesitated to get into the weeds with ML systems, but now after this class I feel comfortable with the workings behind many of the popular pipelining, training, and serving frameworks and how they are motivated/work. You get exposed to lots of material, covering everything starting from parallelisms and then branching off into a number of topics such as scheduling, congestion, fault tolerance, and deployed systems in the real world, etc. The course gave me more confidence in how real world systems work, what the problems are that need to be solved, and made me appreciate the overall hardness of the field. Each paper seemed practically relevant and asked interesting questions. The course makes you read through the papers and really question what makes a paper good or bad, not taking its contents for granted. I learned so much about analyzing figures and understanding where the bottlenecks are, whether the experiments are answering the right questions, and whether the experiments are even answering the question they supposedly answer. The professor is full of insights, and is fantastic at fostering discussions with incisive questions that test deep understanding. The structure of the course is perfect.
Very good class if you want to do research. Professor Yu is an excellent teacher who cares a lot about her students. Highly recommended.
