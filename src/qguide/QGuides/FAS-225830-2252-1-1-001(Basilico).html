What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Do the readings! All of them! It's really easy to skip/skim, but reading these in their entireties will also help you become a much better writer, especially in the social studies style of writing.
This was my favorite Harvard class that I have taken thus far. The course topic could not be more timely. Each week's reading is vastly different but also so related (the booklist is unmatched). Prof. Basilico is a visionary in the field of economics/mental health–it is a great privilege to get to spend 2 hours with 7 peers and him, once a week.

Take this tutorial, no matter what your focus field is, this class is related in some way.

This class easily changed the way in which I approach this world and the communities around me.
This is a good class! If you are interested in anything mental health related, I think you'll like it. There is not a ton of structure to the discussions or the research, but so long as you have some motivation to engage on your own, you'll get a lot out of it. Basilico is super responsive and helpful, but he's pretty hands off, so if you want support on your research project you should reach out and ask for it (I wish I had done more of that).
If you're interested in why mental health or mental healthcare in the USA is the way it is, then you need to take this class. Matt is an AMAZING professor – he brings a wealth of experience from both psychiatry and econ, and he's great at using that experience to facilitate a class discussion. He's also genuinely interested in supporting students and genuinely concerned for our well–being. The syllabus is awesome, but also manageable; I took the class as a non–concentrator and was able to keep up pretty well in discussions despite not having seen most of it before. It was really cool to put "canonical" authors like Durkheim, Foucault, and Fanon in conversation with more modern authors who focus on mental health, like Arthur Kleinman, Anne Becker, Case and Deaton, and Thomas Insel.

One caveat I would mention is that if you're more interested in social theory than you are in mental health, this class may not be for you. We talked about some pretty heavy topics every class (especially suicide), and it can burn you out if you're not prepared to engage with it. As someone who's kind of obsessed with the social environment surrounding mental illness, this was one of my favorite classes I've taken at Harvard, and I loved talking with other people who think about it as much as I do, but it might not be everyone's cup of tea. If it is your cup of tea, though, it's a really good one.
This course gave me the space to understand the complex interplay between the nature of our society and the plight of mental illness. It pushed students to consider how social media or Western culture, or our systems of care could be undermining people's mental health. The instructor, Dr. Basilico, is one of the kindest individuals on campus who is eager to support students in all of their intellectual endeavor's. Matt does this often in the course: using the term paper to give students space to write about whatever might be of interest to them. He's one of the most interesting and kind instructors you can get so take this course!
