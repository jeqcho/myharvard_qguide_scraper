This was a very interesting (and also challenging) class!

Content:

The content of this course is pretty solid and interesting. The first half of the class is relatively easy and covers classical material in Lehmann–Casella (Theory of Point Estimation). For instance, basic concepts such as the MLE, (minimal/complete/) sufficient statistics, UMVUEs, Lehmann–Scheffe, Basu’s theorem, consistency + asymptotic normality of the MLE are introduced and proven. The mathematical rigor is held at a level where mathematical expressions are calculated and theorems are proven properly, with the exception of measure theory, which is wholly ignored. In general, this part of the material is pretty clean (with some occasional Taylor expansion bashes), but motivated and interesting. As a note on measure theory, I think it is fine to avoid this in class (because the goal is to teach inference, not mathematics), but it would be nice to have a reference which one can consult for fully mathematically rigorous proofs.

The second half is more conceptually difficult. It covers classical hypothesis testing (Neyman–Pearson, mixed tests, p–values, sign/sign–rank/permutation tests, asymptotic tests, etc.). Prof. Janson then discusses selective inference, which is the basis of more modern research. The rough idea is that one wants to avoid p–hacking type of results, where one tests many hundreds of hypotheses; therefore, one wants guarantees on quantities such as the false discovery rate (FDR) or the familywise error rate (FWER), both errors based on the number of false rejections. There are some interesting combinatorial–type problems here, and the bounding methods are more conceptual. Then confidence intervals and their relationship with hypothesis tests are discussed; lastly, a brief excursion into Bayesian inference and decision theory is covered. This last part is relatively simple, because much of the difficulty in Bayesian inference comes from the computational aspect (cf: stat 220 as a class which teaches this well). The decision theory part is pretty cool and worth looking at; the complete class theorem is proven.

Overall, the material is definitely interesting and worth learning, as it forms the foundation for any work or understanding of statistics and inference.

Note on Prerequisites:

Understanding of material from stat110/210 should be enough. It is good to know the material in stat111, because this class is relatively theoretical, and familiarity and exposure of the basic ideas in 111 beforehand will allow the concepts taught in 211 to be better put in perspective.

Course Notes + Lectures:

The course notes are very well–written, probably one of the better sets of course notes. Lectures largely go directly off the notes. There is also a ‘recent paper about the topic’ at the end of each small section; these are interesting and give more context to modern research, as this is a graduate course.

Grading:

The grading for this class was somewhat odd and, at times, fairly unreasonable; hence the reason for this (unusual and extra) remark. In fairness, grading a large quantity of extremely ugly proofs (which are unavoidable in a class with an abundance of Taylor expansions) is a difficult task, and understandably there will be occurrences of both errors and personal decisions of the grader which may differ from the understanding of the student. However, I feel that there are several instances where the class grading actively hindered the pedagogical ideology of the course. I will provide an elaboration below.

Mainly, it was not obvious what details to place. The main contribution of stat211, in my view, is a course to learn important ideas in statistical inference, with an acceptable level of rigor. Here, “acceptable” is up to interpretation; however, the lack of inclusion of measure theoretic rigor is a good place to start. In my view, a reasonable sense of rigor is provided by Lucas’s lecturing: the mathematical calculations are written out, while the abstract and long mathematical (such as measure theoretic) details may be avoided. However, in the grading, significant emphasis was often placed on very minor details. For instance, forgetting to cite “uncorrelated => independent”, which is a very standard fact in stat210, was the cause of deducting 2/5 (40%) of points on a problem. There are many other instances of minor typos or omitting relatively simple details being worth an undue percentage of points for the problem (at least 20+%), which I feel like detracts from the ideology of the course.

Exams:

The exams are quite tough, though actually pretty fun. There are 2 problems in both the midterm and the final, which constitutes around 55% of your grade. The problems are actually pretty interesting, and are often motivated by various modern research papers; therefore, the questions being asked are natural. It is probably one of the few instances of exams at Harvard which are genuinely challenging and also provide intellectual value on their own. They are also 16 hours long, so be prepared to spend one long day doing nothing else.

On the other hand, I do have two comments on this structure. First, I think it would be nice to include more than exactly 4 total problems in both exams. Mostly, the consequence of having only 4 problems is that it is literally impossible to have exams which cover a majority of material in the course; for instance, this year, there were 0 exam problems relating to the last two sections of the course (Bayesian methods and decision theory). While there were HW problems on these topics, it would have been nice to have a slightly more cumulative exam. Given around 10–ish sections total, I think even adding 1 more problem per exam and having some problems encompass 2 sections is sufficient.

Second, I appreciate the exams for having intriguing ideas; along these lines, however, it is forced to be relatively open–ended. This resulted in the following issue: problems would sometimes include lines such as, “provide a test satisfying conditions X and Y. Better tests will receive more marks, as long as they all satisfy X and Y”. As a result, it was often unclear whether solutions would receive full points; for instance, it was possible to have a completely correct solution satisfying X and Y and which was morally correct (i.e. accomplished the main task), but did not receive full credit.
While this was generally fine and didn’t have a major impact on grades (which were generally 0+ or 50– anyway), it made it relatively painful whenever one was done with the problems, since one would have to sit the rest of the 16 hours while not being fully certain about the status of their solutions. Moreover, it was annoying to see that solutions which seemed completely correct ended up losing points. While the point deduction may be justified (indeed, there were better solutions available!), it would be nice to have an additional line, just stating what exact level of precision is required. I think that often times, students who would have been able to figure out the full–credit solution end up not doing so, not because of a lack of understanding of material, but rather because they believe their solution is already fully correct.

The story of Statistics 211 is very interesting. If you are an undergrad who is seriously considering the course, it is likely that you are either pursuing the concurrent AM degree in Statistics, or have some other interest in classical frequentist statistical inference, so my response will assume that you will enroll in the course regardless of what I say and instead aim to prepare you for the semester in which you take it. As a result, I will not discuss the course content at length. However, if your motivation for considering the course is not one that I have listed, my recommendation is to stop reading these reviews and instead pick a set of courses that interest you. Note that since I took the course in Fall 2024, many of my comments are directly relevant to the teaching staff of the course in that semester; the professor was Lucas Janson and the course assistants were Kevin Du, Kenneth Gu, and Yuanchuan Guo. For those readers who are skimming the reviews, the course was enjoyable albeit somewhat time–intensive; for those readers who are interested in learning more about the experience, please refer to the below commentary.

First, the positives. As an introductory course in statistical inference, the topics covered must necessarily be accessible to students without a robust mathematical background. In particular, the professor explicitly states that no knowledge of measure theory is assumed, and none of the results in the course are "proven" using such techniques. Thus there are many simplifying assumptions made in lecture that sacrifice rigor for statistical intuition. For instance, though many statistical models of interest are neither discrete nor continuous, this fact is often swept under the rug although a truly rigorous treatment demands the use of measure. Some students who hail from the school of mathematics may view this practice negatively, but since I believe that grasping the big ideas matters far more than bumbling around in the weeds when one is first learning an important and difficult subject, it is fine with me. The course extends on this general goal by providing a set of reasonably detailed lecture notes that closely mirror the content and rigor of material presented in lecture; it is entirely possible to learn the material to the standard the professor requires by reading carefully through the notes and giving all of the pencil problems an honest attempt. For those who have not seen pencil problems before, they are short practice problems, often requiring more thinking than computation, that the professor will sometimes pose during lecture and give the class a short interval of time to consider before reviewing. According to the teaching staff, these notes have been revised and passed down through many iterations of the course (taught by Carl Morris, Joe Blitzstein, Tirthankar Dasgupta, Lucas Janson in chronological order) so their quality is well–assured. Some other classes have lecture notes or companion textbooks, but these are especially recommended because of their clarity and adherence to lecture content. Lucas also provides a detailed schedule of topics in his syllabus, so when reading the notes to review material it is easy to see how much is left to learn to be up to date. One might argue that having such a comprehensive set of notes would decrease the incentive for students to attend lecture especially since my iteration of the course had lectures at 9am on Mondays and Wednesdays which is not a favorable time for sleepy university students, but Lucas posts them sequentially as he covers the material. In fact, attending lecture on a regular basis is a definite benefit in this course because Lucas delivers the material with clarity and poise, so you will rarely become irreparably confused, conditioned on the event that you are awake and have been actively engaging with the material. Some may argue that he adheres too closely to the lecture notes, but given that he is the most recent author of the notes and they provide a lot of motivation, this is actually more of a pro than a con. Moreover, he has a feedback form at the end of each lecture that students are encouraged to fill out; there students can request another passthrough of the material and he will most likely fulfill the request in the subsequent lecture. Generally I am extremely appreciative of this style of lecturing and material availability since for students who do not especially enjoy the professor's delivery or for some reason must miss lecture, the course can still be learned; it is clear that Lucas cares a lot about his students learning the material and takes intentional measures to make the course accessible for everyone. In office hours, he will of course not give solutions to the homework (and indeed he should not), but often it is more than worth attending to discuss the ideas presented in lecture or homework and learn the perspective of a qualified statistician. For these reasons alone, although Lucas will not be teaching the course in Fall 2025, as long as the new professor is not absurdly bad (this will not be the case, given the high quality of the Harvard statistics department) it is more than worth taking.

Second, the gray area. In terms of workload, this course has five homework assignments and two exams, where in my year the weighting for cumulative numerical score was given by the following formula: (0.45 homework average, 0.25 midterm, 0.30 final). There are 48 late hours that can be used on homework assignments in case of emergency; to my knowledge, there is no intermediate cap on the number of late hours that one is allowed to use per assignment. Each homework is around five problems, each with several parts, and students are given approximately two weeks to complete each assignment from the day that it is released. In most cases, all of the material covered on the homework is presented in lecture by the time that the homework is released, so it is feasible to start working immediately if you have been properly following the pace of the course. Some might recommend that you do this since waiting until the last few days could leave rigor errors in your work, but if you are collaborating with classmates it is difficult to align everyone's schedules so early; moreover, sometimes the problem statements are not worded very clearly, so it is more time–efficient to start later because the situation in which you have done a lot of work but misinterpreted the question should be avoided. In fact, for this reason the course of discussion is one of the few courses in which a delayed start strategy is close to optimal. Each of the homework assignments attempts to follow up on a set of concepts that has been presented in lecture, which in theory serves an appreciable pedagogical use because a deeper understanding of the material can be achieved, which may be especially valuable down the road for statistics learners since real–world settings probably do not permit nice assumptions. However, in practice the homework problems often take the form of "relax assumption A and show that theorem B still holds" or perhaps "perform modification B to statistical testing procedure Y and show that it achieves the same guarantee as the original" and other variants along those lines. Since the original results that the homework assignments will slightly amend and ask you to prove are probably all described to some reasonable depth in lecture (if this were not the case, the problems would go beyond the scope of the course), the method of solution that is endorsed by the staff frequently reduces to imitating the work that has already been performed in the lecture notes. The homework is therefore rather tedious to complete and especially tedious to write up; there is often more algebraic bashing than statistical insight. It would be more interesting for students if the course covered why these modifications and related generalizations work, but for this sort of explanation it is perhaps best to engage with the professor during office hours. Indeed, one of the strongest mixed feelings I have about this course is that the lectures are very elegant, and the results in the homework are as well to some extent, but the process by which one arrives at these conclusions is far from it. However, this is perhaps to be expected since statistical inference as a field often involves messy computation, and it should be noted that at the conclusion of every lecture, Lucas makes a respectable effort to impart deeper motivation by providing in the lecture notes references to modern statistical papers that cover related topics. These papers are interesting, at least judging from the ones that I briefly read, and students are not expected to know them, so if you are unsatisfied by the learning that the homework attempts to impart, you can read them at no cost. However, you probably do not care that much about the homework since most Harvard students spend a lot of time on their homework and do fairly well as a result. As I have been in your shoes, you likely care more about your letter grade, and thus you want to learn about the factor that determines the curve. You want to learn about the exams.

Third, the negatives. I will preface this section by saying that no course is perfect; in fact, Lucas has done a terrific job with the course this semester, which is consistent with what I have heard from students in previous years. As you read, please keep in mind that I will give only honest feedback that does not detract from my overall positive impression of the course. Now I will continue from where I paused earlier. The exams in Statistics 211 are take–home, and students are given 16 hours within a several–day window to complete them; the exams used to be 24 hours in length but were shortened. In my year, there was a midterm around the first week of November and a final around the start of finals week in December. Obviously, collaboration is not allowed. Each exam contains two proof–based problems, each of which contains anywhere from zero to several subparts. These are inherently difficult because you are permitted to use a wealth of time and the course materials; many students will spend multiple hours making minimal progress and will submit either complete solutions or completely incorrect attempts. The format of these exams introduces nontrivial stress since four problems determine your grade in the course, and even though there may be multiple parts to a problem, in my year the solutions required similar ideas so if you did not get the first part you were not likely to get the others. Evidently your performance on these exams can have high variance; note also that the course is mandatory for G1 Statistics PhD students who are incentivized to score well, so as an undergraduate receiving an A can be an arduous task. Before taking the exams, it is recommended that you spend considerable time reviewing the lecture notes as well as practice problems (homework and additional) that the professor gives, since knowing both the main ideas at a high level and how they may show up in problems goes a long way in solving the exam under stress. I am conflicted on whether having such long take–home exams is a good idea in principle, since one could make the argument that in a real research setting, progress is highly nonlinear and attempting problems with more time is a closer imitation than spending only a few hours in an exam room. Some other courses ask students to complete final projects instead of final exams, but that is not preferable to the current regime in Statistics 211 since the course material is very difficult to learn rigorously; most undergrads would not be able to conduct nontrivial research in their first semester covering it. In fact, the research setting interpretation of the exams may indeed be correct since in my year most of the problems were incredibly open–ended, with the problem statements saying that more points would be awarded to solutions that "were better" by a specified metric; in particular, one question asked students to make necessary assumptions and implied that correct solutions with weaker assumptions would receive more points, despite the course not covering what different approaches might look like. This is one obvious point of criticism. Anecdotally, there were several cases on exams in which student solutions that were comparable to the staff solution in rigor and correctness received substantial point deductions, and the given reason was that stated above. This grading scheme seems inherently flawed in that students cannot gauge whether their answer is satisfactory despite meeting the explicit requirements of the problem statement, and if one wants to assess genuine understanding of the material, it seems far more pedagogically valuable to award correct ideas that achieve slightly suboptimal statistical guarantees compared to the staff solution full or close–to–full credit, rather than deducting 1/5 or more points, where other solutions with incorrect ideas or largely missing rigor are not deducted substantially more. Under this grading scheme where students are compared to the staff solution, one would expect that a student that achieves stronger guarantees than the staff solution should receive more than full marks; is such behavior reasonable? As an aside, a consequence of having only four problems on exams was that much of the content in the latter third of the course was not tested, including Bayesian inference and decision theory, but since a student who takes the course should be learning all of the material anyway, this is not major. The other remark I have is regarding the course staff aside from the professor; not all of the course assistants exhibited this behavior, but based on the language of the comments left on solutions, with extremely high probability there was a nonnegative number of course staff who consistently acted unreasonably (out of respect for individual privacy I will not give the true number). Reasonably often, points were deducted from homework (and exams, but I have already discussed this to an extent) for incorrect reasons. Often, the penalized solutions either exactly matched those given in the staff solutions or did not cite results that were shown and henceforth assumed to be true in previous courses that Statistics 211 assumes as a prerequisite. One example is "uncorrelated implies independent in the Multivariate Normal setting" which is proven in Statistics 210. `Wait a second, that isn't the title of the course? What happened to "Stat 211: Statistical Inference I" ?' you may ask. Well, that is part of the trap, because this is precisely what the course wants you to think it is. It commences unassumingly, with a beautiful first lecture on statistical philosophy and the inferential framework; the first few lectures continue on relatively unassumingly, with excellent motivation and intuition provided in class and beautiful results covered. Then the first problem set lands and you realise the true form of the evil in front of you, and from then on you realise that you are better off trying to purchase the Mirror of Erised to survive the assignments than trying to do them in any reasonable conventional manner (including nitpicking AI generated answers, as helpfully pointed out by Professor Janson). To explain a little bit – maybe it is just the nature of statistical inference research, but a very large number of problems in this course involve a 'come up with a ... that satisfies (insert laundry list) of properties and accomplishes ... task' with very few clues from the lectures, lecture notes or texts that give you a starting point or intuitions on where to proceed with this type of problem, so when you are left to your devices and expected to find out from the (often vague) prompt what exactly it is you have to do it could often feel like you are tasked with performing black magic. Like any good school of magic, Hogwarts has over the years perfected a rigorous curriculum to train its students in various forms of magic, and of course has OWL and NEWT exams in subjects such as Potions (Stat 110), Charms (Stat 111) and even Defence in the Dark Arts (Stat 210) to prepare young wizards and witches from taking on real–world threats – it just unfortunately happens to be the case here that taking on the Stat 211 exams right after successful completion of these three courses is akin to what one would feel like battling Lord Voldemort and his army of Death Eaters fresh out of your NEWTS – while it is the case that there are a few Harry Potters around in the course that are able to survive this monumental task unscathed, there are many who have prepared and understood the content well who horribly fall victim to this terrible slaughtering.
