Matthew's lectures are very rambly and difficult to follow. David's lectures are fantastic (shame he only taught 1/3 of the course)
One major strength of this course is its deep integration of microeconomic theory, game theory, and probability/statistics, particularly in the expanded sections developed by Professor Rabin. This semester, over two–thirds of the material came from Rabin’s contributions, which were both intellectually challenging and highly rewarding. The problem sets initially felt quite difficult, but working through them with classmates and attending TA Ophir’s excellent review sessions greatly enhanced my understanding. I strongly recommend forming study groups, as collaborative discussion really helped clarify complex concepts. The green “expanded” slides, especially in the optional exam sections, are incredibly rich with insights—reviewing them carefully was invaluable for consolidating knowledge. The final exam, while slightly simpler than the homework in conceptual depth, was still quite intense (e.g., 5 problems and up to 16 subparts in 3 hours), so time management was crucial. Overall, this course not only improved my technical skills but also gave me the confidence to approach behavioral economics papers with far less fear. It’s a demanding but truly worthwhile course—don’t be intimidated. And special credit to TA Ophir, who was outstanding throughout the semester.
The topics are fascinating. If you want to get better or get more exposure on micro theory, you should take it. Also, the professors are incredibly insightful and great at giving feedback on research proposals.
Do not take a course with Matthew Rabin. I cannot emphasize that enough. He is a wonderful person and a great economist, but a bad teacher in every way. He rambles in lecture and does not explain most things clearly. His problem sets are badly designed, take too long, and have not much pedagogical value — they are often quite disjoint from lecture.

David Laibson is amazing — I took this course hoping to learn behavioral economics from him, and it turned out that he was forced to give 2/3 of the course to Matthew so that Matthew could satisfy a teaching requirement. Three hour lectures of Matthew Rabin are very hard to sit through.

IN SUMMARY: This is the only class at Harvard that I regret taking.
This course is an excellent and comprehensive overview skimming major topics in behavioral economics, from an economics lens. Be ready to work through challenging readings and problem sets, but doing so will be rewarding.
As with many graduate–level courses, I would recommend taking this class only if you have a clear sense of your research interests in economics. That said, if you are at all interested in behavioral economics, this course is a must. You’ll be learning foundational theories from the very people who helped develop them.

There are certainly ways the course could be improved, but most of these feel like second–order concerns. What matters more is that the class provides an excellent platform to explore key ideas in behavioral economics and to ask meaningful questions.

I highly recommend doing the readings—they are essential for fully understanding the material. I also strongly encourage students to ask questions during class. Both professors are extremely generous with their responses, and I personally learned more from their answers than from the lecture slides themselves.

This course is especially valuable for students who want to build a strong foundation in behavioral modeling, and who are comfortable with microeconomic theory at the level of a solid undergraduate course.
The lecture format needs some work (three hours with no breaks is just too long) and can get a bit bogged down in the material. But Laibson and Rabin are both great to work with, which makes the class worthwhile.
