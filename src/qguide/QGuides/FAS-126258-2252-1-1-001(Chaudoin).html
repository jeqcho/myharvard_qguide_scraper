Read the assigned readings—often times theses papers breakdown the theory we will be discussing in class; it is incredibly helpful to have that background. Do not miss the test reviews, they are very helpful.
While this class is organized very poorly, it's a great introductory government class. Chaudoin loves what he does, making his lectures engaging and interactive. Definitely a lighter workload (1–2 readings per class), however homework assignments ramp up out of the blue toward the end of the semester. There's no midterm or final, however there are two tests that feel like such. Make sure to study hard for these, they're weighted heavily. The section component is helpful in maintaining the material taught in lecture, however involves a lot of active participation. Lastly, be patient in trying to find where the readings are/where you're supposed to submit homework... the Canvas page is a mess lol. Have fun!
Take this course if you like game theory and international relations. The HW and Readings are very manageable. Chaudoin is a great lecturer, however, he does not prep you for exams much. He can also act like a middle school teacher sometimes if the class or someone makes him upset, which can be irritating.
Overall, this course was ok. I feel the number assignments/ tests we had was the perfect amount. However, this class is heavy on game theory and less on what international relations actually is. Also, at times it felt that a specific word or wording was more important than trying to just understand and explain the content, which was annoying. With that being said I think the final grade breakdown was fair and it's a good enough class to get your IR requirement.
I haven’t come away from this course with abundant knowledge about certain topics I originally wanted to explore. The course focuses heavily on theory instead of content and history.

Professor Chaudoin is amazing.
Take this class if you are interested in a Gov concentration or international relations. It's a great way to fulfil a requirement as it's fairly interesting and, in my opinion, not hard at all. I barely spent anytime outside of class and still did really well on the tests, and the homework assignments are not too difficult. You just have to be able to follow instructions well and apply concepts flexibly.

It is a pretty unique class though so don't expect a typical gov class – it's much more focused on models and social scientific concepts than actual IR. i'm not sure if I learned a lot of concrete things. But on balance I would take it again as it's pretty easy and still interesting.
This class is not what I expected it to be. Yes, we talked about wars, but the focus was more on models, theories, and predictions than the actual wars and conflicts between states.
Take this class with reservations. It's a great intro to more statistical gov concepts and war bargaining, which is not advertised as it states it's a class on international relations. If you're down to do some math and learn about the costs of war and want to fulfill that gov credit, this class is for you. However, make sure to take it a little more seriously. It may feel like Stephen is amazing at lecture and he's throwing jokes, making lecture fun and all, but in reality he's lowkey a harsh grader and is looking for very very specific answers. Other than that take it, but pls pls lock in the from the beginning. Shout out to Dana, best TF, she helped so much on exams and homework assignments, get her in section, she's so sweet and kind.

I would definitely advise you to practice with the models and engage in class.
The course provided an excellent introduction to the theoretical aspects of international relations, and I appreciate the study of war models and financial systems throughout the world in a general manner. It is quite a difficult task to cover such a broad spectrum, and I believe this course hits on several key aspects without getting too bogged down in specific historical events in international relations. This course changed me in that I realize that I need to focus less on specific historical events and make broader connections between occurrences in the field of international relations, in order to demonstrate my knowledge of "the ways of the world."

However, the class sometimes lacks in in–depth, concrete explanations of certain models (war, financial systems, etc.), which, if included more substantially, could help students in the course grasp the logic of these models. Possibly, a more evident incorporation of specific historical events that exemplify these models during lecture could benefit students' understanding of the generalities of the course. I do realize, however, that analyzing specific events takes a lot of time, which may not be possible for this course's tight schedule in covering such a wide variety of topics.
