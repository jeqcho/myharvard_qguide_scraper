TL;DR: I will describe this class using a Hoare triple.
{Happy} CS 2540 {Sad}

Now onto the details.

This class is bad. Like, really bad. I wish “CS2540 says I speaksfor CS2540” so that I could make CS2540 say all the problems about itself. I want to robustly declassify and transparently endorse the pain of this class to the rest of the world while maintaining nonmalleable information flow.

Starting with the lectures. They are not informative. On a lattice, it feels like Steve’s lectures are secret untrusted and I am public trusted with how difficult it is to make the lecture information flow to my brain. But when you ask a question, it flips; you suddenly become secret untrusted and public trusted as you can’t get any meaningful questions to Steve.

It feels like Steve assumes too much during lectures. The prerequisites that he states (Good CS student and taken Formal Reasoning before) is nowhere enough as Steve will sometimes just skip over details and assume you know them when he has never taught them before. This creates problems; eventually he will just lose you unless you have studied this topic before, which is unfair to the students who took the listed prerequisites in good faith. Even with some knowledge of PL, a lot of his lectures are indecipherable. Steve says HisLectures speaksfor Steve, but the problem is that HisLectures says Indecipherable speaksfor HisLectures.

Onto the homework. Why do they exist? What is the line between a “check” and a “check–plus” grade on the homework? Nobody knows. The line is so nonexistent that it will make you question whether you know the line using epistemic logic. What world are you in? Are you in a world where the line is clear? The homeworks say no. Are you in a world where you know that the homeworks are meaningful? The homeworks say no. In fact, using epistemic logic, you will eventually come to the conclusion that K_{You}(CS 2540 is a bad class). Of course, the only way you will know this is if you experience what set of worlds you may be in by taking the class, but I hope that this observation is enough for you.

In fact, the class can be written in Tamarin like this. [CS2540()]––[]–>[Out(BadClass)]. Which, speaking of, is the worst homework I have experienced. No instructions, just pain. No practice in class, just pain. Steve gives you zero preconditions, and somehow expects your postcondition to contain happiness of working with Tamarin. Give us those preconditions. But given that the prerequisite list is terrible, is it a surprise that the preconditions are lacking?

The project. Nothing but pain. Lack of instructions. Lack of rubrics. Lack of basis on grading. It’s so arbitrary that you can’t ever know if Steve is grading fairly or not. In fact, it seems likely that he isn’t. “Steve will always grade fairly” is a safety property, and if Execution Monitoring tried to enforce this property on this class, the class would have been canceled as soon as Steve began to grade. “Steve will eventually make good lectures for CS2540” is a liveness property, but it remains unsatisfied. No well–founded relation can be found to save this class.

If you are still considering taking this course, think of a model of CS2540 that we will reason with. Reasoning through, we find that the class is vulnerable to a side channel. Through the pain it inflicts on its students, a side channel characterized by the stress of its students is created. Even when you have exploited this side channel, why would you try to experience a class of this exploitable model yourself? CS2540 and a class where you struggle and don’t learn much are contextually equivalent.

To this end, remember that you have your secrets. With how public the pain of CS2540 is, if you want to enforce noninterference, you should not take this course and begin the transfer of information from a secret source to a public source. You might think you are going to take a fun formal reasoning course, but you have actually been exploited by a Man in the Middle Attack. You are communicating with a terrible class that is pretending to be a good formal reasoning course that has purposefully set up a Needham–Schroeder protocol to abuse this attack after throwing away Lowe from its protocol.

Do not take this class. 1/5.
