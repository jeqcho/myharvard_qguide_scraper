ES152!!!!!!!!! Gage Hills!!!!! This class and Prof Hills are a legend for a reason. TAKE IT.

If you're taking ES152 without experience in EE before (lol me) you're probably a bit terrified. Here I am to say: YOU CAN DO THIS AND YOU WILL LOVE IT AND HAVE SO MUCH FUN. Never have I had a prof so engaging, interested in the material, and really accommodating to ensure you can fully understand the class's materials. Yes, ES152 is pretty hard and will take up some time –– BUT IT'S AWESOME (like I made a filter for a speaker to play Ed Sheeran, 'twas awesome). If you're considering EE, ES152 is one of the first steps. In fact, it was the first step for me, and it completely validated my interest in the concentration.

You've got this, go do it EE youngling!!!! :)
This is a fantastic class! It’s definitely a lot of work—sometimes challenging—but overall, it’s thoughtfully designed to help you learn the material effectively. The structure encourages you to engage with the teaching staff through various avenues like Slack, office hours, and sections. While attending office hours is optional, I believe it’s a crucial part of the class experience. If you’re a bit of an introvert like me, you might miss out on some valuable insights from direct conversations with Gage, but please try as that is part of the class design (fortunately or unfortunately depending on who you are). Don’t hesitate to Slack Gage and ask questions—he is incredibly helpful!

For problem sets, it’s easy to fall behind because they can be quite lengthy. Try to stay on track, as keeping pace with the class allows you to collaborate with friends, attend office hours, and benefit from section discussions. These often cover portions of the problem set, which can be incredibly helpful—something you’ll miss if you fall behind.

The labs are awesome and super fun! To make the most of your time, go through the lab assignments beforehand and complete any tasks that don’t require lab equipment. This will save you time and help you get as much done as possible during the lab session.

Lastly, if you haven’t taken ES 50 or don’t have much soldering experience, practice ahead of time! The soldering competition is a fun and rewarding part of the course.
Take this class! Gage makes lectures so much fun while teaching the class in a way that everyone is brought along together. He almost always quickly understands students' questions, making his responses thorough and very effective at clearing up confusion. He creates a great culture in the classroom, so I actually look forward to attending class. Psets can take a lot of time, but just ask anyone in the class to do it with them, bc it is much better to do them as a group/with help. The class covers a lot of material but you will learn so much and you will feel very accomplished at the end of the semester. Also, labs can occasionally be a bit tricky but staff is very helpful and will get you through sticking points.
One of my favorite classes I have taken so far; a great and quite extensive introduction to analog circuit elements! This course covers a TON of content, so do be warned, it is a major time commitment (~10 hours spent on problem sets, ~4 hours spent on labs outside of lab)... Without further ado, my minimally organized thoughts:

Gage Hills is a legend (despite using ~5 slides of his 100–slide lecture presentations in class) and always available on Slack; circuit measurement devices and LTSpice simulation software are your friends; it is possible to fry an op–amp; transistors are basically magic; the frequency domain and phasors are incredible handy tools for circuit analysis; lonely boy; breadboard circuits may not compare to integrated circuits in terms of efficiency but are quite cool to build yourself; some breadboard wiring experience is recommended (but can be learned on the fly if necessary); I am not built for soldering; all the assignments are take–home (problem sets, the midterm, the final) with the exception of the labs which are done partially in scheduled lab, partially in unscheduled lab, and the rest at home; midterm questions can be negotiable; deadlines can be negotiable; the final is not negotiable; TomHenry will carry you through the problem sets; there's a fine line between analog and digital circuits... in fact analog circuits typically lie on the line while digital circuits use either extreme; definitely take if you 0) have the time 1) have even a little interest in circuits and devices 2) want a fantastic experience with only a little suffering!
Inspired by a previous year's anonymous post: I have lots of thoughts about this class, but I'll keep it short:

This class is peak.*

* = Professor Hills is what makes this class shine. Please know that this class is probably the most dense class you will ever take at Harvard, and that is not an exaggeration. This class needs to be split up into multiple classes (perhaps the title of this course has been telling us something). For example, I want you to go and look at the Plan of Study document for Electrical Engineering if you haven't already, and look at the POS doc for Mechanical Engineering. MechE has a lot of core classes but they're split up into digestable courses. I'm not sure why this class isn't split up, but I can almost certainly say on behalf of the students in my class, that we as students would learn and retain a lot more if we spent our time going over what are the most important topics in all of EE (IMO). But yeah, it's peak.
