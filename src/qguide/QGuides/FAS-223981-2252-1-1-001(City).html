I really wanted to like this class, but unfortunately I left very disappointed. I came into it with high hopes given the course feedback the previous year and that it was run by members of the graduate school of education. However there were so many elements that fell short that I would definitely not recommend anyone take this class unless significant changes are made for next year.

For one, the class was extremely disorganized. Despite there only being 3 major writing assignments, we wouldn't get clear instructions for them until pretty much a couple days before they were due. Even worse, when asking for clarification on the structure of the assignments, different TFs would provide drastically different info, creating a large amount of confusion.

In regard to the non writing assignments, the lack of clarity was just as bad. Many of them, such as the teach–in, felt like poorly planned afterthoughts. It would have benefitted us if there was some sort of guidance on how these assignments connected to the course, but instead they felt like it felt like performative busywork. The total time commitment for these assignments was way too large for the practical learning benefit, making many components feel extremely frustrating and tedious.

The same critique goes for many of the readings. In isolation, the readings were on the whole very well chosen, however little was done to place them in context and to help ensure that we could understand the value of the readings as this was seldom discussed in class. This made performing the readings feel pointless, especially given the length and time commitment for many of them. In addition, I felt that many of the readings leaned too philosophical, and there were not enough informational readings. As someone who did not have strong background knowledge of the US education system, I felt that I didn't have the prerequisite knowledge to engage deeply enough with a lot of the readings, and as a result I didn't really learn anything from them.

The lack of actual information being provided is a criticism I have for the lectures as well. Often times we would be paired into discussion groups to talk about a topic, but we would not be provided with sufficient background information or context about the topic to have a meaningful and productive discussion. Given that this is a Gen–Ed, it seems obvious that there would be a wide range of different levels of background knowledge, but it felt like very little thought was put in to considering these topics from the average student's level of knowledge. Many of the lecture activities also felt completely pointless and were structured super unclearly as well, contributing to the issue of disorganization and lack of clarity.
