I recommend this class for people who want an introductory course about sports analytics. We learned a lot, about different methods of analysis, sports and relevant stats and metrics, and just how it all works. However, the course was at times not organized very well. Pset were posted late (though we always had at least a week to complete it). Sections and office hours were frequently cancelled or moved by TFs, which was not helpful at all. I showed up numerous times to a section (with other students), just for a TF to not be present (various TFs). We got 3 of 6 psets graded by the end of the semester, and these were not graded until right before the midterm. Practice test/study materials were posted much later than promised as well. I sometimes really struggled to understand the feedback from TFs as well. However, it was the first year this course was being offered, so these delays are understandable. The content was still fascinating, and the final project assignment was particularly engaging. I found the psets manageable most of the time, but sometimes they took a lot longer than anticipated, or had weird issues with the data that were not anticipated when the pset was created, or typos in the source code that caused problems down the line. Ed was a useful tool, especially as other students often helped answer questions when the course staff did not. Overall a fascinating course, and Kevin was clearly very passionate, and I hope that in future years it finds its footing a bit better.

Be careful about taking this course due mostly to its organization. The course was very disorganized this semester. Part of this may have been because this was the first time this course was offered, but it was still unacceptable even for that standard. The professor would always claim that a homework assignment would be released "tonight" or "tomorrow morning", just for the assignment to be nowhere to be found until many days later. So you really won't know when the homework will be released or when it will be due. One time the professor claimed a homework would be released on a Friday and due the next Friday before spring break, just for the homework to not be released until the next Tuesday morning, and even then he kept the homework due that Friday (so no weekend to work on it) until the entire class protested and he extended the due date. These are just some examples of the poor organizational structure of the course which limits it before discussing any of the content.

The content of this course is sometimes interesting if you're interested in sports, though often times this course was more focused on teaching 139/149 level (based on reading their course descriptions) concepts than actually teaching sports analytics, so we didn't get to cover as much of the actual substance of sports topics. I think that if this level of knowledge (logistic regression, model selection, random forests, etc) is required to do sports analytics at even an introductory level, then the class should just have these as prereqs. Walking into this class with just a Stat100 background leaves you incredibly confused on these parts and is not very reasonable for a second course in statistics, especially if you're not a concentrator and just interested in sports. I would say a Stat 104 or Stat 111 background at minimum is required to do well in this class.

Homework assignments were quite long and tedious but could often be completed with lecture and/or lab notes, as well as ChatGPT for assistance. But again, you never knew when the homework would be released, so you may have had to do it at a time of the week when you were busy.

The class had one midterm in mid April split between an in class portion and a take home portion. The in class portion was quite difficult and most students did not do very well since again it focused more on 139–level topics than actual sports analytics, leaving many confused. The take home portion was equivalent to a pset and seemed a bit redundant since it did not test any concepts that weren't on psets. The only difference was that you were not allowed to use ChatGPT on the take home while you could on the psets, though anyone can see the problem with this. Regardless the take home could also be completed mostly with lecture notes and looking at old pset solutions.

The course presentation gave you a chance to complete a group project in an area you enjoyed, though feedback was limited and expectations were not entirely clear.

I'd say you should be cautious in taking this course unless some massive changes are made; you may be better off taking a class such as ECON 1042 instead to learn similar topics with less of a burden on you. If your background preparation is too limited such as just Stat 100, you'll struggle a lot with the statistical methods in this class. But also, it seems that if you've taken a class like 139, a lot of the material is just redundant and you don't get to spend as much time on the actual substance of sports analytics. So the class occupies a very weird middle ground and would be much better off either gutting the more advanced statistical concepts and making it accessible to more people, or instead making 139 a prereq so that the class can focus on the substance. So it really doesn't make sense for anyone to take for this reason in its current form, in addition to the organizational issues discussed earlier. I'd take a look at the syllabus that the professor releases on the Canvas page and see if anything has changed in the last two years, especially regarding organization (e.g., are the pset/exam release and due dates clearly posted?).
