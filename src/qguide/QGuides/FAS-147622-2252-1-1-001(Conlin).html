This course is great for those interested in both ASL and linguistics! unfortunately it is not great if you only have background in one, or if you have more than introductory knowledge in linguistics, or if you have less than strong knowledge in ASL. it is disorganized and you'll get the most out of it if you do the readings, which are not always posts. if you have the passion, take the class! but the best part is doing the readings. lectures can be very very slow.
This class was fun, but it was very poorly organized. Although the course requirements indicate that students need to know either ASL or linguistics, I wouldn't recommend taking the course if you don't have experience with both. Don't bother to look at the syllabus because it does not reflect the actual workload. According to the syllabus, we were to have twelve journals, three quizzes, and two exams. We had six journals, two quizzes, and one exam. The content of the class is either very surface level introduction to Deaf culture (not helpful for the ASL students) or surface level linguistics (not helpful to the LING students). Overall, the class was enjoyable, but it was so poorly organized that I can't recommend it without warning. If you are a person that likes structured course with clear goals, this is not the class for you. If you are pretty go–with–the–flow, you might enjoy it. The professors are very kind and they are really excited about the content, which is nice. All the students, ASL and LING, are struggling to understand at some point or another. If that's your vibe, go for it.
You can take this class with just an introductory linguistics course as your background, but really you would get the most out of it if you've taken a few of the other core linguistics classes like 102, 104, or 106. It is also best if you know a bit of ASL or some sign language. Without that background, you may get incorrect ideas of some of the broader linguistic topics like morphology, syntax, or semantics, because they do not introduce them clearly or in a way accessible to people with no experience in them. Also the three professors disagree sometimes which is interesting, but can be really confusing if you don't even have a basis in the material.

(For people who don't know, this class met with ASL VI which was technically taught by Nozomi Tomita. Thus we had three professors for it.)

I loved this class because it is a topic that has fascinated me for many years now. I was thrilled by every reading and every class discussion was quite fun.

If you care about your grades or need more structure, this class may be stressful. They do not care much about grading things and are often disorganized even in assigning work. You may have a homework due one day and you don't even have the prompt to the homework. It is rather frustrating, also because only one of the professors ever even responded to emails.

I recommend this class as an elective later in a linguistics concentration or secondary ideally, once you understand how the process of linguistics works and don't just expect to be taught how things are, if that makes sense.
