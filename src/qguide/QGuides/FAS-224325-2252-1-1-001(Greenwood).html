If you're interested in learning about rhetoric/its history & evolution, take this class! Prof. Greenwood is an incredibly engaging lecturer and she consistently encourages discussion which I really appreciated. To be clear, though, this class will not teach you how to be a better public speaker per se but more so the ways in which rhetoric has evolved and how it is perceived modern day. There is a lot of freedom with the paper assignments—sometimes too much in my opinion, where there isn't really clear instruction and then grading can feel a bit arbitrary. But overall, I would definitely recommend this class, whether or not you are a Classics concentrator or have taken other classes in the department before.
One of the best classes I have ever taken in my eight semesters here, no doubt. If you are at all interested in rhetoric and speeches (as you should be!) but want a more academic and historical treatment than Expos 40 can offer (or you didn't get into Expos 40), you should take it. Prof. Greenwood is one of the most phenomenal lecturers I have had the pleasure to experience: ridiculously knowledgeable and prepared, always caring and kind, and very encouraging. If you are scared off by the prospect of Latin and Greek, do not fret: you absolutely don't need to know any of it but you will certainly leave the class appreciating just how much ancient Greece and Rome have influenced rhetoric and culture in the U.S., Europe, Africa, and beyond.

We were a small group of about 20–30 students from different Harvard schools which was really incredible, as people had so many different experiences and thoughts to share with the class. For the sake of the experience of the students who take the class, I really hope that it stays this way, although at the same time I want everyone to take this class.
Dr. Greenwood is really incredible. Even if you plan to never do any public speaking in your life, this class helps you decipher the rhetoric of others.
A wonderful comparative and open–ended approach to the study of rhetoric. The fun of this class is its exploratory nature, which always leads to unexpected discussions of contemporary rhetoric applied in the context of classical rhetorical theory from Cicero to Quintilian to Aristotle. This course was very accessible, even as someone not concentrating in gov/social studies/classics studies. Most importantly, this course provides you with a critical lens backed by centuries of rhetorical theory to confront the abundance of rhetoric that we are flooded with in our modern–day attention economy.
This class is thoroughly okay.

Pros:
Greenwood is an excellent speaker, very articulate, great for a class on rhetoric. She is understanding, accessible, entertaining to listen to, and has interesting life experiences.
The work (weekly discussion posts, midterm, two papers) was quite manageable and I learned a lot from doing them. Though, I find the two papers too open ended; the class did not directly teach the skills needed for them.
The original speeches you read are great to learn from and compare.

Cons:
Discussions consisted of each student monologuing vaguely and with hand wavy terms with not much interaction.
Though we did not shy away from controversial questions which is great, there did seem to a "correct" answer that could only be qualified.
Overall very vague words with muddled definitions (if defined at all).
You don't ACTUALLY learn rhetoric, as in, how to speak persuasively in a given situation. This is rather looking at rhetoric from a third person perspective.
This was my first Classics course. I was looking for an intellectually stimulating elective for senior spring, and this was the perfect fit. Professor Greenwood is brilliant and innovative. She is so accessible and makes an inclusive classroom environment. Joe Biden’s senior advisor, Michael Donilon, visited our class one day during a lecture on American political rhetoric.
Professor Greenwood is excellent. She is a compelling lecturer and gave an outstanding overview of the history of rhetoric. The course uses interesting themes that challenged me to think about rhetoric in new ways. Definitely a great introduction to classics, politics, and literature.
This course is eminently fascinating and a must–take for anyone interested in the rhetoric and discourse of our times. Whether you are a classicist hoping to venture into contemporary applications or a non–classicist interested in political rhetoric, this course will shape your understanding of the topic on a theoretical and practical level. Professor Greenwood's lectures a eminently fascinating—it is actually amazing how *much* she knows about not just her field but any of the topics we broach. The essay assignments and readings are thought–provoking, so I recommend really investing into the stakes of those tasks. TAKE!
Take this class for an incredible lecture experience—Professor Greenwood is—appropriately—a master rhetorician, and each class flew by. She is a very warm person with an incredible work ethic. Readings are very manageable, as are weekly discussion posts. The only warning I have is that she is a strict grader. Her feedback can be overly critical and excessive (a full page or more or notes). While her comments were always interesting and showed her passion for teaching and the material, it felt inappropriately nitpicky given that this is an undergraduate elective. The comments were much more suited to a graduate course or a tutorial. Also, her expectations were not always made clear—I recommend going to office hours several times with drafts to ensure the best possible outcome.
Professor Greenwood's knowledge and charm make lectures enjoyable. A background in the Classics is recommended but not necessary for taking this course. This course isn't a "how–to" for Rhetoric like Expos 40, but instead explores interesting questions about what rhetoric actually is.
Professor Greenwood is the most amazing professor I have had at Harvard. TAKE THIS CLASS. She is brilliant and you will learn so much. Really great way to explore the classics if you are not a concentrator (no greek or latin necessary) and the subject requires memorization and deep learning which was so refreshing at Harvard. I cannot speak highly enough of Professor Greenwood and her brilliance.
