Lovely class. Professor Dymek is so enthusiastic and interesting. Neuropoetics isn't a huge field so some classes are more interdisciplinary than others, but it's all interesting discussion. The final paper is very flexible so you can write on a topic of your choosing.
This is a great class! Professor Dymek was so lovely and encouraged open discussion and collaborative learning. If you're intimidated by humanities but love the sciences this is a great class to take!
TAKE THIS CLASS. Although there isn't as much focus on actual poetry (if ur background is more poetry inclined) and I didn't realize it would be heavy on psycholinguistics so just be prepared for that lens. Anne is amazing and she really cares about the subject matter
EVERYONE SHOULD TAKE THIS CLASS!!!!!!!!!!!! Anne is the most empathetic, intelligent, and interesting professor that you will have during your time at Harvard. This class is very much her brain child, and it covers topics from so many different fields. I came into this class with a literary background and left with the ability to write a neuroscience research paper that discussed linguistics and philosophy as well as the dorsal medial prefrontal cortex. Take this class if you want to think about why we find language beautiful.
What a delightful, unusual interdisciplinary course! Anne is a wonderful and hilarious professor who really cares about the subject. Everyone in the course had very different backgrounds academically and artistically, so we had really interesting discussions, and everyone's super friendly so we bonded throughout the semester. The assignment structure and readings could be a bit unpredictable, but I learned a lot, and talking about poetry and philosophy in the dark academia Barker Center room was always a highlight of my week. Would strongly recommend!
** This class offers a fascinating exploration of how poetry and neuroscience intersect. You’ll gain insights into how the brain processes language, emotion, and creativity, and you’ll never read a poem the same way again. I would definitely give yourself ample time to do the readings (don’t expect to just read them the night before Friday’s lecture). Be prepared to challenge your assumptions about how meaning is constructed and experienced—both cognitively and emotionally. Dive into the research, embrace the complexity, and don’t shy away from asking big questions. Anne is a INCREDIBLE professor and changed the way I view poetry and also my outlook on learning in general. This course isn’t just about poetry or neuroscience; it’s about understanding what makes us human.
This class is about what you put into it. You can get away without too much effort. But interdisciplinarity is hard to teach, and it's on you to actively engage with the materials if you want to actually learn something that you can apply to YOUR own interests and study.
This is the most transformative class I've taken at Harvard, simply because of Anne. Her approach to interdisciplinary learning, general passion for the humanities and all together the world is so inspiring.
Even though this class is a seminar and lecture–based, everything was so open to discussion and everyone shared their thoughts freely and openly. That being said, the readings are very crucial to participate in discussion, and believe me, you want to participate. It's very valuable and you learn a lot from what other people think. It's so much fun. This class is interdisciplinary, but I think this class would benefit more from diving into poetry more. I think this class focused too much on the brain. I loved that Professor Dymek would engage with us. She was always open to questions and she would answer them honestly and would ask us about what we thought. She was always eager to hear what we had to say and made this class one of my favorites here at Harvard.
A fascinating course! Anne is a lively and engaging professor (and philosopher) and I always learn so much from her courses. This course seems to attract interesting people! I'm grateful for the new friends I met. In terms of the actual content, the flow of the course doesn't feel totally polished. It's tough to meaningfully bridge the relationship between linguistics (poetry) and neuroscience, since this field of research is still emerging, and the course is a good but not totally satisfying attempt. Some previous knowledge of the brain will help greatly. Prior experience with "independent research" / good Googling / Internet discovery skills will help a lot, since the final project is mostly self–directed.
