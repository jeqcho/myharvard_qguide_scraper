If Michael Brenner is teaching the class: Don't take it if you don't have to. Lectures are disorganized and unprepared, homework is sparse and focuses more on helping the professor with his personal research project than helping you learn, section is useless, and the TAs can't provide help on the homework when you need it. If you really have to take it for your degree and Michael Brenner is teaching it: Don't go to lecture and read the lecture notes and Bender & Orzag — you'll learn a lot more that way.
The topics were interesting, though I did not like that the course took such an *extreme* turn to focus on using AI for homework. I understand that the structure of this course had to be altered to account for the new technologies, but I felt that I didn't learn much of the intended course content because of this transition. Take this if you are interested in AI.
Professor Brenner’s Math 201 class ranks among the top two or three courses I’ve ever taken at Harvard. His lectures are phenomenal. Michael Brenner guides you so masterfully that you feel as though you’re discovering each concept on your own. His knowledge of the material is unbelievable, and, more importantly, his ability to share this knowledge is something very few professors and lecturers at Harvard can achieve (at least in my four years of classes as a physics and math concentrator).

This class is a must–take for anyone interested in theoretical physics. You’ll learn a multitude of approximation techniques that are ubiquitous in modern physics. Even if you think you already understand the basics, this course will deepen your intuition in ways you never imagined possible.
This course was adapted this semester to address the fact that many generative AI models are able to solve typical problems in this course. This meant the homework involved inventing and solving problems that these models cannot address. It invents a unique challenge for the course but ultimately challenges you to think about what makes a problem difficult.
This course can be time–consuming, but you can consistently receive help on your homework before/after lecture, at discussion, and at office hours, and the resources on Canvas and in the course notes are very helpful in doing the homework and studying for the exam. The final project is also open–ended and can be a fun exploration of a topic of your choice.
The class is a bit disorganized, but Prof. Brenner is very funny. I really like the opportunity to work on the HardMath2 project with him and his team.
If you can handle a slightly disorganized class, it is definitely a class worth taking if you study some numerical field.
This is a hard class geared towards researchers. I did not enjoy the HARDMath component (since I didn't feel like I got much out of it other than being a problem monkey), but the content itself was great. Brenner is an enthusiastic lecturer. Do not fall behind.
Michael Brenner is extremely enthusiastic about the course. This is also what encouraged me to take the class in the first place. He is very flexible and adjusted the course to recent developments in machine learning. The opportunity to work on the HARDMath project as a class was class. Lectures/Canvas/Course Notes are quite unstructured at times.
Somewhat disorganized with Prof. Brenner. The course notes are good but incomplete, and the lectures are basically a repeat of the notes. The material is pretty interesting, and the psets do help reinforce the material – I did learn a lot of cool techniques. James is an excellent TF, though, and with him as lecturer the class will probably be great.
I highly recommend taking this course. Course notes are great, and you can self–study even if you don't get the material during lectures. If you deal with PDEs/numerical methods at all in research, you can build on top of what you cover here with the final project. It's a good grad course where you'll learn the basics, and you're given the freedom and incentive to apply it to your focus.
If you are taking the class, it is likely for a master's requirement, in which case you have to take it. I personally was not that enthralled by the content. Lectures were unclear and attendance was pretty sparse as a result. Homework assignments turned into making a dataset of problems for the professor's paper rather than building knowledge of the content. All in all, I wish I did not have to take this class.
Brenner is kind and cares about his students. This year, given advancements in AI, the class took a different format. It was still taught well.
