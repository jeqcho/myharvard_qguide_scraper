The common conception of this class fashions it as something akin to ritual sacrifice, an idea that does not really reflect reality in the slightest. Yes, the course is difficult. Yes, you will probably struggle at various points throughout the semester (or maybe the whole). LS50 is still the best formal learning experience I have ever had, hands down.

I had a lot of fears coming into the class: My science background was not at all comprehensive. I did not take AP Biology nor AP Chemistry (or their IB equivalents). I had no formal research experience. Al of my STEM education during high school revolved around physics and math.

As Andrew will tell you early in the class, everyone comes into Harvard with their own strengths and weaknesses. (I'm still pretty bad at chemistry.) Your ability to succeed is not at all dependent on how much time you spent in a lab during high school, so take what the website says seriously: The only formal requirements for this course are a motivation to learn and a strong background in calculus. Don't be intimidated by the high–powered pre–meds or students with published research. They'll definitely be at an advantage for the basics, but LS50 moves pretty quickly beyond what anyone has ever learned in high school; nobody I surveyed had ever heard of statistical mechanics before the class. (The textbook they teach stat mech out of is intended for graduate students. Give it a thorough read during the unit; it will undoubtedly clarify concepts for you.)

I won't pretend that I was never challenged by the work or always felt supremely confident while trying to complete a problem set. Especially in the first few weeks of the class, I was at nearly every office hours and pestering my peers with what I'm sure were very basic questions about biology and chemistry. Thankfully, my classmates and course staff were wonderful; once I got the basic concepts drilled down, I was able to learn at the same pace as everybody else.

Even though the course is challenging, I never felt overwhelmed. I had time to have fun over the weekend and enjoy college without being married to the library. You'll learn quickly that the amount of time you spend in the library at Harvard is less correlated with how well you're doing your classes than you might think; consider that people complaining that they spend all of their time studying were probably going to do so no matter their schedule.

Another benefit that most students cite when taking this class is the robust community built throughout the semester. The fact that enrollment is capped at 40 students means that you'll get to know nearly everyone in the class. Our year was especially social: You might not have the exact same experience, but I can say confidently that you will make at least a few friends during your time. We've had dinners, dorm room get–togethers, trips to Boston, gift exchanges, and late–night runs to the various restaurants in the square. Even though I'm not pre–med, planning to do research in the life sciences, or really that interested in biology, I'm taking LS50b in the spring largely because of the amazing people I've had the privilege of studying, laughing, and having (friendly) arguments with.

As for the course staff, they were all excellent. They all enjoy teaching the class because the students taking it are self–selecting: The proportion of students uninterested and putting in minimal effort to engage during lectures is much lower than many of the larger intro courses at Harvard.

As a final, but important, point: Don't think that you won't enjoy the class if you're not a biology fanatic. I'm a computer nerd through and through, but had lots of fun during the class learning about the underlying physical mechanics driving our lives. This is really a class on problem–solving, and you will be better for it, no matter who you are or what you're interested in.

That said, don't take this class if it looks miserable to you. The schedule is brutal: You will be in class every morning at 10:30, not including one section and a three hour lab. That's a big time commitment, and naturally precludes an involvement in a number of other cool classes. Remember, you're already at Harvard: How you spend your time is up to you, so don't continue to burden yourself with the expectation of being better than everyone else. If you don't want to take LS50, don't take it.

Some final tips:
– If you're on the fence, just lottery for the class (it gets more and more popular each year, so there's a decent chance you won't get in). If you change your mind, you can always turn it down. They won't be offended.
– Start your problem sets early. They're released late Friday afternoons, but I would usually start mine on Sunday night and work on it throughout the week. It's a lot more efficient (and less stressful) to burn the concepts into your brain over a longer period of time.
– Ask lots of questions, no matter how dumb you think they are. Even Harvard professors make mistakes or say something inaccurate; if you're confused about something, there's a good chance there's someone else also scratching their head.
– Be social. This class would be pretty unbearable without the cool and ridiculously intelligent people you get to meet.
– Stay off your laptop during lecture; get a physical notebook if you can.
– Give a solid attempt at problems on the psets before going to office hours.
– GET LOTS OF SLEEP. Your problems will compound if you are not getting enough rest each night.
– Don't stress about the grading. The point of this class is to learn heaps of information about modern science, which is punishing enough; the TFs are not trying to fail you.

Professor Berry is right. LS50 absolutely *does* become your life. You will meet your LS50 classmates 5 times a week in the morning for lectures (though this number will gradually decrease as the term wears you out), once a week for sections, and once a week for lab. Because the work in LS50 is hard (every other QReport you have read so far, my dear reader, has mentioned this, so I won't harp on about it), you will in all likelihood go to office hours and form P–Set blocks. Thus, you will meet your LS50 classmates much more than the schedule suggests. You will inevitably have lunch and dinner with them. You will inevitably find some people the company of whom you like. Emma Nagy, the chemistry lecturer for LS50a, is amazing. I found the section explaining how the different properties of different amino acids made them behave different in proteins particularly interesting. The course is also unintentionally funny – or maybe its us who are funny. Lock ~30 university students in a room and all make them fixate on something and they're bound (if you know you know) to develop at least 4 group wide inside jokes after 8 hours. Show a sophmore LS50 student (one of your CAs!) a cartoon of two people wearing the exact same except one has a black right shoe while the other has a black left shoe and caption it 'Enantiomers'. A few words on LS50's difficulty (I WILL harp on about it. Compounding this information with the other QReports you read, I hope you will come to your own conclusions on what parts of the course you will find hard. No hurdle is insurmountable though. If you put in effort, you will be able to learn the material, even if it is after a struggle. Your perseverance will be rewarded.

Course support in LS50 is excellent. The CAs, TFs, and professors are all extremely kind and helpful. Take the course. Its good! However, a word of warning too. LS50 is what you make of it.
