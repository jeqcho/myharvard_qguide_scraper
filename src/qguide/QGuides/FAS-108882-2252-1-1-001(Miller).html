APPHY 50B COURSE EVALUATION:

As a student that has completed the full AP50 series, I have several thoughts on the course.

First, for context, the structure of the class includes the following weekly assignments, as listed below:
• Sunday by 11:59 PM – Reading Assessment: you read about 20–30 pages in the textbook and leave about ~10 comments to ‘stimulate conversation’ with your peers
• Sunday by 11:59 PM – Readiness Assurance (Individual): you complete around 10–20 practice questions individually based on the topics you learned from reading
• Tuesday by 9 AM – Tutorial (Individual): you complete an application–based assignment on Desmos consisting of about 20–30 slides on the weekly topics you learned from reading
• Tuesday IN CLASS – Tutorial (Team): you review over the Desmos tutorial assignment with your team and complete check–ins with the teaching staff + correct your answers – ONLY THIS ROUND IS GRADED
• Tuesday by 6 PM – Readiness Assurance (Team Round): you re–visit the Readiness Assurance from Sunday with your team and re–submit what you think is correct – ONLY THIS ROUND IS GRADED
• Thursday by 9 AM – Challenge (Individual): you complete a 3 question problem set individually before class and submit to Gradescope
• Thursday IN CLASS – Challenge (Team): you review over your challenge with your team and complete check–ins with the teaching staff + correct your answers – ONLY THIS ROUND IS GRADED
• Thursday IN CLASS – Skill Session: you take your newly learned concepts from the week and put it into practice by completing different activities + a short Canvas quiz – ONLY THIS ROUND IS GRADED

As it relates to grading and work, this class will make you WORK. However, this comes with the benefit that AP 50a/b does NOT contain exams, and only grades you on the TEAM component of assignments. As a result, you have a chance to correct all of your work before submitting it to be evaluated. Additionally, if there are still mistakes, you have the chance to do markups to correct it, ensuring that you perform well in the class.

Overall, the reality is that AP50a/b will GUARANTEE you an A if you submit your work on time. The structure is outlined very early in the course, and the requirements are extremely straightforward. However, you must stay on track for all assignment and compete EVERYTHING on time. If you fall behind and either miss ONE assignment or have ONE dissatisfactory grade (and fail to make it up in a reasonable amount of time), it will automatically bump you down a full letter grade. However, as long as you complete all assignments on time and follow the course structure perfectly, there is no way you can go wrong.

Notably, the learning atmosphere within AP50b remains one of the course’s biggest weaknesses, especially as it relates to specific members of the teaching team. While a majority of the teaching staff are supportive and work hard to create an encouraging atmosphere, there are very evident exceptions to this. Instead, the actions of some members of the teaching team continue to intimidate and discourage students, leading to the creation of an extremely toxic environment that fails to promote learning.

One of the course heads, Robert Haussman, is the most unsupportive individual in the entire class. Although he seems to have a calm and encouraging personality when you first meet him, this is merely a facade. When students ask questions on Slack or in–person, he often responds in the most passive aggressive tone. For example, when a student asked about the progress of grade snapshots (a grade preview that the teaching staff typically provide at multiple time points throughout the semester to keep students on track), he chose to respond that he did not have sufficient time to fulfill this and told the student to go calculate it themselves, as the sheet to do so has “been there all year” long. This highlights his complete disregard of the needs of students and decision to blame students for a shortcoming of his own. Then, during check–ins, his discouraging personality is even more evident, with Robert often staying with groups for 30+ minutes on something that should realistically only take 10–15 minutes maximum. Beyond the questions asked within the assignment, he chooses to ask students follow–up after follow–up. While this would normally be acceptable (especially if used to try and further student understanding), he often becomes extremely disapproving and notably disgruntled when students have the smallest ounce of confusion. Having an instructor like this not only FAILS to create a helpful environment that facilitates student engagement and learning, but also TURNS STUDENTS AWAY from asking for help when they need it. Looking forward, this is an area that needs significant improvement, or AP50 will continue failing to meet the needs of its students.

Unfortunately, this issue also persists with some of the course TFs. One TF in particular, Ri Dresser, is very similar to Robert and also leads to the creation of an unsupportive and toxic atmosphere in class. As one of the TFs that regularly does check–ins, they are known throughout the class as being one of the harshest graders, often giving VERY low scores to students for making simple mistakes. For context, check–ins are graded between 0–3, with 0 indicating no effort (or not completing the assignment), 1 indicating minimal effort, 2 indicating satisfactory work and effort (even if some of the work is wrong), and 3 indicating work that extends beyond the expectations of the class. With this in mind, 2 is the “normal” score that students should expect to receive as long as they present their work in a reasonable manner. However, Ri has a tendency to give VERY low scores, ranging from 1.3 to 0.8 to even 0.5, despite there being no valid reason for doing so. In fact, there are days where Ri was shown to give consecutive “failing” scores ranging between 0.5–0.8 to SEVERAL groups back to back, being the ONLY TF to do so. Beyond just the grading, Ri is also very unapproachable due to their attitude. In class, Ri oftentimes grips their head in agony and sighs loudly when students indicate confusion. In effect, this makes students extremely uncomfortable and creates an environment that appears to punish making mistakes (or being unsure about something), as opposed to encouraging learning and individual growth. Similar to the note above about Robert, the fact that members of the teaching staff act in this manner completely TURNS AWAY students from being passionate and wanting to learn physics. Instead, all it causes is students purposely trying to avoid these members of the teaching staff, knowing that asking them for help will only lead to further judgement and frustration.

Despite my aforementioned complaints about AP50b, credit is due where credit is deserved – the saving grace for this course and silver lining amidst tons of issues is Kelly Miller, one of the new course heads. This semester, Kelly was introduced as one of the new instructors and began taking over the AP50 series. She will also be the primary course head going forward for both AP50a/b.
