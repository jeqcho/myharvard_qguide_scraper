This class gets a lot of hate that it doesn't deserve. I took it as a sophomore, having taken CS50 and CS61 before it. Here are some pros and cons:

Pros:
+ You get a lot of freedom to explore areas of interests, especially in the final project. I had the opportunity to read research papers and learn about Computer Networks topics that I found interesting, and then use what I learn to build a project.
+ Low pressure. If you put in consistent effort, not more than 2h per week, you won't have to cram much before exams. The material is straightforward, and the assignments are not difficult.
+ What you learn in class is what you're tested on. You're quizzed on facts about networks, and not asked to solve complicated problems on the exams. Compared to CS61, the exam prep felt like a vacation to me.

Cons:
– HT Kung is the worst lecturer I've encountered since coming to Harvard. He's monotonous, doesn't explain things well and sort of expects you to learn things by yourself. He's accessible and answers questions, but the lectures and the lecture notes are not helpful.
– You don't get enough guidance on the final project, and there isn't much support to help you with the implementation. You have to work on everything by yourself.

Overall, I recommend the class as chill but "you get what you put in" kind of CS class. Do your own readings and research, and use the lectures as an opportunity to ask HT Kung about what you learned.

Take this class!!! The course content is very interesting and unlike any other CS classes I’ve taken at Harvard thus far. Professor Kung is also a wonderful teacher.
Overall: CS 1430 works its way from the basics of network layers to the concepts and protocols underlying modern networks, including wireless networks, satellite networks, and blockchain. It culminates with your final project that will build on an advanced topic of your choice. Topics and course structure are subject to change year–to–year with emerging trends, particularly in AI (in both your learning and in AI learning).

Professor: HT is lovely. The class is quite concept–driven (especially in the era of generative AI), with HT making sure you understand “why” protocols are designed the way they are, “where” a protocol applies, and “what” assumptions are behind the protocol, beyond just “how” it is implemented.

Textbooks: Kurose and Ross is useful for nitty–gritty protocol details (particularly for the first programming assignment), though don’t get too caught up in the nitty–gritty.
Kurose and Ross, Computer Networking: A Top–Down Approach, Eighth Edition.

Topics covered: Network layers and protocols (Physical layer, link layer, network layer, transport layer, application); L1–to–L3 interactions; L2 switching and L3 routing; routing protocols (distance vector, link–state); L4 transport protocols (TCP, UDP); TCP congestion control; wireless networking (antennas, RF, ALOHA, CSMA CA/CD, CSMA backoff, ns–3 simulation); internet from space (LEO, inter–sat links, HARQ), software–defined networks; network security (public key, Diffie–hellman, RSA, hash chain, DNS); blockchain/bitcoin (decentralized distributed ledger, public key encryption and authentication, ownership transfer, double–spending prevention, proof–of–work consensus, bitcoin mining and incentives), AI–assisted networking (reinforcement learning (RL) based random access channel protocol vs. slotted ALOHA, RL–based congestion control vs. TCP).

Course structure notes: Two programming assignments (one individual, one group), two open–note midterms, group final project presentation and write–up on an advanced topic of your choice.
