This course undoubtedly offers one of the best entries into the medical humanities/anthropology/sociology. If you are interested in using the social sciences to study medicine in America, I'd highly recommend this course. Professor Alam is an incredible lecturer who tells the story of medicine in compelling thematic case studies that take you from the standardization of medicine with the Flexner Report to the introduction of Bidil in the early 2000s. This course goes far beyond what others do and will provide the best survey of major historical events in the history of medicine.
This course was one of the most intersectional and nuanced classes I have taken, and one of the most prevalent regarding understanding the long term themes underlying the medical system. The professor was an amazing lecturer that made the learning process involved and the TF was very helpful in section and outside of class. I would heavily recommend taking it, regardless of your concentration, but I think it is so fundamentally important for pre–med students that will go into these fields to gain the awareness of how some of these systems perpetuate harm so that they are more prepared.
I think this class is one of the most engaging ones I have ever taken! It weaves together so many topics in health, from global health and humanitarianism to the HIV epidemic to eugenics to medical professionalism, and it does so purposefully and logically. Section is really centered around discussion and connecting everything you've learned in class up to that point. Assignments give you quite a bit of freedom to write about what you want, which can be daunting but rewarding in the end.
This was the most disappointing class I have ever taken at Harvard.

I was incredibly excited about it initially, as the reviews and syllabus seemed to perfectly align with my interests. However, the course fell far short of my expectations.

Professor Alam appeared far more focused on pursuing tenure than fostering an engaging and collaborative learning environment. Discussions felt stifled, as the professor discouraged alternative viewpoints and gave little room for critical thinking or meaningful dialogue.

Even more frustrating was her approach to student inquiries. When asked for clarification on paper prompts or assignments, she responded dismissively, telling students to simply “read it three more times.” This condescending attitude made it incredibly challenging to feel supported or valued as a student.

Additionally, her lectures are bland and could be summed into three bullet points. She seems to fill her talks with buffer words trying to prolong a point.

Moreover, the TF's grading on papers was disheartening for students who spent days and days on the paper, receiving B– and Bs was not only surprising but we were left with no explanation.

Ultimately, the lack of openness and dismissive tone made what could have been an inspiring course an extremely negative experience.
