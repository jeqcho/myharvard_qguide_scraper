Overall: HIST 1465 centers on the US as an actor on the world stage and how the US has influenced world order, from US empire to decolonization, from Cold War dynamics between the US, the USSR, China, and the so–called Third World to the unipolar moment after the collapse of the Soviet Union. There is a particular focus on the US interaction with the world, starting with the beginnings of the US overseas empire in the late 19th century and leaving off at the US response to 9/11.

Professor: Manela is a wonderful lecturer. He does a tremendous job of painting the picture of the period through maps and media while balancing these with timelines and biographies. He is also keen to point out relationships between historical figures across periods and how that may have affected the thought processes of those at the helm, as well as historical figures and historians with ties to Harvard.

Textbooks: Required for both primary and secondary source readings. Other secondary source readings will be provided.
Dennis Merrill and Thomas G. Paterson, Major Problems in American Foreign
Relations, Vol II: Since 1914, 7th ed. (Wadsworth, 2010).

Topics covered: World order in the 1890’s; US joins the fray, Americans as colonizers; empires at war and revolution; envisioning and making a post–WWI world order; fragility and collapse of the post–WWI order; road to WWII; WWII as a race war (TA lecture, subject to change); remaking world order… again; Cold War order emerges; Korean War, Cold War goes global; rise of the “Third World”; decolonization and development; wars for Vietnam; global 1960’s; bio–orders: people, food, germs (TA lecture); 1970’s “shock of the global”; end of Cold War order; unipolar moment; 9/11 and its aftermath; a new Cold War. Similarities were also drawn to the shift in the US approach to world order that is associated with the early months of 2025, though they are not the focus of the course.

Course structure notes: Weekly readings and section; two essay assignments; two midterms and a final exam.
