What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
One of the hardest classes at Harvard. Good grad student community. Not for the faint of heart.
This class is amazing for someone who does or is looking to do research in this area or adjacent areas. It's definitely catered towards actually using these things in practice, giving you a bag of tricks and tools you'll need. It will be very useful for your current and future research, especially the homeworks in my opinion.

If it's your first time ever seeing this stuff, you may find it very difficult and it would be good to review some things (maybe a YouTube overview lecture on RMT, there's a good one by Marc Potters) before you start. If you don't see yourself ever using these computations again in your research and you're just interested in the material then it's probably not the course for you, as you will likely get frustrated with the nitty–gritty computation details you'll need to learn and perfect.
This is the best course I've taken at Harvard so far. I'm an undergrad and this course was the best introduction to ML theory research I could have asked for. Professor Yue is an amazing lecturer. Most of the time, he recalls everything from his memory and doesn't even need lecture notes! He also motivates the way the proofs are structured so things don't seem like they came out of left field. Lectures move quite quickly, but that's a good thing since you will learn to comprehend things on the go.
This class has one of the highest learning–to–time ratios. Yes, some rigor is skipped, but I absolutely loved the content and problem sets. Going through the calculations, listening to Pf. Lu's amazing lecture, .. I'm heavily considered working on RMT. AMP is super cool, and frankly, I couldn't ask for anything more from this class.
This is a great class that introduces you to important techniques in high–dimensional statistics and machine learning that are used to make various predictions about behavior of models. For instance, we derived (and proved) equations related to random matrix theory (eigenvalue distributions of random matrices), linear modeling (predictions about generalization error in ridge regression), and universality. All of these concepts find great applications in many machine learning problems (theory) so would recommend the course if you are interested in that sort of thing. Professor Lu is amazing, he really knows the subject and uses it daily. He is extremely knowledgeable.
