This was probably the best class I've taken at Harvard. The readings were amazing. I learned so much about the production of knowledge and how the process of science works. Thinking about why we know what we know, how we know we can trust the experts who produce knowledge, and thinking about all historically–contingent reasons why we don't know certain things is deeply important and extremely interesting. The discussions were also amazing. Professor Oreskes is really good at facilitating discussion ––– she knows when to move the class on to a different topic and always has interesting questions to pose and information to add. The students in the class this semester were also really engaged and thoughtful and smart which also made this class a great vibe. There is a lot of reading though. However, you get out what you put in and your time will be well rewarded in this class. You're asked to write a short summary of the argument of each reading. This is a challenging assignment but Professor Oreskes gives good feedback on it so you'll get better throughout the semester. It's also great because it ensures that everyone comes to class having done the reading and allows you focus your reading on understanding the text (rather than trying to figure out what you think about it, which is often a pattern I fall into with classes where I'm asked to write discussion posts). I think that it's a genius assignment and that all reading–based classes should put in this requirement (though it might be harder in larger classes to Chat–GPT proof it. Professor Oreskes has high standards which is part of why the assignment works). I really cannot recommend this class enough, I feel like it should be a requirement for all Hist Sci majors.
This class quickly became one of the best courses I have ever taken at Harvard. This class is DIFFICULT. You are expected to read, comprehend, and succinctly summarize 300–1000 pages of reading per week. But this, I have come to realize, is gold–standard pedagogy. Our discussions felt electric and vital, because everyone came to class with such a strong grasp of the material. Professor Oreskes is an engaging, thought–provoking seminar leader. This was an intellectual feast!!! It was challenging in the best way, and full of fascinating case studies. The study of ignorance is incredibly important, and this class will shift the way you think about knowledge and power. EXCELLENT class.
Start the reading early so you can think about it! Be prepared in class.
This class will help you better understand both science and the world more broadly in crucial ways.
Absolute BEST histsci course of college, maybe favorite course of all time. This class reshaped how I look at the world, how I think about history, and challenged some of my long–held views on science. This is what seminars were meant to be.

Oreskes is a genius and brilliant teacher who fosters exceptional discussions (with the occasional hilarious tangent). She welcomes dissent and confusion (even if she has strong opinions and isn't afraid to let you know). The readings are all bangers (except for Anasazi America, which Is at least very skimmable). At least this year, we had an amazing group of people including a bunch of journalists with really different perspectives than you typically get in an undergrad class.

This class is not easy. Oreskes assigns a book per week (usually 300–400 pages) and a 1 page summary of the readings that's not just graded for completion. Almost invariably I ended up staying up the night before class finishing the summary. That might sound like overkill, and some weeks it feels like it, but you learn so much. It sounds stupid in the age of ChatGPT, but I found that learning to quickly and clearly summarize the main argument of what I read and my own work changed how I read and how I thought. All that work is worth it.

If you've ever wondered how we know what we know, what forces shape science and public health, and how anti–scientific forces have become so powerful in the U.S., this is the class for you.
This is a great class that fundamentally changed the way I look at the world. The readings are definitely a lot, but doing them is so worth it. Prof. Oreskes is a great seminar leader and her wealth of knowledge is very impressive! I would highly recommend taking this class.
