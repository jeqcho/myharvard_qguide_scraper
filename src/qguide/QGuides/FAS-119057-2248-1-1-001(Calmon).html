Overall: ES 250 is a course in information theory, including applications to communications, statistics, and machine learning. At a high level, information theory quantifies the number of bits worth of information a variable with a certain distribution can be encoded (compressed) to, and the amount of that information that can be transferred between variables through some probabilistic process such as a noisy channel. While some achievability results are covered (how to actually implement algorithms that approach information–theoretic bounds), the focus is mostly on proving bounds. As an engineer, this course was quite mathematically heavy, though my mathematician or statistician colleagues may beg to differ.

Professor: Flavios lectures are detailed and thoroughly planned out. Mostly blackboard, but slides as necessary for visualization. The pace of the course is on the fast side since it covers an ambitious amount of material.

Textbooks: Cover and Thomas was extremely useful for understanding alongside lectures. Also a good source of alternative or supplementary proofs and lemmas.

Elements of Information Theory, 2nd. Edition, by Thomas M. Cover and Joy A. Thomas, Wiley, 2006.

Topics covered:
Data compression and information measures: Shannon figure 1; source coding, unique decodability, Kraft Inequality; optimal codes, Huffman codes, mismatched codes; entropy H(X); KL–divergence D(X||Y), conditional entropy H(Y|X), mutual information I(X;Y), f–divergences; chain rule, data processing inequality (DPI), Fanos inequality; entropy rate; asymptotic equipartition property (AEP). Reliable data transmission over noisy channels: channel capacity; Shannon limit; random codes, joint typicality, and achievability of Shannon limit; converse results and source–channel separation; linear codes and other practical coding schemes. Gaussian channels: differential entropy h(X) and similar continuous versions for other measures; capacity of AWGN channels; parallel channels and water filling (algorithm). Noisy compression: quantization; rate–distortion theorems; predictive coding and connection to compression standards. Information theory, statistics, and machine learning: method of types, large deviations, and Sanovs Theorem; information projection, hypothesis testing, and the Neyman–Pearson Lemma; Maximum Likelihood, log–loss, and cross–entropy. Other topics: differential privacy, other information measures, machine learning and supervised learning applications.

Course structure notes: Long biweekly psets, proof–based, building on top of the material in lecture. In–class midterm, also proof–based. Final project: paper–like critique of existing info theory paper OR paper–like writeup of your application of info theory to a field; plus in–class presentation. Perhaps a few guest lectures throughout.
Overall, a solid introduction to information theory. Assignments are good for the most part, though some parts can be vague and grading can be nitpicky. Course communications occur on slack, and there can often be long delays (over a week) for getting a question answered.
This is an incredible class. Flavio is perhaps the best lecturer I've had at Harvard, and lectures are always engaging. Overall very friendly and considerate course staff.

That said, it is also very difficult. A background in statistics is a must, and a background in electrical engineering would be helpful for certain topics.
I took this as a college senior, and I found it pretty tough with my only statistics background coming from Stat 110 and 242 and CS 181/184. Measure theory came up very rarely, but it was never necessary to understand the concepts. Flavio is an outstanding and engaging lecturer, and we had two excellent guest lectures as well. Overall a super cool class to get up to speed on compression and get a taste for various modern applications of information theory such as differential privacy.
Information Theory is an important topic, and this is overall not a bad introduction to it. Flavio is an enthusiastic teacher, and in the beginning of the semester is quite good. However he often speeds through things and leaves no time for questions, and there seemed to be little support outside of class. The textbook is the industry standard – Cover & Thomas, and one can learn a lot of information theory just by reading this book. You probably should have backgrounds in linear algebra and probability at least at the level of 110 and 111. I don't think this is a bad class but just wish it had more support and clearer supplementary material and office hours, so I can appreciate and understand the motivation of things better.
