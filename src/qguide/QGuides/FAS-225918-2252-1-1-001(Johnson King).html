This is an excellent overview of the seminal texts and movements in metaethics in the last 80 years. ZJK helps you understand the ins and outs of the key positions and the core arguments against each of them. She also pushes you to figure out what you actually think about metaethics and defend your view against the strongest objections to it. ZJK has excellent pedagogy, and the course is set up to help you engage actively with the material throughout the semester (1–2 page reading responses weekly, activities in class, writing assignments that build towards a longer final essay, and a review exam at the end of the course). You will come out of the course as a better philosopher with a much stronger understanding of the literature.

The most mixed bag I have taken at Harvard.
On the good side, Johnson King is a fantastic professor. She is knowledgeable, gives engaging lectures, cares a lot, is accommodating to students' needs, and all the other things you would want. The course materials are great, probably the best list of papers I have seen assigned for a philosophy course.

On the other hand, the course is structured like a high school course. Johnson King believes in importing "active" learning methods like activities into the course. What that practically means is ~half an hour each lecture where you, for instance, play a game defining random words to demonstrate that it is hard to define words. This often takes away from deeper analysis of the course materials or even answering students' questions. That means that you probably have to learn separately in order to go deeper into the topics of the course.
Similarly, assigned essays are universally very short and focused more on getting you to recite a set of statements than to think about the subject. This means that your essays will be more about fitting a large number of ideas in a short number of words than about the quality of those ideas themselves.
Further, the feedback on the course is unusual. Many assignments are graded without comments, meaning that you have to volunteer to meet your teacher in order to get any comments at all, and the comments that you do get (on papers and such) is very light and vague.

So, take it if you are very interested in the topic and would like to have the opportunity to have this professor, but do not expect it to be the best philosophy course offered in a particular semester.
This class is absolutely wonderful! Zoe is one of the best professors I have had here at Harvard. Not only is she super funny, but she knows her philosophy so well and does a great job breaking it down during lectures. Zoe's lectures are super engaging– she is very bubbly and enthusiatic, and she always have activities during lecture! One thing I would say about this class is it's super important to stay on top of the readings. The reading summaries (while they may annoy you in the moment), will be your absolute best friend when it comes to that final! This is a class both undergraduates and graduate students can take, and I highly recommend it as an undergraduate myself– what I will say is that the readings are very complexed, so you have to be committed to sitting there and putting in the effort to understand. Zoe and Jing Hwan are fantastic and are readily available, which is great. The two papers are very short (1500 words), and make sure you come to class to get your participation points! Overall, this has been one of my favorite classes, and I'm really going to miss it!
