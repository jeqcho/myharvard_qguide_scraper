Take this course if you like a discussion–based cancer biology course. The majority of the semester will be spent reading, discussing, and presenting recent literature. You'll learn quite a bit about cancer mechanisms, factors or events that accelerate/promote tumorigenesis, immunotherapy, personalized therapy, cancer monitoring and tracking, cutting edge technology, etc through these papers. Assignments mainly involve creating scientific summaries and questions for assigned papers and preparing for journal clubs, discussions, and presentations. Overall, it's different from a typical biology course and I quite enjoy taking this course.
I feel so knowledgeable about Cancer Biology after this course.
This class is SO MUCH FUN!! It was a push, but would definitely recommend, it made me really gain an appreciation of how cool science is now that we're in college –– whether you're thinking about studying cancer or not!
This is a very manageable upper–level biology course where you will spend a majority of your time learning how to break down complex research papers, analyze data in figures, and present the authors' findings in a concise and translatable manner. Most of this course is student–driven, so be prepared to read articles, ask questions, and present in front of the class. The main homework you'll be doing is just preparing before every class by reading the assigned paper, coming up with questions, and writing short summaries over the papers. I didn't feel very stressed at all throughout this course other than having to prepare presentations (group and/or individual).
This class is good if you want to learn how to read papers and give effective presentations and summaries. I thought it would be more learning about cancer, but it was mostly just conversations and questions about papers having to do with cancer.
MCB160 is a pretty interesting class. Every class is essentially a thorough discussion about a recent cancer biology paper published in Cell, Nature, or Science. In its first rendition the class was almost entirely ran by student–led presentations/journal clubs/discussions, which has its pro's and con's. I think there needs to be more time devoted to just lectures (in–depth explanations of a paper's background) as opposed to presentations that mostly skim the background. Prof. Hunter is very accessible and enthusiastic about the course content! Grading is pretty harsh, especially with the written assignments that ask for very short summaries of very technical papers. That being said my scientific writing skills did massively improve over the semester, so there's definitely some benefit to it. I recommend this class to anyone who's interested in bio/med research, especially those interested in going to grad school at some point after college.
This class is hard but it's absolutely worth it! You'll have to put in a lot of effort but I would recommend it for anyone going into science or medicine. You learn how to communicate well, how to effectively read papers and improve your writing skills – you just need to put the effort in. The professor really does want to help you grow so ask for help, schedule extra office hours, send him Slack messages, do what you need to do to take advantage of his expertise and make sure you improve!
I would recommend this class if you are looking to take a class titled "exploring the literature on cancer: genetics, genomics, and therapeutics." The course gives you the opportunity to read a ton of literature on the topic, but you don't actually learn much about the topic of cancer beyond the very specific therapeutics that you're reading about. You should also know that the class is entirely taught by the students taking the course. The professor only talked about two review papers and the rest of the course is run by student presentations on the assigned readings. Grading on assignments is also a bit mysterious since rubrics were never provided and you try to improve your grade simply based on the comments that were given each week on your assignments. There is often very little guidance on how to complete assignments and no feedback is given on week–to–week presentations. This class is definitely for those who prefer a less structured learning experience that is more flipped classroom style.
To be brutally honest, I had a very mixed experience with this course. I enjoyed reading the papers and learning the content, but the volume just felt overwhelming and consequently took away from the course. That being said, I have faith that Craig will take our feedback into consideration and will make the course a lot more manageable. Therefore, I strongly recommend this course. The summaries are graded very harshly, but they ultimately did help me improve. The papers, while high in volume, had a great distribution and were super interesting. The presentations, journal clubs, and discussions were all great (though probably could have been reduced to journal clubs and presentations), and I enjoyed preparing for and participating in them.
Anyone interested in learning how to read papers or in cancer biology should take this course!

You get to read about all areas of cancer bio (genomics, epigenetics, immunology, and therapeutics). You learn about mechanisms of therapy resistance, novel methods (spatial transcriptomics, for instance), and more. You also learn how to critically read papers, asking if the figures actually support the authors' conclusions. You learn to think about the big–picture implications of certain results as well as the nitty–gritty methods behind certain figures/metrics.

Discussions and presentations are generally low–stakes and you get to know your peers relatively well. It is fun to ask each other questions and be collectively puzzled about some poorly written sections of papers.

There is also a big emphasis on improving science writing and presenting. These skills are very useful for anyone doing research or planning to pursue some research/medical related field. As someone who already had experience in many aspects of this class, I can confidently say that I learned a lot that I can use in research.
Take this class. It will challenge you to get better at reading and thoroughly understanding science research. It is completely research–paper based, meaning you won't be studying or memorizing information, but you will grow a lot in reading comprehension of dense papers. It will help you improve presentation skills in a variety of ways (you'll lead group and individual presentations, small group journal clubs, structured discussions, and panel discussions). Prof Hunter and the TF offered thorough, constructive feedback on each assignment –– they will encourage you to improve each time. I loved this class and the discussions we had!
