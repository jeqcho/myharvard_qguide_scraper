Physics 195a is a great class! You learn all about a new domain of physics that is quite different from others, but draws from quantum mechanics, electricity and magnetism, waves, and even some chemistry. Professor Mundy is awesome, and does her absolute best to make the course accessible for all.
Some pros of the course include:

– Generous grading on Psets and Exams
– Infinite time on exams (Proctored by Professor Mundy)
– Small "gimme problems" on psets and chill class pacing (oftentimes we would pause at the halfway point in class for a short break)
– a wide scope of content (which means that if you don't like something, you will be doing something else the next week anyways)
– Very nice, accessible, approachable, and knowledgeable teaching staff
– Sections are essentially just pset help, and office hours are helpful too
– Taking 195a primes you well to take other Quantum Materials courses

Some criticisms:

– Psets can be a bit tedious
– Section isn't made to answer questions, it's mainly just there to explain hard Pset problems
– I don't think that the course will be taught by Professor Mundy next year, so it may have very different course structure

TL;DR: Physics 195a is a great course that will teach you all about Solid State Physics, a very unique realm of physics. Professor Mundy is awesome, and the course is structured to be quite chill. The course is a decent time commitment (~7 hours per week outside of class), but there are plenty of support resources. Take this class if you're interested!
PHYS 195a is incredibly accessible as a course (ex. midterms are untimed, so you have as long as you need) and Prof. Mundy is extremely clear in going through the calculations and making k–space make sense, if you've never seen it before. If you've only ever taken PHYS 143a (or CHEM 160, or even just CHEM 10) this class is still completely doable, and Prof. Mundy makes every effort to meet the class where it is (ex. when she found out that none of us had seen perturbation theory before, she gave us an impromptu lecture on it, instead of leaving us to figure it out). If you want a more theoretical approach to solid state, I would take 295a instead, since Prof. Mundy is an (extremely passionate) experimentalist, so the course begins with historical models, as opposed to Bloch's theorem, which is an approach that felt less intuitive to me.

The biggest perk of taking PHYS 195a is that Prof. Mundy is one of the kindest, most approachable professors on the entire campus. Not only did she have two open office hours a week; take the time to get to know us, and remember our hobbies/passions/research outside of class; but she also let us out in the middle of class to grab snacks from the Physics department tea break, which definitely helped my attention span for the last 30 minutes.
Great introduction to solid–state physics and condensed matter research! Julia is an amazing lecturer as always and makes the material very accessible. I came into this course with a rather poor understanding of quantum mechanics (did not do so hot in 143a) but was able to not only finally understand that material but also apply it to begin probing and understanding the quantum theory of solids. This course begins with older, classical theories of solids before delving into early quantum models, real–space & reciprocal crystal lattices, tight–binding, x–ray diffraction, and degenerate perturbation theory. The course ends with a discussion of semiconducting materials and devices, along with some really cool tours of condensed matter labs at Harvard and the chance to research, understand, and present on an advanced topic in condensed matter research. Overall, course material is made very accessible in lecture and section (also the Simon textbook is so, so helpful), and the p–sets and exams were pretty straightforward. Take this course!
julia mundy is the goat. she has clearly thought a lot about how the course should be structured and 195a is like the perfect balance between a chill class that doesn't stress you out and a very *solid* intro class for solid state/condensed matter. the course really emphasizes the connections between experiment and theory, which makes the psets and lectures both enriching from a physics perspective and interesting from an applications perspective. the class feels like an overlap of chemistry, physics, and applied physics and everyone in the class has some insight to bring in terms of their background and research (whether that be from physics or chemistry). the TFs are absolutely amazing and super approachable and so is mundy herself. they're all super super understanding and lenient without "baby–ing" you
Overall: 195a covers the physics of crystalline solids. Solid state takes quantum mechanics, which can barely handle a few electrons, and teaches you how to use crystalline structure in solids to explain electric, magnetic, optical, and thermal properties.

Professor: Julia is a lovely professor, lectures are clear and organized. Also great to chat with outside of class and is a super useful resource for anyone interested in physics and/or graduate school. Unfortunately, she is planning on teaching a different course next year.

Textbooks: Simon is useful for supplementing understanding from class, and is referenced in psets as needed. Ashcroft and Mermin is a textbook used in courses such as 295a. All on Harvard online reserve.
The Oxford Solid State Basics, Simon, Steven H., Oxford, OUP, 2013
Solid State Physics, Ashcroft, Neil W., Mermin, N. David, New York, Holt, Rinehart and Winston, 1976

Topics covered: Drude model, conductivity, Hall effect; free–electron model, k–space, Fermi energy/surface/wave vector, and energy density; density of states and paramagnetism; chemical bonding crash course, Hamiltonian and Eigenenergies between two atomic orbitals; tight binding model for 1D chain of atoms, Brillouin zones; Crystal lattices, Bravais lattice, Wigner–Seitz cell; simple cubic, FCC/BCC, crystal basis; Reciprocal lattice, families of lattice planes, Miller indices, plane spacing; x–ray diffraction, Bragg and Laue conditions, Ewald sphere, structure factors; 2D square lattice tight binding, 3D simple cubic/FCC/BCC tight binding, tight binding with bases; Bloch’s theorem; Nearly–free electron model, electronic band structures and zone schemes; metals, semimetals, and band insulators; effective mass, Fermi surfaces under fields; intrinsic/extrinsic semiconductors, holes, and applications to PN junctions and transistors. Connections to superconductivity, vibration (phonons), and magnetism throughout.

Course structure notes: Weekly psets. Weekly section is extremely useful for understanding; includes a few lab sessions with live data (e.g. x–ray diffraction data) collected for the pset. Two in–class midterms. Various lab tours. Final project presentation on an advanced topic building on course material, or alternative for seniors applying for graduate school.
