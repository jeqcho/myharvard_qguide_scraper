LOVED this class! It's such an amazing introduction to complex moral and ethical considerations that arise as a part of the human existence–whether that be for world leaders, investment bankers, doctors, even you as a student! There are weekly readings that could be long/crammed if you need–but it's all worth it for the discussions sections. If you buy into the course, you'll really learn a lot and gain a new viewpoint on how the world and your decision–making calculus should work.
This is a great light first year seminar. Do not be discouraged by the amount of reading in the syllabus as for the most part you do not have to spend a lot of time reading. It is a great introduction to contemporary and ancient ethics with a focus on applying it to real scenarios. We analyzed everything from Truman's decision to use the nuclear bomb to choices of bankers and CEOs in the industry that they made.
If you're at all curious about moral philosophy, take this course! It was one of my favorite classes this semester, and the discussions we had about everything from Truman's bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki to the validity of Machiavelli's advice in "The Prince" were fascinating. Professor Badaracco did an amazing job facilitating and asked questions that were very thought–provoking. This seminar does involve reading a book every week, but don't worry too much about this! Even though I wouldn't consider myself a fast reader, the workload ended up being quite manageable as the books were interesting and generally on the shorter side. Overall, I highly recommend this class!
This should absolutely be one of your top FYSEMR choices if you are interested in leadership, philosophy, business decisions, or critical thinking. Professor Badaracco makes class enjoyable and stimulating and the out–of–class weekly readings are all amazing.
This was my favorite class out of all the ones I've taken! Professor Badaracco is an amazing professor who truly cares about everything we discuss (and the students themselves). The topics within morality and leadership were also so incredibly rich in insights and possible reflections, making every class something I looked forward to hearing and contributing to.
Take this course. As a STEM concentrator, I worried that it might not be for me, but if you are into philosophy you'll love it. You will learn a lot of different things about decision–making and morality that will come in handy in many aspects of your life. The workload is not heavy at all, and you will enjoy the discussions a lot more if you do the readings. There were a lot of eye–opening moments where the course made me question my life choices and gave me a brand new perspective. It was definitely the highlight of my semester.
This course can be interesting but it is only rewarding if you put in the time to do the heavy readings (around one book per week). Each class is primarily a student–led discussion on the book that was read that week. Only take if you have serious interest in these kinds of discussions and are ready to participate and invest time into reading.
If you're looking for possibly the best introduction to what intellectual vitality and healthy disagreement looks like, take this class. Professor Badaracco is genuinely just a wonderful human being and it's so obvious he cares about his students, and you will learn about what it means to defend your position without using a single number. However, if you're looking for a super intense seminar, this probably isn't the one for you.
This class was honestly such a gem. It taught me so many ways of thinking and broadened my perspectives, not just on gray–area problems but in all areas of life. I would highly recommend taking this course.
