I can’t say it enough: TAKE THIS CLASS!! I can confidently say this has been the most transformative and influential class I’ve taken at Harvard in my entire undergrad experience. Professor Izadis classroom is a place for radical change and compassionate dialogue. I truly looked forward to stepping into her classroom every single week. The course content, assignments and discussions were incredible. Professor Izadi herself is the definition and embodiment of an AMAZING teacher. She truly cares for every single one of her students and her passion shines through everything she does. This course taught me so much, within and beyond the classroom that I’ll carry forever.
This class was one of my favorites this semester. The content is super interesting because you're learning not just about African American history or Indigenous history, but about how these histories intersect. I learned about so many things that I don't think I would've learned about elsewhere. The readings were super interesting and manageable, and our classroom discussions were also really interesting. We had a midterm paper and a creative final project which we're both very manageable because of how available and supportive Professor Izadi was. She really fits the course to what the students are interested in and is incredibly approachable and understanding. She also planned multiple out–of–class excursions for us (movie screening, MLK speaker event).
There has never been a course I’ve taken where I’ve felt so supported by the instructor as I have in EMR 158. Professor Izadi is as kind as she is brilliant and wise, and it shines how caring and invested she is in every student’s development in the course. She makes a conscious effort to know and understand our backgrounds, our hopes, and how her course can propel us toward the dreams we each hold. Substantively, EMR 158 highlighted connections and convergences between Indigenous and Black histories I had never considered, even as a history concentrator. It pushed me to be critical, to interrogate how knowledge is produced and preserved, and to speculate and think creatively to fill in the groups. The readings—by some of the most important thinkers and activists the U.S. has seen—were wonderfully introspective, and the assignments provided flexibility to write about topics we were personally interested in. We also watched Killers of the Flower Moon, which was incredibly moving. It is all heavy, dark content, but Professor Izadi presents it with grace, and she’s all the more willing to discuss further in office hours (every question or thought you have will get space). This is a diamond of a course, one of the best I’ve taken at Harvard as a graduating senior, and I cannot recommend it enough.
