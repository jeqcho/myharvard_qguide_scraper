There is definitely lots of outside practice required to lock the muscle memory in, but it's very fun and feels very useful to know.
Great intro language course! The ASL department expanded a lot this year and there were some logistical hiccups that came along with new professors. Notably, the integration between Canvas and the weekly short video lectures/questions platform (Signing Naturally) was messed up halfway through the semester, which led to many of us having failing grades in Canvas even though we did all the assignments. Nozomi wasn't the most responsive about this during the semester, but it is fixed now!
Overall the course is a lot of fun and not too hard. ASL is a really interesting language and learning about Deaf culture has been both fun and informative. I genuinely enjoyed taking this course a lot and would highly recommend.
TAKE THIS CLASS!! It's so much fun and you'll feel incredibly accomplished when you realize how much you've learned.
I would definitely recommend taking this class if you have any interest in learning ASL! I had an amazing experience starting to learn the language and learning more about deaf culture. I think the class is an encouraging and fun learning environment and would recommend that everyone at Harvard take at least one ASL class!
This course is definitely one to consider! The change from speaking to signing can be challenging, but ASL is such a wonderful language to learn. The instructors are also very nice and helpful. Their teaching styles are easy to follow and fun!
Overall, the ASL department has done a good job at revamping the program, especially in regard to out–of–class events like Silent Dodgeball and ASL Zone. However, I did not appreciate how the syllabus for the class was not consistent across instructors and was disappointed by the fact that we had to pay $100 for course materials (the GoReact platform seemed unnecessary). The actual learning of ASL was relatively easy and well–facilitated, but I felt that the written assignments were more busy work than meaningful learning tools. They didn’t seem to contribute significantly to my understanding of ASL or Deaf culture. Additionally, I was frustrated by the delayed feedback on nearly all assignments. It created stress towards the end of the semester as I did not know where I stood in the course or what feedback I needed to improve my signing. Despite these concerns, I genuinely believe Professor Tomita did an excellent job teaching ASL while incorporating important lessons about Deaf culture along the way. I look forward to continuing my ASL education and hope the department will consider this feedback as the program continues to evolve and settle.
