I think this course is awesome, especially Professor Bose's lectures. Professor Bose is definitely passionate about the course and is very knowledgable about the region. As a Southeast Asian I think I learned quite a lot of new things. I would really recommend this course as it gives a very good overview of Southeast Asian history, and talking to Professor Bose in office hours had been a great experience. You should do the readings as they are really good. But I think TLDR as long as Professor Bose keeps teaching this class the quality of his lectures is a very strong reason to take the class!

Caveat:

My major complaint is the grading that is unreasonably harsh and quite baseless. I love criticisms and feedback that actually help me grow as a writer, like I get in another class I am taking this semester, but the criticisms and feedback for the essays in this class doesn't really make sense, and even after meeting during office hours to clarify I am still not satisfied. Some of the "feedback" and point deductions just simply feel made up. There is also a lot of criticisms about my grammar or this single typo, both for the written midterm and for the review essay assignment, and I heard from others in the class they got that too. After I pointed out it is not a grammar class but a history class they seem to switch gears and argue my "argument" is weak etc but it all seems more like an attempt to justify the harsh grading instead of actually having substantive criticisms.

I guess another thing is that the perspectives given on some Southeast Asian country seemed quite Western and does not really reflect how people in that country see the events, which sometimes is fine as perspectives differ but sometimes some data are just omitted which gives a distorting picture on some episodes in the country's history. You probably won't notice if you are not from the region but I think they are quite minor and overall it is still a good class. It is just that some ideas about race or gender are really Western impositions on stuff people from the region see very differently, but I guess that's not surprising.
A really great course with a really great lecturer on a subject that receives surprisingly little attention (my perception) at school here. It is important to do readings each week because you will generally be expected to not just know the modern history of each Southeast Asian nation from their first colonial encounters to the fight for independence but also be able to compare these histories with each other on exams and in discussion sections.
Lectures are boring. Assignments are also kind of boring. Professor is a big name. You are probably better off taking other history courses.
Interesting class for pretty much anybody. Really do the readings though, that's priority number 1. Assignments aren't too bad, though the broadness of the class can make retention a little difficult.
That they should read the Chandler & Owen textbook and not even try with the Steinberg — and also that not every reading needs to be thoroughly understood, but that you should engage with the course material so that you can understand and contribute during section.
This is a pretty interesting class that is worth taking if you're looking to learn more about the region. The overview is very broad and high–level: given the breadth of the class, each country only really gets covered every two weeks or so, which can make it a little difficult to follow. The readings are long and sometimes not the most helpful since Professor Bose runs through content again in class anyway. Assignments are interesting and it's one of the few classes that I've enjoyed studying for the midterm.
I'd say that this is a great survey course but the grading is SUPER harsh, even if you are a humanities concentrator.
