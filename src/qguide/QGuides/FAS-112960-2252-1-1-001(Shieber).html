This class will be fairly difficult. You will have a lot less support and training wheels, unlike CS50. The office hours and the TFs will be your best friend; they are amazing and they are super helpful. You will be learning a brand new language, and the manner in which that programming language will require you to think will be very different from what you are probably used to. The psets are fairly lengthy and sometimes confusing, but they are very doable. On a side note, good luck with the midterms.
This is a really good class, but it is also uniquely old–school, which is sort of what everyone is really complaining about. Classes in Harvard CS no longer *really* need you to read the textbook, or go to class, or really meaningfully engage with the material in any way, and instead hold your hand all the way through (yes, even 124, where the only thing you really need to pass the class is religious devotion to office hours). CS51 is specifically designed to avoid this, and therefore forces you to do all of these things: I think this is great pedagogy, and it is a fantastic way of learning (even as one of the older students in the class, I still feel like I came away with a ton), but I can see how students who come in just having taken CS50 and expecting a similar experience may be a bit blindsided.

The hours/week are not bad: PSETs are themed, which makes them pretty interesting to complete (there's music generation, epidemic simulation, and cryptography, among others) and really don't take that long (I rarely had to go to office hours, for instance). The downsides are the two exams AND a final project, which is honestly a bit much; I'd be in favor of dropping a PSET, going down to 7, moving the final exam to be during finals period, and moving the project down to be due the last day of classes.

Prof. Shieber is also amazing: he is, like the class, old–school, but this is not a bad thing –– he genuinely wants to see you grow in the class, and is generally a very knowledgeable, approachable guy even though he is one of the titans of the field. Overall, a pretty fun class, I'd take it again.
Sometimes, this course is strange in that it tries to be a course on abstraction but also a course on the particular quirks of this one obscure coding language. Overall, it is a strong and engaging course on both of these things.
This was a good trauma bonding class. I made such good friends and got closer to other people throughout office hours, because everybody is struggling together. This class is probably going to get a lot of hate in the Qreports, but honestly, its not that bad. It is crucial, because otherwise you are going to be completely lost in the problem sets, and th elabs are a good lead up into the problem sets. Also, make sure to have good friends in the class who can help you figure out parts of the code if you can’t make office hours each week. Another hint about office horus is try to find a.TF that will acutally check your correctness score so you can find any hidden flaws with your code, especuially for some of the longer problem sets like PSET 3 or PSET 7.

The final project isn’t terrible but try to knock it out as early as you can so you can invest time studying for your other finals as well. Also, the midterms are very important because that is what will differentiate you grade wise from the rest of the class. I would do each old practice test beforehand, go through each lab, watch each video, and takes about all of them. I organized my cheat sheet by chapter and then would include weird edge cases on it. Overall, you don’t have a lot of options of classes to take your freshman spring if you don’t have a theory background, so just do it.

The course has a lot of office hours and support throughout which made the course very manageable to do. If you are not super comfortable with programming, don't take this class. CS50 is set up to help you succeed. CS51 is not. I have only finished two semesters at Harvard, but I can say with confidence that this is the most disorganized class I've taken here so far. For the first half of the class, office hours weren't available until Saturdays (the lengthy psets are due every Wednesday). There are no office hours on Wednesday, but that's because you are expected to start early, yet no office hours until Saturday. I will say, they eventually added Thursday office hours, though they were cancelled half the time. It also became clear that office hours were also set up so that less people would go (quad at 8pm, quad at 10am on a Sunday). There are other office hours though, but just know you may be waiting a very long time to ask one question. As for the exams, the first exam was nothing like the practice exams and the solutions were never even posted on the course website (good luck if you have a regrade request!). The second exam was definitely more fair, though there was a derivation question that wasn't like anything we'd learned in the course, but I will be reasonable and mention that it was on a previous practice exam, but it's more like something you'd do in CS124, so I don't understand why it's on this exam. I know some people loved this class, but I hated it personally. It took up so much time for no t much return, and prevented me from focusing on classes that are actually decently structured like math and expos. If you already have limited time, DO NOT TAKE THIS CLASS. If you require a strong support system and are very frustrated by disorganization, DO NOT TAKE THIS CLASS. Pset grading takes forever. And even then, once it's graded and you can see the autograder results, you can't see how you lost points on the autograder. Oh and there's a midterm on the last day of class (a final basically) and still, there's a final project. I also think it says something that so many people switched to pass–fail after the first exam, which had a mean that was not a passing score, but hey, curves are a thing so take that w a grain of salt. Anyways I hated this class and the horrible organization and assignments but there is a chance you may like it.
If you enjoyed CS50 and are looking for the next step up.... do NOT take this class!!! I went in thinking so, but it is a very strange course that uses an outdated language to teach you coding that isn't applicable in the real world. It is very fundamental, and useful if you're going to use it for your major, but not actually useful in your day to day life. The grading is extremely harsh, and the midterms, homework, and class assignments have no relation to each other. you have to self study everything from the textbook and videos as class is simply you working on assignments with hardly any time to both ask questions and learn and finish the assignments. Overall, a very pointless and difficult class, definitely the worst thing I took this semester. It eats up your time and effort, and is not very applicable to the real world. The professor himself has stated that he does not use Ocaml, the language of the class, in his day to day life. There is no point in taking this.

Important note: especially do NOT take this class if you don't use a Mac or Linux – I and some other Windows–using friends had a lot of trouble adapting to the system used in this class, which requires a VMware system, something the course staff (including the professor) were not equipped to handle. When I had problems, no solution was offered, and my grades initially suffered as I couldn't even use my laptop properly. Later, it became slow and buggy, but I learned to live with it, although it resulted in having to take more time to repeatedly restart the software, and complete my psets much slower due to the lagging interface. If you have a windows, using ocaml will be almost impossible, so I recommend not taking this class.
