If you genuinely commit yourself to the workload of this class and try to understand what it means for you particularly, the class can be life–changing and impactful. This is a really solid course that covers a lot of breadth in philosophy. The lectures can be a bit difficult at times due to the lecture with profs mostly talking at students for the most part, but oftentimes there are interesting points and perspectives that are brought up. As someone with limited philosophical or undergrad humanities work, I found the course manageable, with some amount of reading/skimming and brushing up on the summary of key points, and I was still able to feel like I understood and learned so much. The greatest value of the course is that we are able to cover so much ground on existentialist philosophy and so many authors have interesting perspectives, in a way that the profs (who are married! and work together well) really are able to distill the key points well.
I would recommend taking this if you are interested in the reading list (as I think most would be). The depth of engagement with this philosophical tradition is great for the survey that this course is. Having a midterm, final, and final paper isn't optimal, but the workload throughout the semester is manageable. The lectures are fine, sometimes dry, and not super organized, but I would view this as a course to take for the reading and section discussion.
This course is certainly not a gem, but it's certainly do–able, and it is certainly worth it. The Lamberths are the kindest people, and they really make an effort to get to know their students. You'll get a broad overview and understanding of existentialist philosophy and you will come out of it thinking more deeply about the meaning of freedom, religion, authenticity, and more. It really changed the way I viewed the world.

This class was *genuinely life–changing.* I walked in expecting just another gened, but I found myself totally hooked on philosophy and existentialism by the end. Even though I've been at Harvard a while, something about this course made me feel like I hadn't yet learned a single thing in class. What does it mean for humans to be free, and what should we do with our free will? What is faith and should we embark upon it? What kind of a life do you want to have lived when you die? These are the kind of urgent questions we really don't get time to think about, and this class is a great opportunity to engage with some truly creative thinkers on the human condition (Kierkegaard, Dostoevsky, and Sartre were some of my favorites). Courtney and David are also super lovely and sweet people and will take the time to go deeper into any one of the thinkers or course topics if you're interested. Go to office hours. Go to tea. It's nice. :)

The course was a fair (as in just) amount of work: ~100 pages of reading a week, but on the bright side, no busywork assignments like discussion posts or weekly responses. Lectures can be hard to follow if you're not used to philosophy but are very rewarding if you pay close attention. The midterm and final are passage ID from the books + short answer questions – very easy if you did the reading and very difficult if you didn't. DO THE READING. If not for the grades, then because the books in themselves are worth reading.
