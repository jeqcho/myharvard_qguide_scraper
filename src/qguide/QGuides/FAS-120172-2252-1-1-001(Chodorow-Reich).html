Context – I am a (regular) econ concentrator with a 1b/21a math and 1010a econ background.

...Overall...
Take this course if you are an AM concentrator, want to go into academic economics later on, have taken a real analysis course, or just want to punish yourself. Econ 1011b is a great introduction to some of the main mathematical models macroeconomists use to analyze economic phenomena. However, this course is quite poorly organized which makes it quite a lot if you're not a math wizard.

...Lecture...
Lecture in the first half with Ludwig was incredible and I could easily follow along, with the models making sense and seeming useful (though less focus on the math, so self–study is required). Gabriel's lectures are just pure math with no context – the first 5 minutes seem interesting with the background and then become impossible to follow after the first math slide comes on.

...Slides...
Lecture slides were the only core course materials, which were either quite sparse on the math (Ludwig) or insanely complex and hard to decipher, especially algebraically (Gabriel). You have to guess the algebra.

...Homework and Feedback...
Homework was mostly manageable except for the absolutely brutal 2nd pset which took more than 15 hours to complete and LATEX. Absolutely no feedback on the (completion–based) homework apart from 2 word comments from CAs.

...Section...
Section was mostly useless. Slides went into various tangents not previously covered in lecture, including "behind the scenes" derivations, while they didn't help much with deriving the core models that are tested on the exams. The addition of math was a good idea too, but it failed in principle because most math rule slides (lagrangians, implicit derivation) require at least an upper level real analysis course to understand, which is far past the math requirement specified (21a). Don't go to section – you're better off rederiving models yourself and asking questions at office hours. Some of the CA's are incredible!

...Exams...
Exams focus heavily on rederiving the models in lecture with various modifications. To prepare for these, just have every single step of every derivation memorized to the point where you could do them in your sleep. You don't really need to know the economics behind the models since it's just a small part of the exams, especially on the midterms, so focus on the math!

...TL;DR...
EC1011b is a great intro to macro models with quite poor organization. Take this course if you are AM (since you have to) or are a masochistic math wizard.

1011b is an awesome course with amazing teachers and TFs and relevant, eye–opening and deep content. You’ll learn about long–term growth in the first half and short–term fluctuations in the second half, and they change the content each year to be more relevant.

The content, though, is often too messy, and it is unclear what you need to know for each model. For example, do you need to know the full (totally painful) derivation of a model, or do you only need to know the setup and intuition and results of that model?

In my experience, the important things to know the full derivations of are the foundational models (Solow + NGM in first half, IS–MP + New Keynesian in second half). Knowing the applications + implications of the other models, as well as their results and setups, are also important, but you don’t necessarily need to know which variables to take FOCs of in the Lagrangians and stuff.

Exams are really difficult and are designed to induce significant dispersion in the grade distribution, which means they are designed with trick questions the writers know only some students will get etc. So don’t worry too much about raw scores.

This is a difficult course, one I took because I wanted to challenge myself and not have my grade rely on multiple choice quizzes such as in 1010A or the 10 series.

This course has two components, which I will evaluate separately.

First (half?) of the course, taught by Ludwig Straub. You've probably seen it on the other QReports, but this guy, oh my god, he has a teaching ability that surpasses any professor I've encountered at Harvard. Lectures, slides, and presentations are clear, understandable, and excellent overall.

Second (more than half) of the course, taught by GCR. GCR is an accomplished researcher, for sure, but he lacks teaching skills. I still remember the first time he taught us this semester. We switched from Ludwig's 25ish slides to some 70 pages of LaTeXed hieroglyphics. Much effort was put into these slides, I am not going to argue with that; however, those are not meant for teaching. GCR's style assumes that we know much of the academic language he uses on a daily basis, some of which I had never seen before, which would be fine if he covered it in class, but no! This does not happen. Good luck on spending hours on reading slides after class (which will be needed with GCR, not needed with Ludwig) and asking ChatGPT to explain every single one of them. Literally, IDK what I would've done without it. Office hours are helpful, but it would've probably required me 10 hours every week to go through the slides there.

I have some final suggestions that relate to this structure.

Homework: Fair – Office hours were helpful (very!) and allowed me to submit perfectly polished PSets (8 throughout the course). Beware of the LaTeX requirement (kept me up until 2am multiple times). PSet 2 was definitely too long (20 latexed pages), might be worth to split it up. The switch to weekly PSets is nice after GCR arrives, and I would've preferred if we had that for the first midterm

CAs: Shoutout to Shuheng, Arjun, Ryan, Meghan (or Megan). Passionate and smart people! Very helpful.

TFs: the most stuck up! I am sorry, but the TFs kept emphasizing how we should not complain about the harshest grading I've ever seen on exams. No mistake was allowed, if you didn't align with the solution perfectly, you were deducted fully. No room for any extension either, any regrade request was thrown back at you, definitely very unhelpful.

Midterm and Finals: Midterm I was okay in retrospect, though the median was a 69 (percent). Just make sure to go through the PSets. Midterm II was not okay. There were 23 questions to do in 75 minutes, no time to recheck answers, and the median ended up being a 54. Why make this midterm so long? There is a curve at the end (which is never revealed throughout the course), but like be so for real, you first slam us with an enormous amount of questions in such little time and then grade us super harshly.

Suggestion: GCR should teach the first half, and Ludwig should teach the second half. The second half of the course is much more mathematically complex, many more models to remember, and overall, not ideal for GCR's teaching style. Please consider reviewing this structure.

I'll try to organize my review in three parts: the good, the bad, and the ugly.

The good:
The material is fun and reflects the real world. Rarely will you see a model and think, "I'm never going to see this." Many of the models we discussed were, in fact, reflections of the real world, and we felt like we could see how well the models did at explaining recent stuff. OTOH, the class is up to date with the stuff covered, and does not rely on ancient papers or historical macro, but instead employs many modern Macro models. YMMV for this, but Psets were graded on completion and not necessarily correctness. Office hours (WITH THE PROFESSORS) were helpful. Whenever we worked with data for Psets, it was very simple stuff and nothing too difficult. I was a big fan of the Professor for the first half (Ludwig). This man was energetic, fun, kind, and super approachable. He made the content for the first half interesting, and I feel like I learned a lot from him. If you put in the work, I am sure you can get a good grade.

The bad:
The Psets have a lot of variance in terms of difficulty. Some Psets can be done in a few hours, and are just the same (or slightly different) problems from the Lecture. Other Psets were too long (and thus, too difficult) or simply put, way too hard. There is a discussion board where you can ask for help and get questions answered. This was helpful, sometimes. It depends a lot on which TF answers the question.
The second half of the course was, unfortunately, the weakest. It was more difficult, but in my opinion, made to be more difficult. The professor for the second half (Gabriel) is not bad, he's just outshone by Ludwig. His lecture style was off. He was very monotone, kept his hands in his pockets, and perhaps the worst was that he would throw a lot of math up in the slides and expect you to follow along. Again, he is not a bad professor, but there's a huge contrast between the first and second half, especially in teaching quality.

The ugly:
Nothing was worse than one of the TF's. Two PhD students were TFs, and one of them was atrocious. The other one was awesome and genuinely much better. He took over section towards the end, and thank God he did. I wish I had switched to his section sooner. His sections in the SC basement were miles ahead.
However, the other guy was disrespectful, unhelpful, inattentive, and rude. His sections were a waste of time, going to his OH would be useless because he wouldn't try to help, and the list can go on. I think TFs change with the years, so perhaps you'll be luckier. I can't speak for the undergrad TFs.
For exams, they may ask any bullet point from the slides or any model. They will not tell you what to focus on. Your best bet is to try and memorize as much as possible, but good luck!

TLDR: It's not an easy class, and if you feel like you struggle, it's not on you! However, the course can be improved. Make exams easier. Make the teaching staff (TFs) better. Make the second half more similar to the first half.
