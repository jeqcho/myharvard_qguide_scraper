This class, if taught by Prof. Johnson, will probably be completely different than many of the classes you take at Harvard. Prof. Johnson really emphasizes board work, and it definitely has its pros and cons––pros is that you will probably become really close with the people you work with, you will be able to explain some astrophysical concepts without even knowing it (because it is drilled into your head), labs are super cool (you get to observe with the Clay Telescope!!) and because you are essentially doing all the worksheets in class, you won't have a lot of work outside of class (and the midterm structure, although kind of stressful, was actually really enlightening in the end). However, the con is that every class was more or less kind of draining, and you may not feel that you are getting the astrophysics concepts themselves. Some of the concepts covered in this class were less intuitive than others, which didn't really fare that well when there weren't structured lectures, but you will get through it. Overall, I think this class (even though it had its ups and downs) did give me the introduction into astrophysics that every concentrator (or someone just interested in the field) needs.
Great course. You get what you give in this class so there's a lot to learn if you're interested.
This course has a very non–traditional style with minimal lecture and pre–work, and simply pure boardwork going through worksheets. When there is homework, it happens every 2 weeks or so, but takes a bit of time to complete and then write up into notion which can be tedious and annoying. If the class remains the large size it is despite the minimal room, make sure to push for space to actually work directly on the board since this classes whole thing is attendance and participation (20% when I took this, which make sense since working on the boards is where most of the material is learned). Personally wish there was more supplemental material for those without a very strong physics background, but if you're in a group with physics students (which is likely), they are a great resource for filling in the gaps. I won't lie that there were times this class was a real nuisance (pset write–up, one night–lab, 3 session day lab that happens at iffy times in the week), so take with caution and simply power through if you have to. But if you truly want to learn astronomy maybe look for a more straight forward class since this is a very math and physics dominant class a opposed to a facts about stars and how they come to be class,

We are released into the wild after a quick teaser lecture and then there Professor Johnson and the TFs walk around and provide us guidance when needed. I like that the course takes into consideration our effort and participation. I like the psets because they refined my understanding of the topics. I also appreciate the lack of psets (none were assigned) towards the end of the semester, but it did compromise my mastery of the skills a little bit.

Astro16 is one of my favorite courses I have taken at Harvard. Professor Johnson and the teaching staff are absolutely excellent. The format of the class is "flipped" and involves a lot of independent work in group settings through problems, usually after brief lectures by Professor Johnson to start you off and introduce a concept. Personally, since I learn best by doing, this was an extremely effective way to learn. It was so much fun to get to know and learn from and work with the other people in our group, and we formed a close bond over the semester. Also, working in groups to try to figure out ways to tackle these challenging problems is legitimately a really good way to learn. Homeworks were very reasonable on Professor Johnson's part and you always feel very supported throughout the class. I thought the labs were a little bit frustrating, mainly because it felt like we didn't have enough background to write the level of depth needed for the reports. Nonetheless, the night lab where you try to observe an eclipsing binary on the Clay Telescope is REALLY COOL. The midterm exam is a group midterm where you have to explain class concepts to a random person who knows nothing about astrophysics. This is an AMAZING exam format. It really forces you to go back through all the material and make sure you fundamentally understand astrophysical concepts, so you can't get away with just knowing how to use equations. This is not something that we do nearly enough of at Harvard, and I really appreciate Professor Johnson's approach to learning. Whether you are an astro concentrator (and have no choice) or just interested in astrophysics, I strongly recommend this class, and specifically taking it with Professor Johnson!
Astro 16 was a chill course. The class structure is a chalkboard setup with a small group, similar to the Math 21 series. You learn a lot and get to know your group members well.

Workload:
– 4 psets (after week 4, homework assignments were dropped because the professor claimed we demonstrated knowledge of the material in class).
– 2 lab reports: one day lab (meet three times before spring break), one night lab (9pm–2am once). Despite looking forward to it the least, the night lab was my favorite experience out of all of Astro 16. We used the telescope on top of the science center and looked at the stars in the middle of the night.
– 1 oral midterm: the professor brings in "random" people, and you and your group must teach them astronomy concepts. It was chill and low stakes.
– NO FINAL EXAM
