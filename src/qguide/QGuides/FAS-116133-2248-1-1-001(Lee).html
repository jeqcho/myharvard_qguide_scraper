Comments
Must–take course for any econ concentrator or anybody who wants to feel the practical parts of economics. An incredible course that applies your 1010A/B concepts that you thought you learned for no reason to real world events (mergers, auctions, network effects, etc). Robin is top 3 professors in the Econ department for teaching – don't think I've ever gotten clearer explanations. Topics are incredibly rich and interesting and the papers you read are understandable (no unnecessary readings in this class). If Ruby is still TFing next year, you're in luck.
This is a really interesting ECON course that covers topics that go beyond economics and are applicable to business concepts. Topics such as pricing (in terms of vs. a competitor, pricing a new product (i.e. Amazon Prime), and segmented pricing (popcorn sizes), the effects and payoff of advertising, how to think through competition in a multi–firm scenario in terms of price and production quality. Interesting and applicable ECON class!
start the psets early (they are really helpful at testing your understanding of the material)
The class was taught really well and the topics we covered were interesting. If you're interested in market structure, mergers, collusion, or game theory, this class is for you. I also appreciated how we discussed the papers – I learned how to read economic papers efficiently and identify the key takeaways of each paper. I wish I took this course earlier in my Harvard career!
This class is very math intensive. You don't get a formula sheet, and there's a lot of equations to memorize. However, it is learnable, and the subject matter is genuinely interesting. Professor Lee did an amazing job making a 3 hour lecture feel engaging throughout, and those three hours honestly always passed so quickly. Such a talented lecturer.
Great class with a good mix of theory and empirical papers that demonstrate the results from class; PSets are light and material is really interesting at times (but still using elementary tools); wished at times that the theoretical portion was more challenging, however
Definitely take this elective if you're at all interested in how markets work and firms make decisions. It's not super easy, but very doable, especially given most of the harder models and concepts are covered in class but explicitly not tested. Professor Lee is incredibly approachable and engaging; overall, a great person to spend 3 hours learning from every week. Probably the only class that I went to all the lectures for.
Robin is a great professor and this is a class that is very interesting! i personally really enjoyed the material. to be honest, this was the first econ class i had at harvard that I genuinely felt like my professor cares and learned so much!
I really enjoyed this course. The first half was a bit dense and a review of micro, but I found the second half to be more real–world applicable. This is honestly my favorite econ elective I have taken. The class is very manageable –– the PSETs are super doable and the midterm is very fair. The course is very well taught. Robin does a great job making the material comprehensible and engaging the class in interesting applications/examples of these theoretical models at play. Ruby's sections are also very helpful for clearing up confusion. I would highly recommend taking this course as the workload is light and the material is interesting!
This class is way harder than I was expecting. The problem sets are very confusing and require a lot of time in office hours. Robin is a good lecturer but the materials does not necessarily translate into understanding the problem set and exams. I would recommend another hard micro elective over this for AM
Ruby is an amazing TF. The course itself is very well taught, Prof. Lee is an amazing lecturer (otherwise I would not be able to sit through 3 hours of lecture lol), and the content itself is some review of Micro with added depth and applied to questions of monopoly power, mergers, firm pricing, etc. –– all relevant and interesting. Especially, the paper readings (once a week) are both interesting and fair, in the sense that they are fairly short.
This course takes a bit to get used to and spends a lot of time on theory. You'll find yourself doing math and basic models for almost the majority of the course and only towards the end does the material shift to applying it to real world problems. The lectures are engaging, but the math can get confusing and the professor introduces concepts that are covered in courses like Stat110 and Math21a that most Econ concentrators have not recently engaged with. While most of this material is not tested, I wish that it would have more application other than being shown and then glossed over in the course. I would definitely recommend taking this course P/F to not worry about the problem sets (which are pretty generously graded) and the exams, which comprise most of the grade. I felt like this class was more aimed at AM/Ec concentrators given the relatively high level math involved as compared to the rest of the Econ department.
