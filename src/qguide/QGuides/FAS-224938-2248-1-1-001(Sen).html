The lectures are just liberal echo chambers in which HKS students talk incessantly and confirm their own opinions. That said, the material is interesting, but the memos are graded pretty arbitrarily. Jack (the TF) was a lifesaver!
This is a really interesting course on a broad swath of landmark Supreme Court cases. If you're interested in learning about how politics, policymaking, and legal challenges work, this is a really engaging and fun class to learn from. Prof Maya Sen is great and guides the class well, but I would say it does feel a bit slow at times and perhaps a bit monolithic in relatively liberal viewpoints. Nonetheless, it felt like a really wonderful opportunity to learn about a key component of how law gets made in recent years.
This course is nothing short of fantastic. Professor Sen is an exceptional professor and Jack was a great TF. The content—everything from equal protection to administrative rule making—is incredibly interesting and super fun to learn about. The assignments are also perfectly designed to test your understanding of the material and be relevant to the real world. This class has solidified my interest in going to law school. Please take it, you will have no regrets!! I really hope a sequel to this class is offered in the future.
Professor Sen's lectures were so thorough and engaging and she did a great job of breaking down and summarizing dense SCOTUS cases and complex doctrines. It was a great insight not only in how to read legal cases and apply principles to real life current events like a lawyer to create a legal challenge to a policy, but also how to think as a policy maker when designing legislation to withstand constitutional challenges. The memo assignments centering on how to apply past constitutional and administrative law precedent to current events pulled directly from the news headlines in order to design policies or legal strategies was always fascinating and a great challenge that further cultivated my interest in going to law school. Grading was entirely fair and feedback was very helpful to keep improving with every memo. I could not recommend this class enough for anyone looking to go to law school.
If you have an interest in public policy and reading cases, I highly recommend this course! Lecture is always so engaging, and time always passes by so quickly due to the interesting conversations we have in class. As an undergraduate student, I was really uncertain about taking this course, but as long as you take the time to read the cases and visit office hours, this course is manageable. It might take some extra time, but in my opnion it is worth it.
I'd definitely recommend taking this course if you're interested in constitutional law, the Supreme Court, and/or going to law school. It did help to have a little background in constitutional law (specifically from Richard Fallon's undergraduate class), however, this was by no means required and any benefit that this gave me disappeared about halfway through the course when we started covering more administrative law, immunity, etc. The only thing that was a bit tough at times was the paper prompts, it sometimes felt like they were too complicated for what we had learned in class and that certain concepts could have been covered in more depth to avoid confusion. On net though, I'm really glad I took this course, the teaching staff is amazing and so supportive, and it was a great experience overall.
This course covers a lot of material in a really short amount of time. There is a lot to read and the lectures move pretty fast. You will take what you give from this course, but the more you apply yourself the more you learn. Professor Sen is really awesome and is clearly very passionate about the topic. There is a lot to learn from her and the course materials as a whole
Excellent course to take if you're really into the justice system and law. Gets into really nitty–gritty stuff, but is not brutal on assignments. Writing the memos was actually pretty enjoyable and engaging. Professor Sen is super friendly, knowledgable, and helpful. I would say take Con Law first if you're a college student due to the more technical focus of the class, but overall one of the better courses I've taken here.
