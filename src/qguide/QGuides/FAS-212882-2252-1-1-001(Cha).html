In a world of grueling and unnecessarily tedious intermediate biology classes, SCRB 50 is a breath of fresh air. At the same time, you will learn SO much about molecular biology. I feel infinitely more prepared to read and understand the cutting edge papers in developmental biology. after taking this class. I never felt like I was completing busy work or being asked to accomplish a random task that is rigorous for the sake of being rigorous. Still, SCRB 50 is no joke and requires a great deal of care and attention from the students' end. Definitely go to the optional Friday lectures.

Amie deserves a special shoutout. She is the foundation of this course and puts so much good energy and effort into making the course a meaningful and pleasant experience for the students. You can really tell that she enjoys her work as a teacher and advisor. Lecturers like Amie are a rare treat, even at Harvard.

The lab section is the most thoughtfully designed lab experience I've had in a classroom context – the whole semester builds up to one project where you get to knockdown a gene in planaria.
TAKE SCRB 50 OMG. You will not regret it. This is the first Harvard STEM class I have taken where I have felt safe and supported in my learning. The whole teaching team in AMAZING and care so much about you and your sucess, both academically and personally.
Great class! I took this as a pre–med without LS1B or SC10, and it was fine. Amazing course staff and an AMAZING lab – you will never think about planaria the same again (such a FUN time in this lab). Very informative and relevant course, it felt useful and the grading/ assignments are fair and helpful (nothing felt like busywork). Would highly recommend.

This is another pre–med course, but it feels much less like a weeder than most in a great way. The PSETs/assessments are somewhat similar to SCRB 10 in the sense that they are entirely experimental design/data interpretation, but 50 does a much better job of actually matching that in the lecture. There is a heavy focus on application in lecture, teaching us about how to actually use experimental techniques in assessments ahead of time. Also, the course teaching staff are very supportive and receptive to feedback/will give you feedback upon request. For assessments, GO TO OFFICE HOURS!!! Since most of the assessments are collaborative, you can do so much better by actually taking advantage of the fact that it's collaborative and talking to people in a place where everyone is trying to get the work done together. Also, don't blow off the in person assessments – they are a small part of your grade, but they are hard and will sneak up on you if you forget about them.
To be honest, I think this course needs a serious revamp. There were several things I found confusing/poor about this class:

1. It's marketed as an intermediate biology course but was tailored towards people who have never taken biology before. All of the concepts we learned were very surface level, and not the state of the art science that I would expect from the SCRB department. I also was really confused why we spent so much time focusing on how to use AI rather than actually learning the concepts advertised in the name of scrb 50.

2. We spent more time reflecting on what we learned than actually learning. All of the worksheets that we did had more reflection questions than actual content — I don't think these questions achieved any end and honestly they just frustrated me and took up time.

3. The course structure is wayyy too confusing to the point that it detracts from learning. I don't understand why there are pre–lecture videos, a lecture, an application, and then skill building. I think that scrb 50 should just adopt the format of scrb 10 which was simple and way more effective. Three lectures a week, no pre–lecture videos, practice questions interspersed in the lectures. Simple and easy. I learned so much from scrb 10, and I honestly am still so confused by what scrb 50 actually is that I can't tell you anything I've learned.

4. Honestly, it just felt like we were treated like kids sometimes in this class with the worksheets and assigned seats at the beginning. I really didn't get the rigor I would expect from a Harvard class at all.

5. The grading on the exams was actually heinous. Sometimes you would get points off for things that were most definitely implied or you would even get points off with no reasoning as to why.

I would recommend taking MCB 60 over SCRB 50 if you want to actually learn things. There was a lot of busywork in this class that detracted from the actual learning, and it was a huge drain on my semester.

This class had a very interesting format that I did not particularly enjoy. The mandatory lecture and application sessions on Monday and Wednesday had assigned seating. There are pre–lecture videos to watch before every lecture, but I found that the material covered was almost always review and/or was also covered in lecture. The partially collaborative nature of the exams means that there is not much stress, but I often thought they took of points unnecessarily, which was annoying. Because of this I would recommend really spending time on the collaborative exams and double checking answers with as many people as possible. The lab sections were interesting, but it was slightly annoying that most weeks we ended up being in lab for the entire 2 hours and 45 minutes even though the actual protocol took us around an hour. Overall it was not a hugely difficult class and was interesting at times, but often did feel like it took more time/effort than it should have.

The guest lectures are so interesting and are really relevant to modern medicine and/or molecular biology research. Such a lovely little community, and the lab is fun too!
SCRB 50 is overall not that bad. On the surface, the teaching staff are incredibly kind and responsive, there is variety in lecture, and assignments are less frequent than what your other secondary–level biology classes may require of you. This is all, however, sort of a double–edged sword. I still can't quite put my finger on the difference between application and skill–building, which are what you will be doing Wednesdays and Fridays. Assignments are less frequent but more variable; some weeks you may have a research report and collaborative exam together, and some you may have none. I think it just depends on what kind of person you are: I have some friends that have despised the course structure and others that seem to really have aligned with it. The end–all be–all is that SCRB 50 is an intermediate biology course at Harvard, so there are fascinating topics that you will get graded harshly on whether you like the format or not.
