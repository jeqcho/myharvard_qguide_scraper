Ted teaches this class well. The first half is more about concepts in organic chemistry, with the second half discussing much of the important literature in this subfield over the last 50 years or so. Problem sets are all long, and you should try to start as soon as they're posted (don't wait). This year, the final proposal was in groups and presentation only, which made it much more reasonable to do and also more engaging.
Great survey of relevant literature and will strengthen your grasp on principles in inorganic chemistry.
Lectures are pretty interesting if you’re curious about inorganic chemistry and the history of the field, but aren’t related at all to problem sets and exams.
This is a super fun class! Ted is a great lecturer and gives a wonderful overview of the field of small–molecule activation. I definitely became far more comfortable with the literature during the course of this class. I definitely recommend taking it!
Be prepared to learn lots about the beauty of inorganic chemistry and beyond.
Although this class only has one TF, Ted is very reachable outside of his office hours so do email him!
This was an interesting course that made inorganic feel more tangible and concrete to me. At times, the lectures assume background knowledge as there isn't a lot of context given before jumping into the literature (and most lectures are almost entirely summaries of various papers as they relate to a particular topic). Overall, enjoyable chemistry course and Ted cares a lot about the class and the students!
Prof. Betley is incredibly knowledgable, but his lecturing style is very specific — instead of giving a key point, and then illustrating with examples, he disperses the core principles through seemingly disconnected ideas, and expects you to grasp them. This is a totally reasonable expectation for a graduate course, but it is a heads up for anyone who prefers a more organized, explicit lecture. Additionally, PSETs and Midterms have little to do with the lecture material, and could be completed entirely on the basis of knowledge from Prof. Betley's CHEM 40, which means that there is little external accountability for your learning in the course — once again, not a flaw, but definitely something to know, depending on your preferences. However, one thing I appreciated was that Prof. Betley treated us all like adults and scientists, and encouraged student collaboration, from the problem set review sessions, to the proposal project at the end. If you've already taken CHEM 40 w/ Prof. Betley, I would recommend taking CHEM 154 w/ Prof. Nocera instead, for a different perspective on inorganic chem.
To be completely honest, I didn't feel very satisfied by the end of the course. The structure of the course includes one or two lectures dedicated to review of Chem 40 concepts, then turns into a giant literature review for the remainder of the semester. It was cool to see all the different science being discovered in inorganic chemistry, but I struggled with finding the "big picture" of the papers we were discussing. I did like the final group project and being allowed to pick our groups. It was fun meeting with my group, and Ted's critiques after every presentation were really insightful, especially for students who will be writing and presenting ideas for grants.
This is a challenging but very fun inorganic chemistry course. It kind of becomes more of a literature class half–way through the semester, but the content is still quite interesting and Ted is a very engaging lecturer.
This class is absolutely manageable for undergrads, especially if you've taken Chem 40. Ted is a great lecturer and is invested in our success. I'd recommend this course to any Chem concentrator, particularly if you want to go to grad school, as I learned a lot of skills that will be helpful there.
Take the class to develop inorganic chem intuition!
