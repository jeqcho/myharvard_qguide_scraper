It's the best neuroscience course I ever taken. To illustrate, if you read the reading before class and listen attentively, you wouldn't want to miss a thing that the professor said. It's just enlightening. You will walk out to be a better researcher and thinker.
This class is a decent amount of work and is taught at a high level, but it's SO worth it. Dr. Buckner is a great lecturer and the content is fascinating.
Read the papers every week and try to understand as much as possible. The papers are difficult to write but speak to the Professor as he will give good advice and suggestions.
This course was very different from any neuroscience class I have taken before, and until enrolling, I was unaware of just how beneficial that would be for me as a scientist and academic. If you want to have a more well–rounded perspective in the realm of neuroscience, I would recommend this course.
I loved this class. It is definitely a lot of dense readings, but I think over the semester it teaches you how to engage and efficiently read primary literature. Sometimes it seems daunting, but once you go to lecture Randy does a great job teaching. I found myself really engaged throughout the 2 hour lecture. The assignments can be nerve–racking and there are some in–class quizzes, but if you go to office hours and/or book times to meet with Randy or Emma, they are super helpful. I think you get what you put into this class.
All undergrads, please listen: be very careful with this class. Only take if you are very experienced with neuroscience. There are many postdocs, grad students, etc. in this class, despite it being offered in FAS as an undergrad course. If professor disagrees with topic, student is given poor grade.

The class lacks any structure for assignments:
– Doesn’t give page length for several papers, or any guidance on assignment; student can meet with professor to gain clarity, but this is not acceptable;
– No rubric for paper or how grading would be conducted, along with no structure given about assignments in syllabus;
– The final paper is due BEFORE reading period which collides with many undergrad students' midterms, which is literally against the rules by the dean.
I loved this class! Buckner is an awesome prof—and a truly amazing scientist. The seminars can definitely get a little long and intensive but are well taught. Buckner makes neuroanatomy thought–provoking, challenging, and engaging. A good class for a dip into more pure neuro for those who took MCB 80 but aren't neuro concentrators.
To any undergrads considering 1202: the material covered in this course is pretty different than anything you’ll encounter in other psychology or neuroscience classes here. It took me a lot of time and effort to get adjusted, but if you put in the work, it's a very rewarding class taught by one of the leading experts in the field.

The course revolves around readings, pop quizzes, and three papers on the historical use of a technique, an anatomical contrast, and exploring an open research question. These assignments are very challenging (especially for undergraduates) but I would highly, highly recommend this course if you're interested in the material!! Each class is basically a different story about the history of techniques in neuroanatomy or specific brain regions, which I found to be really interesting.
