This course is generally very easy, as there are no assignments due for most of the year apart from the midterm, creative project, and final exam. There is absolutely too much reading, but truthfully you don't need to read everything. Though Carrasco and Fash are knowledgeable and are good lecturers, the course often felt very disconnected as topics didn't build off of each other.

Some comments on assignments/exams:

The midterm exam was fair in its requirements, although definitely lengthy. Because the midterm exam was take–home, the length of the exam is understandable. The creative project was a fun challenge, and it was greatly intriguing to see what other students had done for their projects. The final exam was a different story. For the short answer questions, a requirement of two citations was implemented, which was different than the midterm exam. On the surface this doesn't sound too insane. However, requiring students to cite lecture or section for one of the two examples is absolutely ridiculous. No warning was issued that this would be on the exam, so many students either didn't take notes, or didn't jot down every single detail mentioned in class, instead opting to write down the more important points. However, the exam has incredibly specific terms and images that were likely only mentioned once in either lecture or section and in hardly any detail. It is obscene to require students to cite one sentence muttered in lecture for the final exam, especially when lecture slides are not provided. I understand that this was done to prevent students from cheating, but this was not the way to go about it. Terms should be more broad to offset the requirement.

If you have a genuine interest in anthropology, go ahead and take the course! There's plenty of interesting information to learn, but the organization of the course made it a bit difficult to enjoy.

This is a course with really interesting content, but several aspects of its organization make it hard to recommend. Lectures are engaging, but slides are incredibly dense and often skipped through quickly, which wouldn't be a problem except for the fact that neither the slides nor the lecture recordings (which *do* exist for the extension school) are shared. In theory this is to encourage people to take notes, but in reality it makes it difficult to actually engage with the content as you are scrambling to take down every word. Typing notes rather than handwriting proved critical in order to actually get all the content and find it later.

In terms of assignments, there are far too many readings for a GENED (often several hundred pages a week) with little emphasis on which ones are most important, making it very difficult to actually do them if you're not a fast reader and have a heavy course load. You can get by without doing them, but there's no point in taking a class if not to engage with the content. Other than readings there is basically no work assigned except for the take home exams (there's also a creative project, but it was fun and low stress). However, these exams are *long* (ten term definitions and two essays, ~20 pages in total). They're not too difficult, but they're extremely time consuming, and I would much prefer smaller, more frequent assignments instead of two enormous ones. For the final we were expected to cite lectures for each of the term definitions, which felt ridiculous given that we didn't have access to lecture content after the fact and had to hope we'd caught enough in our notes.

With regards to content, the professors clearly care a lot about the subject matter, which makes lectures engaging. However, one thing I wish I had known going in is that this is very much a history course through the lens of religion. This isn't necessarily a bad thing, but I don't personally find it as compelling to think about history with religion as a focal point given that I am not particularly religious. Section was incredibly helpful and when I actually felt like I understood the content (section slides *were* shared, which also helped a lot). Overall, I don't think my problems with this course are fundamental to the content or general format, but unless they change the organization I can't recommend it.
