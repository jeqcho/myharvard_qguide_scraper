Professor Freeds Math 230 was intense. The course itself was very very challenging – it moved at a rapid pace that was quite difficult to follow along, with proofs of theorems covered not necessarily presented in the most digestible manner (like many other geometers/topologists, Prof Freed drew excellent pictures but was not as clear at bringing out the logic behind the pictures he drew). The accompanying lecture notes were both really long and really dense, which together with the fact that DG is a subject with a lot of hard–to–grasp definitions and is heavily reliant on quick recall of all facts from undergraduate Diff Top (for which apparently Harvard does not regularly offer a sufficiently in–depth course certain years) made them really overwhelming to navigate. Also, in an effort to present the material in a way that emphasises the power of certain tools (for example, the Maurer Cartan form), it seemed as though some of the intuition that might otherwise be present was obscured, though perhaps this is because I am yet to grasp what was being explained to me completely. The problem sets though were mostly very interesting and I appreciated the number of opportunities there were to explore topology–/ analysis–adjacent ideas which really shed light on how important and wide reaching DG is as a field – it was also nice that there was a build your own problem set kind of aspect to the course in order that the class remained educational but not overwhelming. Also, Professor Freed is one of the kindest professors in the maths department and cares most fundamentally about our enjoyment of learning and doing mathematics which is great. Overall, a very daunting class if you are expecting to understand everything the first time through, though as Prof Freed says, growing comfortable with being confused is an essential part of being a researcher, and with this in mind this was overall a good course, and I am definitely excited to keep coming back to the lecture notes to grasp all the magic he has brought to us.
It's hard to tell what Dan Freed is talking about. This was a very high–brow take on Differential Geometry, and I think purposefully hard to parse at times. The lecture notes became increasingly unintelligible. The problem sets are super long, but you're told just to do some fraction of the problems. You determine the fraction. This seemed more like a second graduate course in DGthe version of 230a taught last year could reasonably have been a prerequisite. The problem sets were weekly, but this year the CAs were super accepting of late work. Thank god for them.
Dan Freed's math 230a was quite a challenge! Lectures are fast (but very organized), and the homework problems are very challenging. Dan Freed types very good course notes, and his supplemental readings are helpful. A strict requirement for this class is knowledge of Math 132 (manifolds, differential forms) and a strong background in linear algebra. Some helpful background would be Math 136 and differential equations.
Prof. Freed has amazing office hours. He's super welcoming and guides you through your own understanding of material in a way that helps you understand both the course and yourself better.

His lectures are a mixed bag. He talks with a lot of insight but he can be hard to follow, often because not all constructions are defined explicitly before he uses them. He's also pretty quiet so you should sit in the front. His lecture notes are really good though.

The homework system is interesting. Prof. Freed gives you way more problems than you can handle, so you're allowed (and expected) to not finish the pset. This means that you can pick and choose which and how many problems to do. This is nice because it lets you reach for harder problems / difficult topics at your own pace, but the lack of clarity in what the grading system expects can also make the course frustrating at times. I hope there's more transparency on this end in future iterations of the course.
This is an amazing class if you want your life to become differential geometry for a semester, otherwise I would think very carefully before taking it. Dan Freed is an amazing teacher, and he expects a high level of commitment and enthusiasm from his students. The problem sets in this class were very long and difficult, and lectures moved at a breakneck pace. If you are prepared for this, then you will learn so much in this class about diff geo, which is a very cool subject at the intersection of many different fields. However, if you are not prepared for this high commitment then you will not have a good experience.
Very tough but a good class. Dan Freed is wonderful
This is a great class! Prof. Freed is an amazing lecturer and he is very well–organized, which is why his lecture notes are a great supplement to the course. For people with the requisite knowledge of group theory (Math 122), differential topology (Math 122), and differential geometry (Math 136), I think this course will be a challenging but very rewarding addition to your schedule. To truly get a lot out of the class, I would anticipate devoting plenty of time to this course each week (12 hours + ) but the material itself is both extremely interesting and very relevant to areas of current research, so it should be both fun and useful! Office hours hosted by both Prof. Freed and by the course TFs and CAs are also a great help to be able to talk through the material.
By far the hardest class I have taken at Harvard! But so rewarding. The content goes really fast but Dan is a great lecturer who is really able to help you have a deep understanding of the content and answers questions incredibly well. Office hours, his and the TFs', are a very good resource to make use of.

Dan has an unusual system for HW where the psets were much longer than we were expected to complete, and we were just asked to do as many problems as we could each week, with no information on how grading would work. This could certainly raise or lower the stress depending on the type of student you are, so just be cautious of that. I assume grading will be very lenient but who knows!

It's a great class and I say: if you've taken 132, are excited about learning more about manifolds, and have time for it (it should definitely be your primary focus of the semester; you will need to go back over the lectures a lot outside of class) then go for it!
