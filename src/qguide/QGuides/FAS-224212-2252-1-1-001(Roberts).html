What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Take it! These are essential ideas for anyone interested in curation, conservation, object–based studies, or art history more broadly. Professor Roberts is brilliant, and, while the writing assignments can be a lot, you also get lots of personalized feedback.
This was more of a pilot but if Professor Roberts offers this course again, you should take it! She is an amazing scholar who remains curious about so many different topics, and she prepared an amazing assortment of readings and visits to Harvard's various conservation spaces (Art Museums, Glass Flowers, Arboretum, Observatory) where we got to meet conservators and discuss real problems that they had to think about on a daily basis. Your understanding of museums and conservation will be profoundly changed if you come with an open and curious mind. Under our format, it was a significant amount of writing but without a final paper (a five–page response paper every 2 weeks or so) but I really think it was worth the time to digest the readings/visits – she is also open to topics outside of American art so you can take response papers in other directions as well!
If you're interested in – or even just slightly curious about – repair, this IS the course to take. It's one of the most engaging and rewarding courses I’ve taken as HAA concentrator.
TAKE THIS CLASS!!!!!!! It is truly a once–in–a–lifetime opportunity! You will get to go behind the scenes and see things that many museum staff don't even get to see (a few examples from this semester: seeing a John Singer Sargent painting which had NEVER been displayed, seeing the other *half* of the glass flowers collection which is in storage, and getting to touch a David Drake pot). Conservation labs are endlessly fascinating, and we got to go on field trips almost every week! Profs Roberts and Ballard were wonderful complements to each other––Prof. Roberts provided the academic art historians' perspective while Prof. Ballard provided the working curator's perspective. And both of them were wonderfully creative and supportive of students' work!
