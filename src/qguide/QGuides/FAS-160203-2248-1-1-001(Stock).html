Great seminar! I did this in freshman fall while planning to do an environmental engineering concentration, so I took it as someone with more of a STEM–focused background. The discussions here were interesting and I learned a lot about climate tech and economic policies that I never knew about before (especially US policies since I'm an international students). Prof Stock is very experienced and knowledgeable, he was very helpful when I was working on my class presentation earlier in the term! This seminar also had an amazing range of experts coming in as guest speakers, I really enjoyed hearing from them!!
If you're at all interested in climate policy, take this class! Professor Stock is great and super experienced in the topic. The class is structured almost exclusively around 1–2 student presentations per week that Professor Stock chimes in on, which I didn't find to be the most appealing structure but wasn't bad overall. If you're envisioning just having discussions with the other students like a typical seminar, this is not it. It's also almost exclusively focused on the technical, policy side of things. Low time commitment and interesting readings.
I think this course is absolutely incredible for people who are truly passionate about the federal response to climate change, but I do think that it is designed for a certain type of student to succeed, and if you don't necessarily fit the mold, I would hesitate to dedicate one of your four freshman class slots to this. I think a good number of my classmates and I who had originally had some level of interest in economics and the environment found themselves falling behind as the number of regulations and concepts we covered piled up. The pace at which the course moves is extremely fast for the complexity of the topics we were covering, and I think that if you didn't take your free time to understand the content outside of class/have the passion going in, you wouldn't have the background knowledge needed to truly contribute during class discussions. The majority of this course is structured around two student presentations per class, with each covering an extremely timely but also complex area of federal environmental regulation.

I'm glad I took this course because I think I would've always wondered what I would've learned otherwise, but I do think it showed me that a career in environmental economics is not the right fit. I think before getting into this course, it's important to know that the whole point of the course is to get DEEP into the weeds about environmental regulation (e.g., how credits and offsets are verified, the 45 series of tax credits fro the IRA, etc.) –– if that's what you're looking for, then I wholeheartedly recommend this course!! If you're looking for just a broad–level understanding of U.S. climate policy though, I think this course might not exactly fit what you're looking for.
This was by far my favorite class, and if you're interested in climate and environmental stuff absolutely rank it first. Dr. Stock is very nice and incredibly knowledgeable about the climate field, as well as economics. There's a super great atmosphere (no pun intended) in the class, and I came out of the semester fascinated by the subject matter. There is a great blend of practical and academic in our discussions, and the presentations are enjoyable.
Do not take this unless you are interested in the environment: however, if you are, this a great class to learn from very knowledgeable people.
This is a great seminar if you're hyper–passionate about climate and want to learn from one of the university's greatest names and minds. You touch on really important and current issues/policies and certainly get a good footing in climate if you engage with the material and class sufficiently. Prof. Stock is a typical academic in that he's rather cerebral and so can seem intimidating at first. But he's always looking out for his students and genuinely wants you to get as much out of the course as possible.
The course brought up a lot of interesting discussions and I learned a lot about carbon pricing techniques. The people selected for the class as a whole were also very multifaceted. The guest speakers were very good. Only downside was that it was lowkey at a time when everyone was tired from the rest fo their classes(6–8pm). Also, there was a lot of student presentations and the teacher teaching could be complex and consequently hard to follow at times.
