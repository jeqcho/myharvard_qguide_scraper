This course is truly wonderful and incredibly interesting. I highly recommend taking it if you are interested in learning about the history of race relations in the United States through an empirical lens. Even if you aren't, the course will teach you the importance of questioning what you believe you know to be fact with extensive data. It will also teach the value of honest conversations about difficult topics. Professor Fryer is also an incredible lecturer and very funny, a truly wonderful class.

That said, be aware that the course is very disorganized. The syllabus is out of date and there is little information provided on what is expected from the readings, on the quizzes, or on the exams. Go in prepared to do little week to week work but have to learn a lot of untaught material before the exams. Also reach out and ask clarifying questions! They will get answered but it is really the only way to know if there is reading or what is on the exams.
The course is pretty easy, but requires a fair amount of self–studying for the exams. The two exams are worth 40% each, but nothing else was graded in the course, so essentially 50% each. The lecture slides are hundreds of pages and Fryer only covers about half of it in lecture, so the other half is on you to learn. I felt that the exams tested you on a lot of irrelevant things, such as how experiments were done as opposed to the main findings of the studies. Fryer didn't seem too interested in teaching the course if I'm being honest. It was clear there was minimal communication between Fryer and the TF, and coupled with the fact that the TF did not show up to a single lecture, it made it hard for students to ask questions about preparing for the exam.
A class with good content and mediocre execution. A lot of Prof. Fryer's insights about issues facing the Black community leave you with a lot to think about, and it's cool to see how economic models can apply in so many circumstances. My biggest gripe with the course is the grading and assignment structure. First, there are too many readings. 200+ pages per week is excessive. Second, the assignments (sporadic quizzes and two multiple–choice exams) do not measure how engaged you are, just how well you can memorize and regurgitate the lecture slides. The course disincentivizes you from doing the readings, then punishes you for it. Overall a decent course, but there are more engaging Econ electives out there.
This class was so awesome! Roland Fryer is a great lecturer and makes it very easy to pay attention, even though it is a three–hour seminar. I also loved that we were able to have actual conversations and debates during the lecture. It was interesting to hear everyone's opinions and Roland does a great job at pushing back and really making you think. The readings were definitely a lot, but manageable. They were also important, and I'm glad I have them in my back pocket. The midterm and final exam are the only assignments, and are completely based on the lecture slides and readings. They are very manageable. Overall, this was one of my favorite classes I've taken. I'd take it again if I could. Take this class!!!
TAKE THIS CLASS. Professor Fryer is the most brilliant professor I've ever had. He is unbelievably smart –– but, perhaps more importantly, he is a fantastic, charismatic lecturer. The three hour class totally flew by. The midterm and the final were not hard if you studied –– it's just about memorizing a lot of content, but the tests were all multiple choice.

Be aware that the class is very poorly organized. Readings were unclear, and would vary from 0 (multiple times) to 500 (twice) pages a week. Some weeks you didn't have to do the reading, some weeks it was more important. To me, the class was definitely well worth the disorganization. But if that's not for you, that's fine. But cannot emphasize enough how much I loved this class.

It is so so so so worth taking this course. Please do it.
TAKE IT! It’s so good and interesting.
Every Harvard undergad should be required to take a class with Prof. Fryer and having the opportunity to is a great privilege.

Maybe lecture slides should not be 150 slides long. Maybe the reading assignments are outlandish,

But just engaging with Prof. Fryer for 2 hours and 45 minutes will change your life. Few people have the opportunity to talk with the world's most prominent researchers in academia.

Prof. Fryer also brings in great guest speakers.

Midterm and Final are not difficult if you are good with multiple choice.
While the course content is strong, the teaching staff is extremely unorganized. There are no study materials, no feedback given at all, and review sessions are nonexistent. Attendance makes up about 20% of your grade, but the professor only takes attendance during like three random lectures the entire semester—so if you happen to be sick, you're out of luck. He also doesn’t respond to emails (LIKE AT ALL. EVER. UNTIL YOU BUMP 10 TIMES) about medically excused absences. Office hours are frequently canceled without notice, and your final grade feels like a toss–up
Great class. Interesting readings, great lectures, reasonable exams
TAKE THIS CLASS. You learn so much about the economics of social problems and get very unique takes from Professor Fryer on very important issues. We touched on the economics of slavery, education, Jim Crow, the KKK, policing, crime, and political correctness. The readings are pretty light, and the reading quizzes are chill. Do not underestimate the final... I thought it was much harder than the midterm (which was pretty easy). But otherwise, VERY interesting class.
