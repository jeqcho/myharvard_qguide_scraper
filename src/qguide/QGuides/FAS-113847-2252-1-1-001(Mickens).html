This is a thorough and well–taught operating systems class, with nice programming components. I felt that I was able to come away with the big ideas in operating system design, from foundational topics to more research–oriented ones. There's nothing particularly negative I can say about the structure and content of the class. On the programming front, the homework difficulty is exaggerated. There is so much guidance given in the pset descriptions that it mostly boils down to following the instructions as given, with some debugging to boot.

As a counterpoint to previous Q guides, though, I do feel that this class is overhyped. Yes, it is a well–designed class with good content coverage and instructive homeworks, but it is certainly not at the level of "best academic experience at Harvard" some Q guides seem to claim. The midterms, for example, feel somewhat inadequate as a yardstick for understanding because it generally boils down to fact recall. One might argue that this is just the nature of intro–level operating systems, but then what's the point of giving these midterms if we are allowed to bring in unlimited notes (other than to test our ability to memorize which slides contain which facts)? An arguably better way to test grasp over the material is to make the homeworks harder and more extensive. Another thing to note is that, in typical Mickens fashion, grading is "absolutely [not transparent] under any circumstance," and exams generally use all–or–nothing grading. You may or may not like this, but it's safe to say that this adds more variance to midterm results and thus more stress if you end up on the wrong side of the variance. None of this is to say that the course is not worth taking; in fact, it is probably necessary for anyone who is remotely interested in being a well–rounded computer scientist. But we should also temper our expectations.

This course traditionally gets insanely high Q scores. I agree that it's a good course, but I think that the scores would probably tend to inflate your expectations in a way that's not warranted. At the end of the day, this is just another CS elective, albeit one that is better run and more work than most others. Lectures are well done and engaging, though I do think the fact that they're not recorded somewhat impedes learning as they're quite information–dense. Section tends to be pretty boring, though I don't think that's the TF's fault. Problem sets generally take a fair bit of time, though the deadlines are spaced out pretty well so there's rarely that much of a time crunch if you start early enough.

I'd say each problem set takes about ~15–20 hours total spread across 3–4 weeks, so it's very manageable. I was able to pair this with some other difficult courses but it was undoubtedly a busy semester. The exams aren't too bad and are fully open–note (though no electronics allowed). Keep in mind that this course tends to be filled with (extremely) hard–core CS kids who end up doing all of the extra credit, camping out in office hours 24/7, etc., so be ready for that coming in. I personally did my psets alone but from what I understand this isn't the norm, so if systems and CS are really your thing then I think the fact that a lot of the students become close with each other might be a strong plus.

The TFs are in general excellent — they're helpful, knowledgeable, and in general seem like great people. I can say that I really did get quite a bit out of this course in terms of learning how an operating system works from the ground–up, and it's definitely made me into a much better C/C++ programmer. I'd say take this course if you're really interested in systems, but don't come in thinking it'll be some sort of religious experience like some of the past years' comments might suggest.
Be ready for this course to take more time than expected and humble you. At the same time, this class is such a great community, so be sure to make the most of that!

This is a great class, and I encourage anyone interested in software dev to take it. You'll learn a lot in lectures and ultimately become a much stronger programmer. However, 161's reputation is somewhat overrated, and I was particularly disappointed with the psets. For a class focused on low–level systems, the psets are surprisingly simple; they take time due to messy concurrency bugs, but most design decisions are handed to you and the hardware–facing features (boot sequence, AHCI, etc.) are already implemented. I have no regrets about taking the course, but I wish I came out of it with more understanding of how to write an OS instead of just fixing deadlocks.
You know, there are software engineers out there—wandering the world, blissfully unaware—thinking they make the world go 'round. And then there are people like Linus Torvalds, who actually make the world go 'round. In the wise (and often blunt) words of Torvalds: “Talk is cheap. Show me the code.”

If you’re ready to ascend to this new plane of arrogance, you have to take this class.

You thought you were a programmer? Let's see you write a kernel that can synchronize an infinite number of threads spawning in and out of existence without blocking. Let’s see you design a file system that can recover from a crash because your dog ate your charger. Let’s see if you really have what it takes.

If you're a self–respecting programmer, you owe it to yourself to take this class.
But be warned: earning the right to call yourself a programmer doesn’t come easy. You’ll sacrifice sleep, possibly half your hair, and you may even find yourself chucking your computer out the window. Unless, of course, you’re a genius, which wouldn’t surprise me, given that you go to Harvard.

One final disclaimer: after this course, computers will no longer feel magical. You’re about to take the red pill—and there’s no going back. They’ll still be magical in a new way, but the black box will be gone.

This course is hard. I'm not talking about "oh no I've gotta study for my exam" or "you mean I can't just do the pset in one night?" No, I'm talking about the fact that this is a course that you will pour blood, sweat, and tears into; you will feel like you are personally wandering through the previously unknown eighth ring of hell. And believe it or not, you will love it. It might take until after the semester is over, but the amount that you will get from this course simply by passing it is remarkable. Being able to look at a semester's amount of work, a semester where I was working on my operating system more nights than not, and seeing the thousands of lines that I implemented is truly amazing. If you have any interest in operating systems or low–level programming, I don't think there is a single other course that I would recommend more than this one.

As far as the psets go, they are difficult, long, but rewarding when you put the work in. Additionally, this course is near impossible without the lovely TFs and their office hours throughout the week. Before this class, I (as a second–semester sophomore) could count the number of times I went to office hours on one hand. In this class, I attended at least half of the office hours and they were so immensely helpful. Whether you use it as a time to simply sit down and do work or you use it as a time to talk with other students and TFs about ideas, bugs, or questions.

I hope that everybody at this college gets to experience such a useful and rewarding class like this one.
