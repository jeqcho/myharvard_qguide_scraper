Great class
If you want to learn how to properly read and understand research papers in neuroscience, this is an excellent course to do that (you have one section every week where you basically go over one or two papers you had read in preparation). The class itself has a slightly confusing structure but when supplemented by the papers, you learn a lot of key principles of systems neuroscience. Take it!
The class feels a bit disorganized at times, but Florian is an incredible, brilliant neuroscientist and thinker. We did a lot of cool readings involving bees, ants, ferrets, and other animals. There isn't much guidance for the final exam (no practice tests or anything), but it turned out to be pretty reasonable. The DA assignments also aren't too bad—you can procrastinate until Friday evening as I often did, though I’d recommend starting earlier in the week.
I would only take it if you have a good foundation in neurobiology and some coding experience. The lectures and the readings are very interesting, but the digital assignments can be tedious, especially if you've never coded before. The final exam was also really hard (all the questions were open response) and little to no study guidance was given.
Professor Engert is awesome. Schedule time to talk with him after class, his insights are mind–opening.
The reading assessments and final exam can be difficult so prepare for them.
a good systems–level neuro class for concentrators who don't really like overly molecular/cellular neuroscience. first half of the course is a little dry, but learning about the different animal models later on was pretty interesting. florian is a great lecturer!

assignments are mainly the matlab assignments and quiz readings. matlab is very doable for beginners with step–by–step instructions and lots of resources available for help (office hours, matlab guides). quiz readings requires significant prep with really understanding the papers. although this was definitely annoying in terms of how much time i had to spend on reading the papers, i will admit this definitely improved my skills at understanding dense scientific literature efficiently and effectively.

if you like learning about systems neuro and reading scientific literature/want to get better at reading the literature, this course could be a good fit!
You must do the readings for the weekly quizzes. There are no prep materials for the final so beware of that.
Take this course if you're looking for a practical toolkit on how to design and execute your own research ideas. They approach it from the experimental methodology level, as well as how to analyze the resulting data computationally. Don't worry about the CS component; I am not a coder by any means at all, and it was completely manageable and interesting. It was a truly effective and engaging crash course on experimental design and research! I can confidently say I can now conceptualize how to actually test a concept experimentally and how I would analyze the data. What a wonderful course! As a senior, I can say I really wish I took this earlier because I think I've learned more about how to execute a project than in any of my other classes I've taken in this concentration. Not to mention, Florian is a fabulous, brilliant, and cheeky lecturer.
The subject matter is interesting. However, it is a lot of reading journal articles and learning about studies. Also, all of the homework assignments are coding. It might be helpful to have some coding experience prior.
This is a great class in terms of content and accessibility without extensive MCB/Neuro background. Neuro 80 suffices. However, some cautions: you are given literally zero information about the final exam (three very short sample questions without answers); there was no information about the length/format of the exam so it is very hard to study for. Really, you can only review section and lecture content and hope for the best. The upside is that grading for this course is fairly lenient as long as you do pretty well in the final project. Make sure to receive feedback from the TFs on the project as much as you can so that you can optimize the grade. They really do empathize that the final project grade matters a lot for the final grade so I think what you get on the project is a great indicator of what final grade you will get. The weekly reading quizzes are little annoying but again, I think the grading is fair. Overall, the content is interesting and I think this class is a great way to fulfill upper level MCB requirement without too much stress but be prepared to bit a little confused about your grade and course format for the entire semester.
This class is not a gem. Personally, this class was very disorganized and had no type of structure. Every week, there was a 10–minute closed–book quiz on the reading that was assigned, and that felt like a waste of time. Section is just us talking about the readings that were assigned and nothing else. We did not talk about what was happening in lecture. Every week, we had to code, but the coding did not really correlate with the class. There is a final paper and exam for this class, and there was no type of prep for the exam. They announced it, and that's it. For the final paper, the instructions were very unclear, and you don't really know what you are doing. When you ask for help on the final paper, they are just repeating those unclear instructions. I was pulled in because of the Q–reports, but the class is not a gem. I was taking this class to fulfill a requirement, and I do recommend this class with lots of reservations.

If you have any interest/requirement in Neuroscience then take this class!
