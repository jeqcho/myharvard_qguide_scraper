I’m writing from the undergrad perspective, ESPP concentrator who took EC10A and went to all lectures, sections, and review sessions for this course:

For students: I found this class very meaningful, helpful, and thought provoking. Everyone wants to come out of a class feeling the course expanded their thinking, exposed them to new ideas, and that they’ve built skills and knowledge within the course useful elsewhere—I feel this way about the course. I find the afternoon lecture time hard and Professor Stavins lectures in a relatively tranquil / monotone way for most of the time so try and energize yourself before coming to the lecture as it can sometimes be hard to maintain focus — many students did not come to lecture but you should go!! Stavins has many interesting and relevan stories to share form his experiences with the content we discuss and is humorous — lecture really grew on me throughout the semester as the class got to know one another and we got a better background under us for learning more content. Microeconomics background helpful, but do not be concerned if not! You will be expected to do some quantitative problem–solving on exams, so know that it’s not entirely policy / qualitative analysis. If you haven’t looked at the syllabus, be aware this course grades almost solely on the exams so basically all your learning through the semester is graded through two tests. I will say that the exams felt more quantitate / problem solving like in comparison to lecture — I don’t think lecture prepares you for a lot of the thinking required on the exam and you build your exam abilities more through the problem sets and practice exams — lecture is more high level and qualitative. Lectures felt more like a gov class, while exams felt more like a Ec10 exam. Go to office hours with Stavins! He may seem intimidating but he’s happy to talk to you and you should take advantage of the opportunity to learn about his experiences.
Good class overall. Professor Stavins is so intelligent and is very clearly insanely knowledgeable in his field. Would highly recommend going to office hours. Workload is not too bad generally, but keep in mind the grade is 85% exam–based so you will definitely have to grind for those tests. Take if you are genuinely really interested in climate economics!!

Only take this class if you have a background in economics. I took this class for my ESPP requirement, and understand others must do the same, but it was very challenging as a result. I strongly encourage preparation or a background in the content prior to understand the material.
I wouldn't have taken this class if it weren't an ESPP requirement, but I am glad it is. I think 1661 is a good course. I learned a lot about the intersection between the environment and economics, as well as relevant policy applications. The course is structured well, with a clear outline of what we will cover in the course as well as how the modules connect with one another.

The grading scheme is a little intense, with 15% for 5 problem sets (which were very manageable and can be done with lecture slides/notes in not too much time), 35% on the midterm exam, and 50% on the final exam. So, this scheme is not very forgiving, and the exams hold most of the weight. That being said, the exams aren’t too bad, but they do require a lot of memorization, which can be frustrating. I do feel like I learned a lot through studying for these exams, though.

It was annoying that the lectures weren’t recorded, but I suppose I wouldn’t be very inclined to go if they were, since the lecture can be a bit slow and dry. The optional section component didn’t really add anything for me, and I didn’t find it helpful when I went. However, the course staff are supportive and are happy to answer any questions.

I took this class as an ESPP requirement, and for that, it was really not bad at all (though I did come in with an Econ background, which I think was very helpful). It will also count as an Econ elective for me.
