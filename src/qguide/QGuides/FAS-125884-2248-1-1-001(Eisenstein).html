Take this class if you are extremely interested in extragalactic astronomy. Dr. Eisenstein is very passionate about his work and has brilliant experiences and contributions to the field. It was a privilege to take a class with such a renowned professor. The class itself could be taught better. The classes were sometimes difficult to follow as there was no presentations. The solutions to assignments were never posted. One assignment and one quiz were not graded. No practice tests were released for the midterm or the final. While I enjoyed the material, the way of teaching the class could be enhanced.
Psets are very difficult but TFs are really helpful. Stay on top of the content.
This class does what it needs to do, but is rather boring if you have taken any college physics or astrophysics before. It's not a hard course I just found it difficult to find the motivation to actually do the work, but I usually didn't take more than 3 hours on the problem sets and weekly quizzes. I would imagine that if I'd taken this course in my freshman fall I would've enjoyed it much more as it's an intro course, it is definitely geared towards freshman and people doing an astro secondary. The professor seems like a good person though I would consider taking a different course with him.
If you are considering taking this class, know that a majority of the learning will have to happen outside of the classroom. With a dense textbook, module readings, and then a lecture that just repeats what the dense material had to say, it can be easy to lose interest in the material. Professor Eisenstein is a lovely individual and you can tell that he loves what he does, but most times the lecture can feel flat and unengaging when the material doesn't make sense right away.

Having comfort in physics (mechanics specifically) is essential for this class, and if you feel like that isn't you, going to the TFs for support or finding classmates who are comfortable with the subject is necessary to succeed in this class because the lecture rarely connects to the psets. Most time spent in lecture is going through questions that people submitted about the reading and minimal demonstration of how the formulas might be used in the context of a word problem. You will find yourself bored in class because of this, and the lack of concrete connection between lecture and the psets/assessments can be a pain.

If you are interested in Astrophysics out of pure curiosity, I don't recommend this class, I bet there are better ways to go about it since this class is just reading and asking questions. If you are interested in Astro as a concentration or a secondary, you'll have to take this class, but go in knowing if you are truly new to Astro, i.e haven't taken it before in your life or have minimal physics experience, the people around you are the best way to make the most out of the class.
Try to get started and ask questions about problem sets early. Make sure you understand different ways questions can be asked about concepts for the exam.
The class is taught assuming prior knowledge of astronomy, both in lectures and in the readings/textbook. The psets are generally fine and the quizzes are doable as well. The exams , however, are much more challenging, especially the final. Almost no study material is provided (no practice exams, only a few general concepts that we should look over). Of these broad questions, few if any are actually relevant to what's on the exam. You need to do the readings (both the canvas modules and the textbook); otherwise, you will fall behind very quickly and you will be even more confused. The modules are more helpful for doing the psets/quizzes but the textbook goes into greater detail on the concepts talked about in the modules and in class. I honestly learned more from the textbook than I did from going to class. Professor Eisenstein is clearly very knowledgeable about the material but he does not do a good job in teaching it. The TFs were great and helped clarify questions I had. The class stars off relatively easy/light and then ramps up. The material is somewhat interesting at times rather dull at others. I would say to only take this class if you are an astrophysics concentrator or if you are really interested in the subject material and are prepared to dedicate time to stay on top of things and really understand how all the concepts tie into one another.
I enjoyed this class, but unless you're very interested and very willing to find a group to do the psets with you will struggle. This is a tough class, so if you're not ok with that this class probably isn't for you.
Take this course if you don't know if you want to concentrate or not, it will tell you. Don't expect it to be the best course of your life, but informative nonetheless.
This is an interesting class and professor Eisenstein is super smart and interesting. He can seem a touch condescending at times, but I would try to just ignore it because he is super interesting and has a ton to teach you! Make sure to ask lots of questions, and budget time for the assignments because they will make or break your grade (and your weekend). It isn't the walk in the park I had imagined, but it's totally doable (think math 21a or physics 15a level of difficulty; less bad that 21b or 15b).
DO THE READING! The midterm and final have fact recall that is directly tied to smaller details in the readings and modules. The TFs are great and Daniel is extremely knowledgable. You can ask him anything and he will know the answer. Take advantage of this, the class is very interesting and covers a lot of content that can get tricky, especially towards the end of the semester so it's important to ask questions.
Where do I begin? First of all, the course prerequisites say that no background in astronomy is required, but the book we're learning from says in fine print that a background in astronomy is expected. Second, the course is incredibly unorganized in every single way, especially in terms of following clear learning goals in class and on problem sets. As I progressed through the course, I found myself being confused on what the main takeaways were supposed to be. I told myself, "Oh maybe lecture would clear it up" –– these were fragments, of what I found to be, pure imagination I'd hang on to week after week. I was left more confused than where I initially started every week. "Maybe the book will help", I also said. Nope, completely unrelated material at most times. The professor goes into a LOT of depth about every little detail which is incredeibly unnecessary. Some may find that helpful, but when you don't reference any slides (the Professor just speaks to the class, using voice recordings instead of slides for a reason I do not understand) or any visual material it makes everything that was said very hard to string together. Like I said, it is hard to understand what you're supposed to retain or what the main idea is because of every little detail he mentions. The best analogy I can make is if someone asks for a cup of water, and you dump the entire Pacific Ocean on them and they actually drown. "Maybe the class activities will help?", oh you mean the class activities we barely go to do or see solutions for until the very end of the course? No that didn't help either. Please, put some slides together with the main takeaways and bullet points instead of pages of modules where the level of detail is too much for an introductory class. The Midterm was fair, but the Final was brutal. There are 12 psets in this course with 3–4 long questions each. Sometimes, I can see what the professor is trying to do by using math to explain certain concepts, but he doesn't do it well at all. There is no clarity on what you're supposed to take away from a problem, so it really just turns into you doing math and not understanding why you're doing it. Each quantitative concept is usually covered with a single problem on a pset. So you're expected to understand a quantitative topic using 1/48 of the homework material you're given (which you're not even given solutions to and you don't see any practice problems, like Physics 15a, in class to better understand). "Oh maybe he'd supply practice problems for the final that are relevant to the actual exam to make up for the lack of helpful class activities and well written practice problems?" ...don't be fooled, this was yet another fragment of imagination. Please provide better, more helpful material instead of giving your students a review guide that says (no joke) 'This is NOT indicative of the structure of the final. It may not be the best use of your time to go through and write down an answer to each question.' The professor seems like a nice person, but I'm rating the course the way it was taught rather than the professor's character. On another note, I really want to emphasize that Jea and Kaylee were amazing teaching staff, they explained things very clearly and helped me a lot with multiple office hours, even on weekends (where the professor only had a single 30 minute office hour once a week at a very inconvenient time which I won't dive into).
I enjoyed this class! It's a requirement for any concentrator or secondary in Astronomy, so you probably don't have much choice any ways. The material is clear and relatively straightforward. The TFs are helpful, but I felt like section was not a hugely productive use of my time. Professor Eisenstein is really kind and definitely knows his stuff (he's pretty much a genius and is the originator for some of the stuff you will learn in cosmology). Be sure to do the textbook reading! All of the midterm and half of the final exam is qualitative, drawing on pretty detailed recollections from the modules and textbook. The exams are pretty tricky, and I had a decently hard time with the final. It's hard to study for everything, but the teaching team's approach was to recommend looking over *everything.* This is, of course, hard to balance when you have a lot of qualitative and quantitative materials at the same time. Nevertheless, I thought it was a fair and decent introduction to lots of fascinating phenomena in astrophysics.
