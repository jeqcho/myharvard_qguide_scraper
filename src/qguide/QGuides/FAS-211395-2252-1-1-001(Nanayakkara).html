This was an interesting course.

Prof. Vadhan is clearly very knowledgeable, and has done much interesting work in the area. The course itself is not particularly technical, but rather focuses on applications and discussions. Many of the readings are journal/magazine articles regarding opinions of privacy, and privacy from various viewpoints. There is an introduction to the basic technical details, e.g. definitions of DP, elementary mechanisms, and properties such as post–processing, etc.

Overall, the course does well to provide a broad introduction of DP, both from a theory and application standpoint. Nonetheless, I feel that there could have been more depth and pace to the class. For instance, the introduction to DP–ML discussed some elementary algorithms such as DP–SGD and their implementation, but did not go further beyond (whereas in the literature there are both many optimization algorithms, as well as various proof techniques). Class sometimes consisted of discussions, which were nice, and going over code, which probably could have been omitted (probably reading a 100–line jupyter notebook could be assigned as homework). There are weekly problem sets, which take a relatively short (but still not completely trivial) amount of time. Lastly, there is a final project, which I quite enjoyed. Prof. Vadhan's feedback is quite good, and the field of DP contains a significant amount of interesting theory and application. The poster presentations at the end were interesting and allowed us to see the breadth of work done by other groups.

I would probably encourage taking cs226 for a more theoretical side of this course, but if you are interesting to knowing the basics of privacy and learning an overview of where privacy mechanisms are applied, this would be a fitting course.

A good introduction to the theory and implementation behind differential privacy, including the societal and legal ramifications of its implementations.

Sometimes this class felt disorganized. The worst offender was likely how late submission forms for PSETs were uploaded. The submission forms would often get released on the due dates of the PSET, which happened to make me miss one assignment, because I checked whether one had been uploaded during spring break and saw no form and assumed no homework was due (still my fault for not checking Ed, of course, but could have been prevented if the HW submission forms were up earlier).

The form to upload final project ideas was listed on the PSET, with a due date for the final project ideas being listed before the PSET was actually due. Since I start PSETs quite late, I had missed the deadline to submit a project ideas. Ultimately, I didn’t get to choose my project partners, and even worse, one of them ended up ghosting me and my other partner for half the project. Our project grades suffered pretty hard as a result. The advice I could give to you, reader who may take this class, is to choose your project partners EARLY. Do not wait until last minute. And to the professors, please adjust it so that the due date of the project ideas align with the due date of the PSET.

Some of the participation requirements for the class are a bit vague. Your participation is mostly based on your attending class and answering discussion polls, as well as commenting on readings. But how these participation points are weighted is still unclear, and the number of comments “needed” to be a full participant is also unclear. More elaboration on how participation is accounted for would be great.

The time spent on PSETs could vary quite a lot from PSET to PSET. One PSET (PSET 3 I believe) was overkill, simply because of the amount of times you had to calculate sensitivities under 3 different kinds of distance metrics for several differentially private mechanisms. It got old fast. But some PSETs are more fun, like designing a study on having someone use a differential privacy GUI program. Some are quite short, which is nice.

Despite all the flak I give this class, I still would entirely recommend it. Most of the problems I mentioned are issues that are easily fixed, and I would hope that they get remedied by the next iteration. Professor Salil truly brought an interest into differential privacy that has made me consider changing career paths towards data science and data privacy. As an undergrad, taking this as my first real grad class opened me up to thinking about research, as well, which I had never considered before then. Differential privacy is by no means an easy topic, and some aspects of it will feel unintuitive to you (and perhaps I am still left with some unanswered questions or misconceptions), but I still feel as though I learned a lot.
